# The gut microbiome and the brain — Day 5/7: Diet, probiotics, and fecal microbiota transplantation — what works, what doesn't

## Context

Over the past four days we have assembled a detailed picture of the gut–brain axis. Day 1 surveyed the ecosystem and the core communication pathways. Day 2 drilled into mechanisms — the vagus nerve, short-chain fatty acids, tryptophan, and the leaky gut. Day 3 examined the evidence linking the microbiome to mood, anxiety, and depression. Yesterday (Day 4) explored the most dramatic application of this framework: the possibility that Parkinson's and Alzheimer's diseases have a gut component, and what one large FMT trial in Parkinson's patients actually showed (spoiler: no motor benefit at 12 months in established disease).

Today we ask the most practically important question of the week: can we deliberately modify the gut–brain dialogue, and with what outcome? Three categories of intervention have attracted the most research — (1) dietary change (what you eat shapes which microbes flourish); (2) probiotics, prebiotics, and synbiotics (deliberate introduction or nourishment of beneficial bacteria); and (3) fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT, wholesale transfer of a healthy donor microbiome). Each has a different evidence base, a different mechanism, and a different ceiling of current proof.

This is also a day for epistemic hygiene. The gut–brain intervention space is unusually prone to premature enthusiasm: early positive findings from small trials get amplified by wellness culture, companies sell billions of dollars of probiotics whose specific mental-health claims have little rigorous support, and "eat fermented foods for your brain" circulates as established fact while the direct causal chain remains poorly characterised. Today we trace what randomised trials actually show — which is more nuanced, and also more interesting, than the headlines suggest.

## Today's readings (~23 min total)

**1. A randomised controlled trial of dietary improvement for adults with major depression (the 'SMILES' trial)**
Jacka FN et al. *BMC Medicine*, 2017 — [https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-017-0791-y](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-017-0791-y) — estimated reading time: ~10 min — Open access (BioMed Central / Springer Nature).

The SMILES trial was the first randomised controlled trial ever to test dietary improvement as a treatment strategy for major depression. It randomised 67 adults with moderate-to-severe depression to either seven sessions with a clinical dietitian guiding a Mediterranean-style dietary pattern or a matched social-support control. *BMC Medicine* is a high-impact general medical journal in the Springer Nature open-access portfolio, using full peer review; the trial was prospectively registered and its findings have since been independently replicated.

**2. Gut-microbiota-targeted diets modulate human immune status**
Wastyk HC, Fragiadakis GK, Perelman D et al. *Cell*, 2021 — [https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(21)00754-6](https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(21)00754-6) — estimated reading time: ~8 min.

This Stanford-led randomised trial (36 healthy adults, 10 weeks) is the most rigorous head-to-head comparison of two popular microbiome-targeted dietary strategies: a high-fermented-food diet versus a high-dietary-fibre diet. Published in *Cell* — one of the highest-impact journals in biology — with deep multi-omics profiling of the microbiome and immune system (over 6,000 immune features measured), it provides unusually detailed mechanistic evidence for what different diets do to the gut ecosystem and inflammatory tone. Crucial reading for understanding what "healthy for your microbiome" may actually mean.

**3. Effects of prebiotics and probiotics on symptoms of depression and anxiety in clinically diagnosed samples: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials**
*Nutrition Reviews*, Oxford University Press, 2025 — [https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/7/e1504/7934047](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/7/e1504/7934047) — estimated reading time: ~8 min — Open access (Creative Commons).

This meta-analysis (23 RCTs, 1,401 patients all with clinically diagnosed depression or anxiety disorders) is the most up-to-date and methodologically stringent quantitative synthesis of the probiotic evidence in clinical populations. A critical design choice — restricting to diagnosed patients rather than pooling with healthy volunteers — makes its effect-size estimates more directly relevant to treatment contexts. *Nutrition Reviews* is published by Oxford University Press and indexed in MEDLINE/PubMed, specialising in evidence-based nutritional science.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### Dietary change: the SMILES trial and what it really showed

For decades, psychiatry treated nutrition as a lifestyle factor with no direct treatment relevance for mental illness. The 2017 SMILES trial changed that framing. Felice Jacka and colleagues at Deakin University's Food and Mood Centre randomised 67 adults with moderate-to-severe major depressive disorder — all already receiving standard care (medication, psychotherapy, or both) — to a 12-week program of dietitian-supported dietary improvement or a matched social-support control. The dietary target was a modified Mediterranean pattern: whole grains, legumes, oily fish, lean meats, olive oil, nuts, fruit and vegetables; reduced processed foods, refined sugars, and alcohol.

The results were striking. The dietary support group showed a 7.1-point greater improvement on the MADRS (Montgomery–Åsberg Depression Rating Scale) compared to controls. More notably, 32% of the diet group achieved remission (MADRS ≤ 10) versus only 8% in the social support group — a number needed to treat of approximately 4, comparable to many pharmacological interventions for depression. Effect size was large (Cohen's d ≈ 1.16). Importantly, greater adherence to the new dietary pattern correlated more strongly with symptom improvement, suggesting a dose–response relationship rather than a placebo-driven result.

What SMILES does *not* tell us is *how* the diet worked. The Mediterranean pattern reduces systemic inflammation, improves cardiometabolic markers, and — as we know from Days 2 and 3 — shifts the gut microbiome toward increased SCFA-producing Firmicutes and reduced inflammatory taxa. But which of these pathways drove mood improvement is unknown; gut microbiome composition was not measured in the trial. The study was also small (67 participants) and had only one blinding level (participants obviously knew which diet they were eating). Subsequent dietary RCTs — notably the HELFIMED trial (2017, Mediterranean plus fish oil) and a 2022 Australian trial in young adults — have consistently shown moderate-to-large effects on depressive symptoms, suggesting the signal is real, but large multi-site trials with microbiome sub-studies are still needed to disentangle mechanisms.

### Fermented foods vs. dietary fibre: a cellular-level surprise

Intuition might suggest that dietary fibre — the primary substrate for gut microbial fermentation, generating SCFAs — would be the optimal dietary intervention for microbiome health. The Stanford Cell study offered a more nuanced picture.

In a 10-week randomised trial, 36 healthy adults were assigned either to a high-fermented-food diet (yogurt, kefir, fermented cottage cheese, kimchi, fermented vegetables, kombucha — reaching ~6 servings/day at peak) or a high-dietary-fibre diet (legumes, whole grains, fruit, vegetables, nuts — reaching ~45 g fibre/day). Researchers performed extraordinarily deep profiling: 16S rRNA and metagenomic sequencing of the gut microbiome alongside measurement of over 6,000 immune features and 19 pro-inflammatory proteins in blood.

The fermented-food group showed a consistent increase in gut microbiome diversity (a marker generally associated with better health outcomes) and a broad decrease in 19 inflammatory proteins, including interleukin-6, IL-12p70, and IL-10. Four types of immune cells showed reduced activation. The high-fibre group, by contrast, showed no increase in microbiome diversity. Instead, fibre increased the diversity of microbial genes encoding carbohydrate-processing enzymes (CAZymes), suggesting the microbiome adapted *functionally* without expanding taxonomically. Most strikingly, individuals with low baseline microbiome diversity showed a slight *increase* in inflammatory markers on the high-fibre diet — paradoxically the opposite of the intended effect.

Two caveats matter here. First, the trial enrolled healthy adults, not people with depression or neurological conditions, so the inflammatory reduction does not straightforwardly translate to brain or mood benefit. Second, 10 weeks may not be long enough to capture the full adaptive trajectory of a fibre-rich diet. The authors hypothesised that fibre's full benefit may require a sufficiently diverse baseline microbiome to ferment it effectively — a "microbiome-readiness" prerequisite that has significant implications for personalised nutrition: a dietary intervention may help or be neutral depending on what microbiome the person brings to the table.

### Probiotics for depression and anxiety: a real effect, but modest and variable

Probiotics are the most commercially promoted gut–brain intervention. The evidence for their effect on clinical mental health has been accumulating steadily but is characterised by small sample sizes, heterogeneous formulations, and wide variance across trials.

The 2025 Nutrition Reviews meta-analysis provides the clearest current picture. Analysing 23 RCTs involving 1,401 patients with *diagnosed* depression or anxiety disorders, it found that probiotics significantly reduced depression symptoms (standardised mean difference –0.96; 95% CI –1.31 to –0.61) and anxiety symptoms (SMD –0.59; 95% CI –0.98 to –0.19). In effect-size terms, that represents a moderate-to-large effect for depression and a moderate effect for anxiety. Prebiotics alone — dietary supplements that feed existing microbiota rather than introducing new organisms — showed no significant effect on depression (SMD –0.28; 95% CI –0.61 to 0.04). Eight weeks appeared to be a minimum effective treatment duration.

The strains doing most of the work were members of the genera *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium*, consistent with Days 2 and 3's discussion of these organisms as producers of GABA precursors, tryptophan metabolites, and SCFAs. Specific strains with the most consistent evidence include *Lactobacillus acidophilus*, *L. rhamnosus*, *L. helveticus*, *Bifidobacterium longum*, and *B. bifidum*. However, no individual strain has been tested in enough large independent trials to receive a confident clinical recommendation for a specific disorder.

The honest bottom line on probiotics: the aggregate signal is real and non-trivial for depression specifically. But heterogeneity across trials is high — some show large effects, others near-zero. This variability probably reflects differences in participants' baseline microbiome (supplementing into an already-diverse community may do little), in the formulation (single-strain versus multi-strain), and in outcome measures. Probiotics in these trials are always *adjuncts* to standard care, not replacements. When the meta-analysis is restricted to patients on concurrent antidepressants, the probiotic effect persists, suggesting potential synergy rather than independent action.

### FMT beyond Parkinson's: what about psychiatric conditions?

As established in yesterday's reading, the first large FMT RCT in Parkinson's disease (Finnish JAMA Neurology trial, 2024, n = 47) showed no motor benefit at 12 months in established disease — a reminder that mechanistic plausibility does not equal clinical efficacy. FMT's strongest evidence base remains its approved indication: recurrent *Clostridioides difficile* infection, where multi-donor FMT achieves cure rates above 90%.

For psychiatric applications, a 2025 meta-analysis in Frontiers in Psychiatry of 12 FMT RCTs in 681 patients found a statistically significant reduction in depressive symptoms (SMD –1.21; 95% CI –1.87 to –0.55). The effect was largest in patients with IBS and comorbid depression — a population where the gut–brain connection is presumably tightest — and was seen with colonoscopic and enema delivery routes but not clearly with oral capsules. These results are promising but carry a major caveat: most included trials enrolled patients with depression *secondary to gastrointestinal disease*, and the generalisability to primary major depressive disorder is uncertain. Large trials of FMT for primary psychiatric depression are only now beginning enrolment.

### What doesn't work, or lacks sufficient evidence

- **Prebiotics alone** show no statistically significant effect on depression in the current meta-analytic dataset. They may potentiate probiotics in synbiotic combinations, but as isolated mood interventions they are not yet supported.
- **Generic "healthy eating" advice** without structured, intensive dietitian support has not been shown in RCTs to reduce clinical depression. The SMILES effect required seven individual consultations; a pamphlet or app-based nudge is not equivalent.
- **Single-dose FMT** for established neurological disease does not appear sufficient, as the PD trial demonstrated. Repeat dosing and earlier (prodromal) intervention are hypotheses under investigation.
- **Commercial probiotics marketed for mental health** are overwhelmingly formulated without clinical evidence for the specific strains, doses, or patient profiles shown to matter in trials. The market is largely unregulated in most countries. Even well-studied strains have not been tested in enough large independent trials to support confident individual clinical recommendations.

## Questions to think about

1. The SMILES trial showed that dietary improvement can reduce depression symptoms as an *adjunct* to existing pharmacotherapy or psychotherapy, with a number needed to treat of ~4. If this finding is robustly replicated at scale, should dietary assessment and structured dietary support become a standard component of depression treatment — and what structural barriers (clinician training, reimbursement, time) stand in the way?

2. The Wastyk et al. Cell study found that people with *low baseline microbiome diversity* had a paradoxically *worse* inflammatory response to the high-fibre diet. If microbiome composition shapes how well a dietary intervention works, does this imply we need microbiome profiling before dietary prescription — and what does that mean for universal dietary guidelines?

3. Effect sizes in probiotics meta-analyses are statistically significant for depression as a category, but individual trial results vary enormously with different strains, doses, durations, and patient profiles. Do you think "probiotics for mental health" is too heterogeneous a category to be clinically actionable — or is the right path forward precision matching of specific strains to specific microbial deficit profiles?
