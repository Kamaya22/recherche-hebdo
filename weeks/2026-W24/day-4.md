# The gut microbiome and the brain — Day 4/7: Neurodegenerative diseases — Parkinson's and Alzheimer's through the lens of the gut

## Context

Over the past three days we have built a picture of the gut microbiome as a biochemically active organ that communicates with the brain through the vagus nerve, the enteric nervous system, neuro-active metabolites (short-chain fatty acids, tryptophan derivatives, bile acids), and immune signalling. Today we examine the most striking application of that framework: the hypothesis — backed by growing mechanistic and epidemiological evidence — that two of humanity's most devastating brain diseases, Parkinson's and Alzheimer's, may originate in part in the gut.

This angle is not merely theoretical. For Parkinson's disease (PD), we have biopsies showing misfolded proteins in intestinal tissue *years* before motor symptoms appear; large epidemiological datasets showing that cutting the vagus nerve reduces PD risk; and animal experiments in which injecting the disease-associated protein into the colon triggers the full cascade of brain pathology. For Alzheimer's disease (AD), germ-free mouse models dramatically cut amyloid-beta plaque load, specific gut bacterial communities correlate with amyloid burden in living humans, and bacterial metabolites have been shown to either promote or suppress microglial clearance of amyloid in the brain.

The therapeutic corollary is equally striking: if the gut is a disease initiator, it is also a potential intervention site. Today we examine the evidence carefully — and end with what the first large randomised fecal microbiota transplantation trial in PD patients actually showed (spoiler: sobering).

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### Parkinson's disease: a disease that may begin in the bowel

The Braak hypothesis, proposed in the early 2000s by German neuropathologist Heiko Braak, holds that Parkinson's disease does not begin in the dopaminergic neurons of the substantia nigra — the cells whose death produces the motor symptoms — but much earlier, in the olfactory bulb and, crucially, in the enteric nervous system embedded in the gut wall. The hypothesis predicts a stereotyped, bottom-up propagation: alpha-synuclein (α-syn), the protein that misfolds and aggregates in PD, first accumulates in intestinal neurons, then spreads via the vagus nerve into the brainstem, and only years or decades later reaches the basal ganglia to cause motor disease.

The clinical evidence for this sequence is substantial. Alpha-synuclein aggregates have been found in gastrointestinal biopsies of PD patients — in roughly 75% of cases, compared with only 8–9% of healthy controls, according to a 2024 study in *npj Parkinson's Disease*. Critically, GI biopsies taken years before motor symptom onset in people who later developed PD also show this pathology, consistent with a prodromal gut phase. On the epidemiological side, individuals who received a *truncal vagotomy* (surgical severing of the vagus nerve) for peptic ulcers have a measurably lower risk of developing PD more than five years post-surgery — as if severing the highway blocks the protein traffic. Animal experiments completed this picture: injecting preformed α-syn fibrils into the colon of mice triggers ascending spread through the vagus nerve and full PD-like pathology in the brainstem and midbrain, faithfully replicating Braak's stages.

The gut microbiome's role in this story is twofold. First, *dysbiosis* — an altered microbial community — appears to be a consistent feature of PD. Multiple meta-analyses identify a convergent pattern: SCFA-producing bacteria (Prevotellaceae, Lachnospiraceae, Faecalibacterium, Roseburia, Blautia) are systematically depleted, while pro-inflammatory taxa (Verrucomicrobiaceae, Akkermansia, Enterobacteriaceae) are enriched. The depletion of SCFA producers matters because butyrate and propionate normally sustain the gut epithelial barrier and dampen intestinal immune activation; their loss promotes a "leaky gut," systemic inflammation, and potentially microglial priming in the brain. Second, certain gut bacteria appear to directly promote α-syn aggregation: bacterial amyloids, lipopolysaccharides, and metabolic by-products have been shown in cell and animal studies to seed or accelerate α-syn misfolding.

### Alzheimer's disease: the microbiome–amyloid–neuroinflammation triangle

The gut–Alzheimer's connection is built on a different but complementary set of findings. The most compelling experimental evidence comes from germ-free mouse models: when transgenic AD-like mice are raised without any gut microbiota, their cerebral amyloid-beta plaque burden drops dramatically. Conversely, colonising these germ-free mice with faecal microbiota from conventionally-raised AD mice restores the plaques. This is one of the strongest demonstrations in any model system that the microbiome is *causally* required for full amyloid pathology — though the translation to humans remains to be established.

In human studies, the pattern of dysbiosis in AD patients shares features with PD but has its own signature. Bacteroidota are typically enriched, while Firmicutes, Lachnospiraceae, and Bifidobacterium are depleted. Bifidobacterium depletion is particularly consistent across studies and of potential clinical interest, given that this genus is accessible through dietary interventions. A 2024 paper in *Nature Communications* described a specific mechanism: members of the Bacteroidota phylum suppress the phagocytic function of microglia — the brain's immune cells — impairing their ability to clear amyloid-beta plaques, potentially accelerating plaque accumulation.

The broader mechanistic cascade runs roughly as follows. Dysbiosis disrupts gut barrier integrity, allowing bacterial endotoxins (especially lipopolysaccharides, LPS) to enter systemic circulation — the so-called "leaky gut." LPS then crosses or permeabilises the blood–brain barrier, activating brain-resident microglia. Chronically activated microglia release pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β), which in turn promote amyloid-beta aggregation and tau hyper-phosphorylation. Additionally, microbial amyloid proteins (e.g., CsgA produced by *E. coli* in biofilms) can "cross-seed" human amyloid-beta aggregation through structural similarity. The net effect is a sustained low-grade neuroinflammatory state that accelerates the neuropathological hallmarks of AD.

Short-chain fatty acids are also central to this picture, though their role is paradoxical. Under normal conditions, butyrate maintains blood–brain barrier integrity, supports microglial homeostasis, and may inhibit histone deacetylases to enhance neuroprotective gene expression. In dysbiosis, the collapse of SCFA-producing communities removes this buffering, leaving the brain more vulnerable. However, some animal studies have shown that supra-physiological SCFA levels can paradoxically increase amyloid deposition, highlighting the importance of physiological balance rather than simple supplementation.

### The therapeutic frontier: FMT and probiotics

If the gut microbiome contributes causally to neurodegeneration, manipulating it should have therapeutic effects. The concept is biologically compelling, but the clinical data are sobering at this stage.

For Parkinson's disease, the first large, rigorously controlled FMT trial — a Finnish double-blind RCT published in *JAMA Neurology* in July 2024 — randomised 47 PD patients to FMT via colonoscopy or placebo, with 12 months of follow-up. The primary outcome was change in the MDS-UPDRS (the standard motor symptom scale, off-medication). The result: no statistically meaningful difference between groups. FMT was safe — gastrointestinal adverse events were more frequent in the FMT group (16 vs. 1), but none were serious — but it offered no clinically detectable benefit for motor or non-motor symptoms. The trial's authors noted that single-dose FMT may not be sufficient to alter entrenched dysbiosis in established PD; trials of repeat-dose FMT and earlier intervention (prodromal PD) are ongoing.

For Alzheimer's disease, probiotic trials using Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium strains have shown modest improvements in cognitive test scores in some RCTs, particularly in people with mild cognitive impairment rather than established AD. A 2024 systematic review of four RCTs found improvements in immediate and delayed memory scores, though effect sizes were small and heterogeneity across trials was high. FMT trials in AD are at an earlier stage than in PD.

### Caveats: correlation, causation, and the mouse problem

Two methodological cautions are essential. First, nearly all human microbiome studies in neurodegenerative diseases are cross-sectional or case-control designs: they compare the microbiome of diagnosed patients with healthy controls. This cannot distinguish whether dysbiosis *causes* disease, *results from* disease (e.g., dietary changes, reduced exercise, medication), or is merely correlated via shared risk factors. The strongest causal evidence still comes from animal models, where the translation gap is wide. Second, germ-free mouse studies tell us what happens in an extreme artificial condition — a mouse raised without any microbes — which has no direct human equivalent; "reduced microbiome" in human disease is far milder.

Despite these caveats, the convergence across epidemiological, mechanistic, and experimental lines of evidence is remarkable. The gut is almost certainly *part of the story* of both PD and AD; how large a part, and whether it can be therapeutically exploited before disease onset, are among the most active research questions in neuroscience today.

## Questions to think about

1. The Braak hypothesis predicts that Parkinson's disease starts in the gut. If this is correct, could a gut biopsy for alpha-synuclein become a routine early screening test — and what are the ethical implications of a positive result in an asymptomatic person?

2. The FMT trial in PD showed no motor benefit, but was conducted in patients with established disease. How might the result differ if FMT (or microbiome-targeted diet) were applied in the prodromal phase, before motor symptoms appear? What would such a trial look like?

3. The germ-free mouse experiments show that *no* microbiome dramatically reduces amyloid-beta plaques. But germ-free animals are immunologically abnormal in many other ways. How should we weigh this evidence — and what would be a stronger causal test in humans?
