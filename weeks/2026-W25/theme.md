# Week Theme: The science of sleep

## Why it matters

Sleep occupies roughly one-third of every human life, yet for most of the twentieth century it was treated as passive downtime — the brain simply switching off. The last two decades have overturned that view entirely. We now know that sleep is an intensely active biological state during which the brain replays and consolidates memories, synaptic strength is reset across the cortex, the immune system calibrates its responses, and hormones crucial for metabolism and growth are secreted in pulses precisely timed to sleep stages. At the population level, chronic sleep insufficiency — affecting roughly one-third of adults in high-income countries — is causally linked to elevated risks of Alzheimer's disease, cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, depression, and early mortality.

Understanding sleep matters for anyone who wants to understand the brain, preserve long-term health, or think clearly about public-health policy. It also matters because the fundamental question of *why* sleep is biologically necessary — what irreplaceable function it serves that wakefulness cannot — remains actively contested. The field is at an inflection point: the once-popular "brain-cleaning" glymphatic hypothesis has been challenged by 2024 experiments showing that clearance may actually *decrease* during sleep; the synaptic homeostasis hypothesis has matured into a rich research programme with direct molecular evidence in mammals; and new genetics tools are revealing unsuspected genetic diversity in sleep need across individuals and species.

This week we will build the picture from the ground up: starting with sleep architecture and the principal theories of sleep function, moving through neural circuits and oscillations, memory consolidation, sleep deprivation's consequences, individual and genetic differences, sleep across species and the lifespan, and ending with a synthesis of what is settled and what the most exciting open questions are.
