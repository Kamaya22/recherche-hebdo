# The science of sleep — Day 5/7: Individual differences, genetics, and sleep disorders — why we don't all sleep the same

## Context

Over the first four days of this week we have built a progressively richer picture of sleep. Day 1 established the architecture of a normal night — the NREM–REM cycles, the two-process model of homeostatic pressure and circadian drive, and the major theories of sleep's purpose. Day 2 opened the hood on the sleeping brain, tracing the thalamocortical and brainstem circuits that generate slow waves, spindles, and sharp-wave ripples. Day 3 followed the hippocampal–neocortical dialogue that consolidates fresh memories into durable knowledge. Day 4 looked at what breaks down when sleep is curtailed — the cognitive collapse, emotional dysregulation, metabolic disruption, cardiovascular strain, and immune suppression that accumulate with every hour of insufficient sleep.

Throughout these days, we have spoken largely about *the* human sleeper — an average, typical person. Yet almost anyone who has lived with other people will recognize that sleep need, sleep timing, and sleep quality vary enormously between individuals. Some adults function well on six hours; others are genuinely impaired at anything under nine. Some are constitutionally early risers who feel the pull of sleep at 9 p.m.; others are night owls for whom 2 a.m. is the most productive hour of the day. These differences are not merely habits or discipline failures — they are, to a significant degree, written into our biology and our genomes.

Today we examine that biological individuality. We will ask: how heritable are our sleep patterns? What have genome-wide association studies revealed about the genetic architecture of chronotype and sleep duration? What do rare, extreme mutations teach us about how sleep need is regulated at the molecular level? And what happens when those regulatory systems fail — producing the sleep disorders that affect hundreds of millions of people worldwide? This day is also, in a sense, a bridge: understanding the genetic machinery of normal sleep variation is essential context for understanding why sleep disorders are so diverse, so treatment-resistant, and so deeply personal.

## Today's readings (~22 min total)

**1. The Genetics of Human Sleep and Sleep Disorders**
Zou X, Ptáček LJ, Fu YH — *Annual Review of Genomics and Human Genetics*, 25, 259–285 (2024) — [https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-genom-121222-120306](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-genom-121222-120306) — estimated reading time: ~12 min (abstract; the sections on natural short sleep and Mendelian sleep disorders; the section on GWAS of sleep traits)

This comprehensive review, authored by the group at UCSF that has led the molecular genetics of human sleep for two decades, surveys the full landscape from rare Mendelian mutations causing extreme sleep phenotypes to the polygenic architecture of common traits like insomnia and chronotype. The authors summarize the discovery of natural short sleep genes (DEC2/BHLHE41, ADRB1, NPSR1, GRM1, SIK3), the molecular pathways by which each mutation shortens sleep need, and what animal models of these mutations reveal about sleep's core functions. *Annual Review of Genomics and Human Genetics* is a peer-reviewed review journal published by Annual Reviews, consistently ranked among the most authoritative synthesis venues in biomedical research. The authors (Ptáček and Fu) are the investigators who cloned the first human sleep gene.

**2. Genome-wide association analyses of chronotype in 697,828 individuals provides insights into circadian rhythms**
Jones SE, et al. — *Nature Communications*, 10, 343 (2019) — [https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-08259-7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-08259-7) — estimated reading time: ~5 min (abstract and Figures 1–2)

This landmark genome-wide association study, drawing on UK Biobank and 23andMe data, increased the number of genomic loci associated with being a morning versus evening person from 24 to 351 in a single step. The study finds that these loci cluster near known circadian clock genes (PER2, PER3, RORA, RGS16) and in pathways including retinal phototransduction, cAMP signaling, and glutamate signaling — consistent with the hypothesis that chronotype is determined primarily by clock speed and light-sensitivity. Mendelian randomization analyses further show that morning chronotype is causally associated with better mental health outcomes. *Nature Communications* is an open-access peer-reviewed journal from Nature Research covering the full spectrum of the natural sciences; the article is freely available.

**3. The immunopathogenesis of narcolepsy type 1**
Liblau RS, Latorre D, Kornum BR, Dauvilliers Y, Mignot EJ — *Nature Reviews Immunology*, 24, 33–48 (2024) — [https://www.nature.com/articles/s41577-023-00902-9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41577-023-00902-9) — estimated reading time: ~5 min (abstract and the sections on HLA associations and the T-cell hypothesis)

This authoritative review by five leading narcolepsy researchers — including Emmanuel Mignot, who discovered hypocretin/orexin deficiency as the cause of narcolepsy — traces the growing evidence that narcolepsy type 1 is an autoimmune disease in which the immune system destroys the ~70,000 orexin-producing neurons in the lateral hypothalamus. The review covers the extraordinary strength of the HLA-DQB1*06:02 genetic association, T cell and B cell evidence, and the natural experiment provided by the Pandemrix H1N1 vaccine, which tripled narcolepsy incidence in children in several countries and implicated molecular mimicry between the vaccine antigen and an orexin-neuron surface protein. *Nature Reviews Immunology* is the leading peer-reviewed review journal in immunology.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### How heritable is sleep?

Before asking which genes are involved, it is worth establishing that genes matter at all. Twin studies — which compare sleep traits in identical (monozygotic) versus fraternal (dizygotic) twins to partition variance into genetic and environmental components — have now been conducted for sleep duration, sleep quality, chronotype, and insomnia across many countries and age groups. The results are consistent: all major sleep phenotypes are moderately heritable. Habitual sleep duration shows heritability estimates in the range of 30–50% across studies. Chronotype (morning vs. evening preference) is approximately 50% heritable, with some estimates as high as 54% in large UK Biobank analyses. Insomnia symptoms show roughly 40% heritability (meta-analysis of 13 twin cohorts: h² = 0.39, 95% CI: 0.32–0.44). The remainder of the variance is attributable to environmental factors — light exposure, social schedules, stress, temperature, substance use — and to gene–environment interactions, meaning that the same genetic predispositions produce different sleep patterns in different environments.

These heritability estimates place sleep traits in the same tier as personality dimensions and mild psychiatric traits: substantially shaped by genes, but far from genetically determined. They justify the large-scale genetic studies that have followed.

### The GWAS revolution: 351 loci for a preference

For most of the history of sleep genetics, research focused on a small number of clock genes identified in model organisms — the Drosophila *period* and *timeless* genes, and their mammalian homologues PER1, PER2, PER3, CLOCK, BMAL1, CRY1, CRY2 — and asked whether variants in these genes predicted human chronotype. The answer was often: yes, modestly. Common variants in PER3 (a variable-number tandem repeat polymorphism, Val/Val vs. Met/Met) predict both chronotype and sensitivity to sleep deprivation, effects that have been replicated in multiple independent cohorts.

The genome-wide association study by Jones et al. (2019) transformed the field by doing the search without assumptions — scanning the entire genome simultaneously in nearly 700,000 participants. The result was 351 independent genomic loci associated with chronotype, many of them near previously implicated clock genes, but many in biological pathways not previously connected to circadian biology: retinal phototransduction (consistent with chronotype being shaped partly by light sensitivity), cAMP and insulin signaling, and glutamate receptor pathways. The magnitude of the genetic effect is modest but real: individuals in the top 5% of morning-preference allele burden sleep approximately 25 minutes earlier than individuals in the bottom 5%, a biologically meaningful difference in sleep timing that accumulates across a lifetime. The Mendelian randomization analyses are particularly notable: they provide causal evidence that being a morning person reduces risk of depression, independent of whether depression causes people to sleep differently.

### When the genetic dial is turned to an extreme: Mendelian sleep disorders

While GWAS captures the common, polygenic variation that shapes normal individual differences, a small number of families carry single mutations in core sleep-regulatory genes that produce extreme, dramatic phenotypes. These Mendelian cases have been extraordinarily informative about how sleep need is set at the molecular level.

**Familial Advanced Sleep Phase (FASP)** is the human circadian clock running fast. Affected individuals fall asleep around 7–9 p.m. and wake at 3–5 a.m., regardless of social context, and their core body temperature and melatonin rhythms are advanced by approximately four hours. Mutations have been identified in *PER2* (at the casein kinase Iε binding site, causing hypophosphorylation of PER2 and therefore faster degradation), *CSNK1D* (casein kinase 1 delta itself), and *CRY2* (at a residue that normally stabilizes the CRY2 protein). Each mutation accelerates the pace of the molecular clock. Familial Delayed Sleep Phase is the mirror image, and variants in CRY1 that slow clock speed have been identified in some families.

**Natural Short Sleep (familial natural short sleep, FNSS)** is perhaps the most scientifically productive of these rare syndromes. Affected individuals sleep only 4–6.5 hours per night, wake feeling fully rested, and show no cognitive or health consequences of their abbreviated sleep. They are not merely adapted to insufficient sleep — as established in Day 4, people who are chronically sleep-restricted but don't feel it accumulate measurable impairments. Natural short sleepers differ categorically: their homeostatic sleep pressure appears to be lower per hour of wakefulness, or their recovery is more efficient.

The Zou et al. (2024) review summarizes five genes now identified in FNSS families: *DEC2* (BHLHE41), *ADRB1*, *NPSR1*, *GRM1*, and *SIK3*. What is striking is the biological diversity of these pathways. DEC2 is a transcriptional repressor that acts partly through orexin (the wake-promoting neuropeptide discussed on Day 2) — its mutation leads to modestly elevated orexin, consistent with more efficient wakefulness. ADRB1 (β₁-adrenergic receptor) is expressed in a specific population of neurons in the dorsal pons; mice with the FNSS ADRB1 mutation have neurons in this region that are easier to activate and that respond more vigorously to arousal signals. NPSR1 and GRM1 involve neuropeptide S and metabotropic glutamate signaling respectively. SIK3 (salt-inducible kinase 3) has long been implicated in the biochemical tracking of sleep pressure in Drosophila and mice — the human FNSS SIK3 mutation suggests that sleep need is partly determined by how rapidly "sleepiness molecules" accumulate during wakefulness. The convergence of such diverse pathways on a common phenotype — short sleep need with no penalty — reveals that the homeostatic pressure for sleep is not a single molecular variable but a distributed state regulated by multiple interacting systems.

### Narcolepsy: the autoimmune destruction of wakefulness

Among the sleep disorders, narcolepsy type 1 (NT1) has the clearest genetic and mechanistic story, and it illustrates principles relevant to understanding individual differences in the broader sense. NT1 is caused by the loss of 70,000–90,000 neurons in the lateral hypothalamus that produce hypocretin-1 and hypocretin-2 (also called orexin-A and orexin-B) — the peptides discussed on Day 2 as critical stabilizers of the wake state. Without orexin, the switch between sleep and wakefulness becomes unstable: patients experience involuntary sleep episodes during the day, sudden muscle weakness triggered by emotion (cataplexy), hallucinations at sleep onset and awakening (hypnagogic/hypnopompic hallucinations), and sleep paralysis — all signs of inappropriately timed intrusions of REM-like sleep states into wakefulness.

The genetic architecture of NT1 is dominated by a single allele: HLA-DQB1\*06:02, present in over 90% of NT1 patients across all ethnic groups, compared with 15–25% of the general population. This is one of the strongest HLA associations known for any disease outside the rheumatic and autoimmune disorders — which is precisely what it implies. Liblau et al. (2024) review the now-substantial evidence that NT1 is an autoimmune disease in which CD4⁺ T cells (selected by the HLA-DQ6 molecule that HLA-DQB1\*06:02 encodes) target and destroy orexin neurons. Additional genetic risk loci at T cell receptor alpha and beta gene segments support the hypothesis that the disease is driven by a specific autoimmune T cell clone that recognizes an antigen on, or presented by, orexin neurons.

The natural experiment that clinched the autoimmune model was the AS03-adjuvanted H1N1 influenza vaccine Pandemrix, used in Europe during the 2009–10 pandemic. In Finland, Sweden, the UK, and Ireland, rates of new-onset narcolepsy in children and adolescents increased three- to twelve-fold in the six months following vaccination — a signal detected by pharmacovigilance systems and independently replicated. Studies subsequently identified a specific fragment of the Pandemrix H1N1 nucleoprotein that cross-reacts with a segment of the hypocretin receptor 2 (HCRT-R2) protein — a molecular mimicry mechanism by which the immune response to a viral antigen misdirects itself toward orexin neurons. Notably, natural H1N1 infection also increased narcolepsy risk in China in 2009, suggesting the virus itself, not just the vaccine adjuvant, can trigger the same mechanism. The Pandemrix story is one of the most important lessons in modern vaccine science: it does not argue against vaccination — the absolute risk increase was very small and the benefit of pandemic protection was substantial — but it illustrates how tightly immune and genetic factors interact to produce a sleep disorder.

### Common sleep disorders: insomnia and obstructive sleep apnea as complex traits

Narcolepsy and the rare circadian disorders are Mendelian at their core: single mutations, large effects, clear mechanisms. The two most prevalent sleep disorders — insomnia and obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) — are at the opposite end of the genetic complexity spectrum.

Insomnia disorder (defined not just as difficulty sleeping but as distress and daytime impairment) affects approximately 10–15% of adults and is highly comorbid with anxiety, depression, and chronic pain. Its 40% heritability means that genetic factors matter, but the genetic architecture is massively polygenic. The largest GWAS analyses have identified over 200 significant loci, yet these collectively explain only a small fraction (~7%) of phenotypic variance — the rest is attributable to rare variants not captured by standard arrays, gene–environment interactions, and environmental factors. Notably, the genetic correlations between insomnia and major depression, anxiety, and neuroticism are substantial (r_g ≈ 0.4–0.6), suggesting shared molecular substrates. This genetic overlap is consistent with the bidirectionality observed clinically and discussed on Day 4: sleep dysregulation and mood dysregulation amplify each other through shared circuits, and it is not always clear which came first.

OSA — the recurrent collapse of the upper airway during sleep that produces hypoxic arousals, fragmented sleep, and cardiovascular strain — has a different genetic architecture, partly because its causes are partly anatomical (craniofacial structure, pharyngeal muscle tone, obesity, sex-specific fat distribution). Nevertheless, approximately 40% of its variance is heritable, and large GWAS studies — including one of over 568,000 participants from the Million Veteran Program published in eBioMedicine in 2023 — have identified sex-specific loci and links to cardiometabolic pathways. OSA illustrates a general principle: even when a disorder has obvious structural causes (a narrow airway), genetic factors still predict who, among people with similar anatomy, actually develops the disorder.

### The bigger picture: sleep individuality is real, and consequential

Taken together, today's readings point to a coherent conclusion: there is no single "correct" human sleep phenotype. Sleep need, sleep timing, and sleep architecture are biologically variable traits, substantially shaped by hundreds to thousands of genetic variants acting on circadian clock speed, homeostatic pressure accumulation and discharge, arousal system sensitivity, and immune-neural interactions. The range is not narrow: at the population level, the difference in optimal sleep duration between a natural short sleeper and a long sleeper may exceed three hours — and neither is pathological.

This has important practical implications. Blanket advice to "sleep eight hours" is a population average, not a biological prescription for every individual. The perception gap identified in Day 4 — in which chronically sleep-deprived people cannot feel their impairment — coexists with the opposite phenomenon: people who genuinely need less sleep but are made to feel they are deficient. Distinguishing chronic sleep insufficiency from genetic short sleep, or a morning-type person from a disciplined insomniac, requires taking the biology of individual differences seriously. And recognizing that sleep disorders like narcolepsy represent the failure of specific, identifiable molecular pathways opens the door to targeted therapies — indeed, hypocretin agonists for narcolepsy and targeted approaches for insomnia are active areas of drug development, with more tractable targets than those for most complex neurological conditions.

## Questions to think about

1. The natural short sleep mutations discovered so far are extraordinarily rare — individually affecting perhaps one in a million people. Yet they reveal distinct molecular pathways through which sleep need can be reduced without apparent penalty. Does the existence of these "penalty-free" short sleepers challenge the view that sleep is essential, or does it refine it? What do these genetic findings imply about the specific functions of sleep that can and cannot be "optimized away" by a mutation?

2. The narcolepsy story shows that a mild molecular mimicry between an influenza protein and a neuronal surface protein — in the right HLA genetic context — can trigger autoimmune destruction of a small, functionally critical population of neurons. What does this suggest about the potential for other sleep or neurological disorders to have similarly triggered autoimmune origins that we haven't yet identified? And what surveillance systems would we need to detect such signals prospectively?

3. Insomnia shares substantial genetic architecture with depression and anxiety (genetic correlations of ~0.4–0.6). Does this shared genetic substrate imply that insomnia disorder is better conceptualized as a transdiagnostic dimension of negative arousal rather than a distinct sleep disorder? And what are the clinical implications — for treatment sequencing, for drug development, for how we counsel patients — if sleep disturbance and mood dysregulation are partly the same biological process?
