# The science of sleep — Day 4/7: What happens when we don't sleep — consequences of sleep deprivation

## Context

Over the past three days we have built a detailed picture of sleep as active biological work: the two-process model that times sleep and enforces its depth (Day 1), the thalamocortical and brainstem circuits whose coordinated oscillations generate slow waves, spindles, and sharp-wave ripples (Day 2), and the hippocampal-neocortical dialogue through which those oscillations consolidate fresh memories into durable knowledge while REM sleep processes the emotional charge attached to experience (Day 3). Today we approach the same territory from the opposite direction. Rather than asking what sleep *does*, we ask what breaks down when it is curtailed — and the answer is: nearly everything.

Sleep deprivation is not a rare or exotic experimental condition. The CDC estimates that one-third of American adults regularly sleep fewer than seven hours per night, the minimum recommended for adults by the American Academy of Sleep Medicine. Epidemiological data link this pattern to elevated risks of obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, depression, cancer incidence, and all-cause mortality. Experimental sleep-restriction studies go further by establishing *mechanism*: they show that measurable impairments in attention, emotional regulation, immune competence, and metabolic control emerge within days — or even after a single night of insufficient sleep.

A critical framing point for today's reading is the *perception gap*. A landmark study by Van Dongen, Maislin, Mullington, and Dinges (*Sleep*, 2003) followed participants restricted to four, six, or eight hours per night for fourteen consecutive days. Those sleeping six hours accumulated, by day 14, as much objective neurobehavioral impairment as subjects kept awake for 48 hours straight — yet their subjective sleepiness ratings had plateaued by day 3 and did not continue rising. They reported feeling only mildly sleepy while performing as though severely sleep-deprived. The brain's capacity for self-assessment depends partly on prefrontal circuits that are themselves among the most sensitive targets of sleep loss; impairment disables the very machinery needed to perceive it. This perception gap is arguably the field's most practically important finding.

## Today's readings (~20 min total)

**1. The sleep-deprived human brain**
Krause AJ, Ben Simon E, Mander BA, Greer SM, Saletin JM, Goldstein-Piekarski AN, Walker MP — *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 18(7), 404–418 (2017) — [https://www.nature.com/articles/nrn.2017.55](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrn.2017.55) — estimated reading time: ~12 min (abstract, plus the sections on attention and working memory, and on negative and positive emotion)

A comprehensive review by Matthew Walker's group at UC Berkeley, synthesizing human neuroimaging, behavioral, and electrophysiological data on what sleep deprivation does to the brain across four domains: attention and working memory, positive emotion, negative emotion, and hippocampal learning. The review identifies a key neural hallmark of sleep loss — large-scale network *instability*, in which the brain oscillates unpredictably between near-normal and severely impaired states rather than degrading uniformly. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* is a high-impact peer-reviewed journal publishing authoritative review articles across all areas of neuroscience.

**2. Short sleep duration and cardiometabolic risk: from pathophysiology to clinical evidence**
Tobaldini E, Fiorelli EM, Solbiati M, Costantino G, Nobili L, Montano N — *Nature Reviews Cardiology*, 16, 213–224 (2019) — [https://www.nature.com/articles/s41569-018-0109-6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41569-018-0109-6) — estimated reading time: ~8 min (abstract and the sections on autonomic nervous system, endothelial function, and metabolic regulation)

A mechanistic review tracing the pathways through which short sleep elevates cardiovascular and metabolic risk: chronic overactivation of the sympathetic nervous system, impaired endothelial vasodilation, dysregulated glucose metabolism, systemic inflammation, and altered coagulation. The article integrates prospective cohort data showing substantially elevated cardiovascular event risk with sleep restriction alongside experimental mechanistic evidence, and discusses Mendelian randomization studies that help disentangle causation from correlation. *Nature Reviews Cardiology* is the leading peer-reviewed review journal in cardiovascular medicine.

**3. Role of sleep deprivation in immune-related disease risk and outcomes**
Garbarino S, Lanteri P, Bragazzi NL, Magnavita N, Scoditti E — *Communications Biology*, 4, 1304 (2021) — [https://www.nature.com/articles/s42003-021-02825-4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s42003-021-02825-4) — estimated reading time: ~5 min (abstract, introduction, and the section on innate and adaptive immunity alterations)

An integrative review documenting how sleep loss disrupts both arms of the immune system — reducing natural killer cell cytotoxicity, impairing T cell proliferation and cytokine signaling, and elevating pro-inflammatory markers — and tracing how these changes propagate into elevated long-term risk for infectious, autoimmune, cardiometabolic, neoplastic, and neurodegenerative diseases. *Communications Biology* is a peer-reviewed, fully open-access journal from Nature Research.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### The cognitive cost: a brain that cannot sustain attention

The most immediately observable effect of sleep deprivation is a collapse of *vigilant attention* — the ability to sustain focused monitoring over an extended period. As noted on Day 1, approximately 17–19 hours of continuous wakefulness produces measurable reaction-time impairment comparable to legal intoxication in many jurisdictions. The psychomotor vigilance test (PVT), which asks participants to press a button the moment a stimulus appears, reveals this deterioration with great sensitivity. After one night of total sleep deprivation, PVT lapses (failures to respond within 500 ms) increase two- to threefold; after the 14-day six-hour restriction in the Van Dongen et al. (2003) study, they reach levels equivalent to two nights of complete sleep loss.

The prefrontal cortex is disproportionately affected. Neuroimaging shows reduced metabolic activity, attenuated task-evoked activation, and disrupted connectivity between prefrontal regions and the parietal and subcortical structures with which they normally cooperate. Because the prefrontal cortex underpins working memory (holding and manipulating information "online"), executive control (suppressing inappropriate responses), and planning (modeling the future consequences of actions), sleep deprivation produces a behavioral profile resembling frontal lobe compromise: impulsive, short-horizon decision-making, difficulty suppressing automatic responses, and failures of prospective memory — forgetting to carry out intended actions. All of this is worsened by the perception gap: the prefrontal cortex is also what we rely on to monitor our own cognitive state, and when it is impaired, we lose the ability to detect the impairment.

### The emotional brain without its governor

Krause et al. (2017) review what is perhaps the most striking single neuroimaging finding of the sleep deprivation literature: the amygdala shows markedly elevated reactivity to negative stimuli in sleep-deprived subjects, while simultaneously losing its functional connectivity with the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) — the region whose top-down regulation normally contextualizes and moderates emotional reactions. The result is a brain that responds intensely to potential threats but cannot suppress, reframe, or contextualize those responses.

This connects directly to what we covered on Day 3. REM sleep, concentrated in the second half of the night (and therefore disproportionately lost when wake-up times are fixed early), plays a specific role in processing emotionally charged memories. During REM, the noradrenergic system — which drives acute emotional arousal — is uniquely suppressed, creating what Walker and colleagues have described as an "overnight therapy" window: memory reactivation can occur in a low-arousal neurochemical environment, allowing the emotional weight attached to an experience to be gradually reduced while its factual content is preserved. When REM is curtailed, this detachment process is interrupted. Negative memories retain their full emotional charge, intrusive thoughts persist, and the following day begins with the amygdala already sensitized. Epidemiologically, disrupted REM sleep is one of the most consistent predictors of depression, anxiety disorders, and post-traumatic stress disorder — a causal connection, not merely a correlation, that is now supported by targeted experimental studies.

### Metabolic dysregulation: the hungry, insulin-resistant body

The body's energy regulation is exquisitely sensitive to sleep duration. Within one week of sleeping five hours per night in controlled laboratory studies, healthy participants show a cluster of metabolic changes that together define early-stage cardiometabolic risk:

- **Reduced insulin sensitivity**: glucose clearance slows measurably, reaching values that in some protocols approach pre-diabetic thresholds.
- **Suppressed leptin**: the satiety hormone released by adipose tissue, which signals the hypothalamus that energy stores are adequate, falls sharply — mimicking a starvation signal.
- **Elevated ghrelin**: the hunger-promoting peptide secreted by the stomach rises, driving appetite and food-seeking behavior, with particular craving for high-calorie, carbohydrate-rich foods.
- **Elevated late-day cortisol**: the stress hormone, which promotes hepatic glucose production and visceral fat deposition, shows an abnormal peak in the evening that is not seen in well-rested individuals.

Tobaldini et al. (2019) trace these metabolic changes back to a common upstream driver: the chronic hyperactivation of the *sympathetic nervous system* caused by sleep loss. Sympathetic excess directly impairs insulin signaling in skeletal muscle and liver through beta-adrenergic receptor pathways, and drives adipocyte lipolysis, flooding the circulation with free fatty acids that further impair insulin sensitivity. Mendelian randomization studies — natural experiments using genetic variants that influence habitual sleep duration to disentangle cause from effect — confirm that the association between short sleep and obesity, hypertension, and coronary heart disease is at least partially *causal*, not merely reflecting shared lifestyle factors or reverse causation.

### Cardiovascular strain: the recovery window that never opens

The cardiovascular system exploits deep NREM sleep as a scheduled maintenance window. During N3 slow-wave sleep (discussed on Days 1 and 2), the parasympathetic nervous system dominates cardiac regulation: heart rate slows, blood pressure falls by 10–20% below its daytime values (the "nocturnal dip"), and the vascular endothelium undergoes nitric oxide–mediated repair. The nocturnal dip is not a passive consequence of lying still — it is an actively regulated phenomenon, and people who fail to exhibit it ("non-dippers") carry substantially elevated risks of stroke, myocardial infarction, and left ventricular hypertrophy, independent of their mean 24-hour blood pressure.

Sleep deprivation eliminates or attenuates this dip. Even restricting sleep to five hours for a single night produces elevated blood pressure the following morning. Chronically, short sleep duration is associated with endothelial dysfunction — assessed as reduced flow-mediated dilation of the brachial artery — elevated circulating inflammatory markers (C-reactive protein, interleukin-6, tumor necrosis factor-α), and altered coagulation profiles (elevated fibrinogen, increased platelet aggregability). Tobaldini et al. report that prospective cohort data consistently link self-reported short sleep with 40–48% elevated relative risk of cardiovascular death, effects that survive adjustment for smoking, obesity, physical activity, diabetes, and socioeconomic status.

Emerging evidence highlights an additional mechanism: dysregulated *hematopoiesis*. Chronic sleep insufficiency, mediated by sustained sympathetic activation acting on bone marrow, accelerates the production of monocytes and neutrophils — inflammatory leukocytes that circulate in elevated numbers and are more likely to home to sites of arterial injury, promoting atherosclerotic plaque development. This pathway, identified in mouse models and supported by human observational data, connects sleep loss directly to the biology of arterial inflammation.

### The immune system: activated but undermined

Sleep is not merely permissive for immune function — it is actively immunosupportive. During the first half of the night, surges in slow-wave sleep coincide with peaks in pro-inflammatory cytokine secretion (IL-1β, TNF-α) and natural killer (NK) cell mobilization from lymph nodes into circulation. These cytokines in turn feed back on the brain to deepen and prolong NREM sleep — a bidirectional dialogue that optimizes both immune defense and sleep quality simultaneously. Garbarino et al. (2021) review extensive evidence that this dialogue is disrupted at multiple levels by sleep loss.

At the cellular level, NK cell cytotoxicity — the capacity of these first-responder lymphocytes to kill virally infected and malignant cells — falls significantly after even a single night of partial sleep deprivation. In studies by Michael Irwin's group at UCLA (FASEB Journal, 1994 and 1996), one night of sleep restricted to early morning hours reduced NK cell lytic activity to approximately 72% of rested baseline, recovering within one night of normal sleep. T cell proliferative responses and antibody production are similarly impaired by sustained restriction. A widely cited study by Cohen, Doyle, Alper, Janicki-Deverts, and Turner (*Archives of Internal Medicine*, 2009) provides perhaps the most vivid real-world demonstration: 153 healthy volunteers were inoculated with rhinovirus after two weeks of monitored natural sleep; those who had been sleeping fewer than six hours per night in the preceding period developed clinical colds at significantly higher rates than those sleeping seven or more hours, a dose-response relationship independent of psychological stress, pre-existing antibody titers, and socioeconomic status.

The paradox noted by Garbarino et al. is that sleep deprivation simultaneously *activates* the immune system — raising circulating inflammatory cytokines and leukocyte counts — while *undermining* its directed efficacy. The resulting state resembles the sterile, misdirected inflammation of metabolic syndrome: elevated inflammatory noise without corresponding gain in pathogen defense, and with tissue damage as a byproduct. This connects sleep deprivation's immune consequences directly to its cardiovascular consequences — both are manifestations of a shared inflammatory dysregulation.

### Putting it together: a cascade with no off switch

The four domains covered today — cognitive, emotional, metabolic, and cardiovascular/immune — are not parallel columns of harm. They are a cascade, linked through shared machinery and mutual amplification. Sympathetic hyperactivation degrades sleep architecture (fewer long SWS bouts, more arousals), which prevents the deep stages that would dampen sympathetic tone the following night — a self-reinforcing loop. Prefrontal impairment from sleep loss reduces the behavioral capacity to seek better sleep, exercise, or manage diet. Emotional dysregulation from disrupted REM elevates perceived stress, which further activates the sympathetic and HPA axes, which further fragments sleep. Inflammation in the vasculature and brain contributes to the fatigue and mood disturbance that reduce motivation for health behaviors.

This cascade framing has shifted how leading researchers and public-health bodies now position sleep. Rather than treating insufficient sleep as a lifestyle preference ("I'm just not a sleeper"), the field increasingly views habitual short sleep as a *modifiable risk factor* — comparable in attributable risk to smoking, physical inactivity, or poor diet — for a cluster of diseases that together account for the majority of premature mortality in high-income countries. The implication is not just individual: because sleep patterns are profoundly shaped by structural factors (school start times, shift work schedules, light pollution, economic precarity, caretaking demands), addressing sleep insufficiency at the population level will likely require policy interventions, not just behavioral advice.

## Questions to think about

1. The perception gap — the finding that sleep-deprived people systematically underestimate their own impairment — makes self-report unreliable in safety-critical settings such as aviation, medicine, and long-haul transport. What objective, real-time measures of neurobehavioral impairment could plausibly be deployed in operational environments (not just sleep laboratories), and what ethical, legal, and practical obstacles would such monitoring face?

2. Several of the metabolic consequences of sleep restriction — elevated cortisol, insulin resistance, increased craving for calorie-dense food, impaired executive function over dietary choices — are also features of psychological stress and economic precarity. How does this co-occurrence complicate efforts to disentangle sleep loss from stress in epidemiological studies, and what are the implications for public-health messaging that focuses on individual sleep behavior rather than structural determinants?

3. The bidirectional link between sleep and inflammation means that conditions like depression, metabolic syndrome, and chronic pain both worsen sleep and are worsened by poor sleep. What does this bidirectionality imply for clinical trial design when testing sleep interventions in populations with comorbidities? And does it change how you think about the causal estimates from the Mendelian randomization studies discussed in today's readings?
