# The science of sleep — Day 6/7: Sleep across the lifespan and across species

## Context

Over the past five days we have built an increasingly detailed picture of what sleep is and what it does. Day 1 established the architecture of a normal night and the two-process model of homeostatic and circadian regulation. Day 2 opened the hood on the sleeping brain — the thalamocortical slow waves, spindles, and hippocampal sharp-wave ripples that characterize NREM, and the desynchronized, brainstem-driven activity of REM. Day 3 traced how the sleeping brain consolidates freshly encoded memories through hippocampal–neocortical dialogue. Day 4 documented the multi-system damage that accumulates when sleep is curtailed. Day 5 showed that sleep need, timing, and architecture are substantially heritable traits shaped by hundreds of genetic variants — and that when key sleep-regulatory genes are mutated, the consequences range from narcolepsy to extreme phase shifts to genuine reductions in sleep need.

All of that was largely the story of *one* sleeper at *one* moment in time — a typical adult mammal. Today we zoom out in two directions at once. Looking backward in time across evolution, we ask: is sleep ancient? Did our ancestors sleep? Do jellyfish sleep? What does the distribution of sleep states across the animal kingdom tell us about sleep's deepest functions? And looking across the human lifespan, we ask: how does sleep change from before birth to very old age, and what do those changes mean for brain development, memory, and health?

These two perspectives — evolutionary and developmental — are more connected than they might appear. The infant's brain is in many ways recapitulating an evolutionary programme: the enormous proportion of time that fetuses and newborns spend in active (REM-like) sleep mirrors what we see in species whose brains are entirely immature at birth. Conversely, the deterioration of slow-wave sleep in old age may expose which functions of deep sleep are hardest to sustain as the brain ages — and perhaps which were most critical throughout evolution.

## Today's readings (~21 min total)

**1. Partial homologies between sleep states in lizards, mammals, and birds suggest a complex evolution of sleep states in amniotes**
Libourel P-A, Barrillot B, Arthaud S, Massot B, Morel A-L, Beuf O, Herrel A, Luppi P-H — *PLOS Biology*, 16(10): e2005982 (2018) — [https://journals.plos.org/plosbiology/article?id=10.1371/journal.pbio.2005982](https://journals.plos.org/plosbiology/article?id=10.1371/journal.pbio.2005982) — estimated reading time: ~8 min (abstract, Introduction, and Discussion) — **open access**

This landmark study simultaneously recorded brain electrical activity, eye movements, and muscle tone in two lizard species — the Australian bearded dragon (*Pogona vitticeps*) and the Argentine tegu (*Salvator merianae*) — while they slept. The authors found that both species cycle between NREM-like slow-wave states and REM-like states with eye movements, confirming that two electrophysiological sleep states predate the evolutionary split between reptiles and mammals — a split that occurred approximately 320 million years ago. Critically, the paper also shows that the two lizard sleep states are *partially* homologous to mammalian NREM/REM, not identical: the reptilian "REM" state lacks the full cortical desynchronization and muscle atonia seen in mammals, pointing to a complex evolutionary history rather than a single clean origin. *PLOS Biology* is a fully open-access, peer-reviewed journal from the Public Library of Science; this article is freely available in full.

**2. Sleep as a driver of pre- and postnatal brain development**
de Groot ER, Dudink J, Austin T — *Pediatric Research*, 96(6): 1503–1509 (2024) — [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41390-024-03371-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41390-024-03371-5) — estimated reading time: ~5 min (abstract and sections on neonatal sleep and brain connectivity)

This concise review, authored by clinician-researchers at Cambridge, revisits Howard Roffwarg's 1966 "ontogenetic hypothesis" — the proposition that the active (REM-like) sleep that dominates fetal and neonatal life is not accidental but drives the very growth and wiring of the developing brain. The authors survey what half a century of animal experiments and, more recently, neonatal neuroimaging have established: that active sleep is the primary state of the fetal nervous system from at least 24 weeks of gestation, that it generates spontaneous neural activity patterns that sculpt synaptic connectivity across the brain, and that preterm infants who are deprived of adequate active sleep show measurable differences in white-matter development. *Pediatric Research* is the flagship peer-reviewed journal of the International Pediatric Research Foundation, published by Nature Portfolio.

**3. Sleep and Human Aging**
Mander BA, Winer JR, Walker MP — *Neuron*, 94: 19–36 (2017) — [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2017.02.004](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2017.02.004) — estimated reading time: ~8 min (abstract and the sections on NREM sleep changes and their cognitive consequences)

This widely cited review by Matthew Walker's laboratory at Berkeley — the same group that produced the foundational memory-consolidation work summarized on Day 3 — synthesizes how every dimension of sleep degrades with aging, with particular focus on slow-wave sleep. The central finding documented here is that the prefrontal cortex, which coordinates slow-wave generation and the slow-wave–spindle coupling needed to transfer hippocampal memories to long-term storage, undergoes the sharpest age-related structural decline, producing a 75–80% reduction in frontal slow-wave activity between young adulthood and old age. The authors trace the consequences through the memory system and to Alzheimer's disease risk. *Neuron* is the leading peer-reviewed journal in cellular and systems neuroscience, published by Cell Press; it is consistently ranked among the highest-impact journals in neuroscience.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### Sleep is ancient — and pre-neural

Before asking how human sleep changes across a lifetime, we need to ask how old sleep itself is. The answer is: extraordinarily old — and its origin predates even the nervous system.

The operational definition of sleep used in modern comparative biology rests on four behavioral criteria, deliberately designed to work without EEG electrodes: (1) a period of behavioral quiescence, typically in a species-specific posture or location; (2) reduced responsiveness to external stimuli, distinguishing sleep from simple rest; (3) rapid reversibility, distinguishing it from coma or hibernation; and (4) homeostatic rebound — an increase in sleep pressure and subsequent compensatory sleep following deprivation. Using these criteria, sleep has now been documented in every major animal phylum tested: vertebrates of course, but also arthropods (fruit flies, honeybees, crayfish), cephalopods (cuttlefish cycle through quiet and active phases with color changes resembling REM), and — most strikingly — cnidarians.

The jellyfish *Cassiopea* has no brain and no centralized nervous system, only a diffuse net of approximately 10,000 neurons. In experiments published in *Current Biology* in 2017 (Nath et al.), researchers found that *Cassiopea* satisfies all four behavioral criteria: it pulses less frequently at night, responds more slowly to perturbation (water jets) at night than during the day, and, after nocturnal disturbance, pulses less vigorously the next morning — a sleep rebound. If sleep occurs in an animal with no central nervous system, sleep cannot be principally a function of a brain resting or reorganizing. This points toward a more fundamental cellular-level function — recent work on *Cassiopea* and other cnidarians (2025) has found that sleep deprivation increases neuronal DNA damage and that sleep facilitates genome stability, suggesting sleep may have originated as a mechanism for repairing cellular — and specifically neural — DNA damage accumulating during wakefulness. That function would be useful in any organism with neurons, regardless of centralization.

### From one sleep state to two: the amniote story

In vertebrates, EEG recordings have long distinguished two fundamentally different types of sleep: NREM (slow waves, spindles, synchronized firing) and REM (desynchronized cortex, active brainstem, rapid eye movements, muscle atonia). For decades, the prevailing view held that this bifurcation was a mammalian and avian invention — that reptiles and other "lower" vertebrates had only a single undifferentiated sleep state.

That view was overturned by a 2016 study of the bearded dragon (*Pogona vitticeps*) by Shein-Idelson and colleagues, and then refined and extended by Libourel et al. in the PLOS Biology study from today's readings. By recording local field potentials, eye movements, and muscle tone simultaneously in freely sleeping bearded dragons and Argentine tegus, Libourel's group confirmed that both lizard species cycle between slow-wave states and states featuring reduced cortical synchrony with isolated eye movements — a pattern structurally analogous to mammalian NREM–REM cycling. The key nuance is "partial homology": reptilian slow-wave sleep generates the same general pattern of high-amplitude, low-frequency cortical oscillations, but the corresponding REM-like phase lacks the full cortical desynchronization and generalized muscle atonia of mammalian REM. This suggests that the common ancestor of all amniotes (reptiles, birds, mammals — that ancestor lived roughly 320 million years ago) already had two distinct sleep states, but that the states have evolved in different directions in different lineages since. Birds have NREM and REM sleep that closely resemble those of mammals, and some bird species (like the white-crowned sparrow during migration) dramatically reduce sleep with minimal apparent cognitive penalty — a natural experiment that reveals which aspects of sleep are most conserved and which are most plastic.

### Fetal and neonatal life: active sleep as a construction tool

Nowhere in the human lifespan is sleep stranger — or more important — than before and just after birth.

From approximately 24 weeks of gestation, the human fetus spends most of its time in a state that resembles REM sleep: the brain is electrically active, eye movements occur, the body makes brief twitches, and the cortex is in a state of ongoing, spontaneous neural firing. At term (40 weeks), newborns spend roughly 50% of their total sleep time — which itself totals 16–18 hours per day — in this "active sleep." By comparison, healthy adults spend approximately 20–25% of sleep time in REM, and that proportion declines further with age.

Howard Roffwarg's ontogenetic hypothesis, proposed in 1966 and now substantially supported by experimental evidence (as reviewed by de Groot et al., 2024), holds that this developmental dominance of active sleep is not coincidental: the spontaneous bursts of activity that the brain generates during active sleep act as an endogenous training signal for developing neural circuits. In the visual system, for instance, retinal waves of spontaneous activity during active sleep are essential for the precise wiring of the lateral geniculate nucleus and visual cortex — an effect demonstrated in animals where suppressing active sleep or eliminating retinal input during development produces abnormal wiring that persists into adulthood. In the motor system, the twitches of neonatal active sleep are not random: they are generated by specific brainstem circuits and appear to calibrate the proprioceptive maps that will later coordinate purposeful movement. And at the level of white-matter microstructure, neonatal imaging studies have found that preterm infants who experience more active sleep show better-developed corticospinal and thalamocortical tracts at term-equivalent age, independent of other confounders.

This is a striking inversion of the adult story: whereas in adults, REM sleep acts primarily on already-formed memories (emotional processing, procedural memory, creative integration — as discussed on Day 3), in the fetal and neonatal period, active sleep is constructive — it is wiring the circuits that will later be used for all cognition. The developmental transition from "active sleep dominates" to "NREM dominates" across the first year of life reflects the gradual maturation of the brainstem and thalamocortical circuits that suppress REM generation and produce the coordinated slow oscillations of adult NREM.

### Infancy, childhood, and the maturing architecture

The first year of life is a period of rapid reorganization of sleep architecture. Newborns enter sleep through active (REM-like) sleep rather than NREM — the opposite of adults — and sleep cycles are only about 50 minutes long, compared with the 90-minute adult cycle. By 3–6 months, sleep onset shifts to NREM first, sleep cycles lengthen, and the circadian clock begins to entrain to the light–dark cycle, producing the night-consolidation of sleep that parents experience as "sleeping through the night."

Across childhood, slow-wave sleep (NREM stage 3) actually *increases* in absolute depth and density, reaching a peak in middle childhood (roughly 8–11 years). Slow-wave activity per unit of time is higher in children than in any other age group — an EEG signature of the intense synaptic remodelling that occurs during the school-age years. Sleep spindle density also increases across childhood and peaks near adolescence. These are not merely technical curiosities: the increase in spindle activity correlates with gains in fluid intelligence across development, consistent with the hypothesis (introduced on Day 3) that spindles coordinate hippocampal-to-cortical memory transfer, and that this process is particularly active and efficient in children.

### Adolescence: the biological–social "perfect storm"

Adolescence brings a dramatic reconfiguration of the two-process model described on Day 1. Two changes occur simultaneously. First, the homeostatic accumulation of sleep pressure (Process S) slows down during adolescence: teenagers need to be awake longer than adults before achieving the same level of sleepiness, meaning their natural bedtime is pushed later. Second, the circadian clock undergoes a phase delay during puberty — the biological evening signal (melatonin onset) shifts approximately two hours later, so the adolescent body does not feel sleepy until midnight or after, while waking is correspondingly delayed. Both of these changes are documented across cultures and appear to be driven by pubertal hormones, possibly through direct effects on clock gene expression and on the sensitivity of the suprachiasmatic nucleus to light in the evening.

Into this biological setup, Crowley, Wolfson, Tarokh, and Carskadon (Journal of Adolescence, 2018) add what they call the "perfect storm": artificial light and screen use in the evenings (which suppress melatonin via retinal blue-light exposure and further delay the circadian clock), social pressures that reinforce late bedtimes, and — crucially — school start times that have historically been set between 7 and 8 a.m. for high schools, precisely the hours when the adolescent circadian system is at its physiological nadir. The result is that the median high-school student in the United States obtains approximately 6.5 hours of sleep on school nights — roughly two hours below the 8–10 hours recommended for adolescents. The cognitive, emotional, and metabolic consequences parallel those described for adult sleep restriction on Day 4, but with the added concern that adolescence is a period of ongoing prefrontal cortical maturation: disrupting sleep during this window may have developmental consequences that outlast the sleep deprivation itself. Evidence from districts that shifted high school start times to 8:30 a.m. or later shows improvements in attendance, graduation rates, and reduced adolescent car accident rates — a rare example of a public-health intervention directly grounded in sleep biology.

### Adulthood and aging: the slow decline of slow waves

Once the adolescent phase delay resolves in the early twenties and sleep timing stabilizes, sleep architecture remains relatively stable through early and middle adulthood — though sleep quantity and consolidation begin to show subtle declines from the mid-forties onward. The major age-related changes documented in polysomnographic studies are consistent across populations: increased sleep latency (taking longer to fall asleep), increased number and duration of nocturnal awakenings, decreased sleep efficiency (ratio of time asleep to time in bed), advanced sleep timing (the circadian clock phase-advances with age, reversing the adolescent delay), and — most consequentially — a progressive decline in slow-wave sleep.

The Mander et al. (2017) Neuron review documents this slow-wave story in detail. The decline is not uniform across the brain: it is most severe over the medial prefrontal cortex and surrounding frontal regions, where gray matter volume and cortical thickness decline most steeply with age. Older adults generate slow waves that are lower in amplitude, lower in density (fewer slow waves per hour), and slower in frequency than those of young adults. Averaged across studies, frontal slow-wave activity in adults over 65 is approximately 75–80% lower than in young adults in their twenties. But the problem compounds: slow waves need to be precisely coupled with sleep spindles (a coupling described on Day 2 and Day 3) for hippocampal memory traces to be successfully transferred to cortical long-term storage. With age, this slow-wave–spindle synchrony becomes increasingly imprecise, and the result is predictable: the overnight memory consolidation that normally occurs during sleep — converting hippocampal-encoded episodic memories into distributed cortical representations — is progressively impaired.

This is not merely an inconvenience. The downstream link to Alzheimer's disease is one of the most active areas of current sleep research. Slow-wave sleep promotes the glymphatic clearance of amyloid-beta and tau from the brain's interstitial space (a process introduced on Day 1, though its magnitude is debated). At the same time, early Alzheimer's pathology disrupts the medial prefrontal neurons that generate slow waves — creating a vicious cycle: less slow-wave sleep → less amyloid clearance → more amyloid accumulation → further disruption of slow-wave-generating neurons → even less slow-wave sleep. The quantitative evidence from epidemiological studies supports this cycle: an analysis of the MrOS Sleep Study cohort found that each percentage-point reduction in slow-wave sleep per year of aging was associated with a 27% higher risk of developing dementia. Whether improving slow-wave sleep in older adults — through targeted acoustic stimulation, pharmacological approaches, or behavioral interventions — can break this cycle is a question that several ongoing clinical trials are now designed to answer.

### The unified picture: sleep as a biological constant, not a constant experience

Taken together, today's readings tell a coherent story. Sleep is not a mammalian luxury or a human invention — it is a biological necessity so ancient it predates the centralized nervous system. The specific forms it takes — how long, which stages, how they cycle, when in the day — have been shaped by evolution, developmental stage, and aging in ways that reflect the particular demands of each moment in a species' or individual's life. The infant's brain needs active sleep to wire itself; the adolescent's needs slow waves to consolidate the prefrontal circuits still being built; the adult's needs the full NREM–REM architecture to maintain memory and emotional regulation; the aging brain struggles with the very slow waves that once came effortlessly.

This lifespan perspective also offers a more nuanced reading of earlier material. The functions of REM sleep (Day 3) look different in a fetus — where it is constructive — than in an adult, where it is consolidative. The genetic variation in sleep need discussed on Day 5 almost certainly interacts with developmental stage: a natural short-sleeper may be short on whatever function adults can afford to truncate, but we do not know whether the same genetic shortening applies to the infant's active sleep. And the consequences of sleep deprivation catalogued on Day 4 take on additional gravity in light of today's material: adolescents and older adults are deprived of sleep at precisely the life stages when the brain is most dependent on sleep for its ongoing development or its ongoing maintenance.

## Questions to think about

1. Sleep evolved in organisms with no central nervous system — suggesting its core function(s) are cellular rather than circuit-level. Yet the species with the most behaviorally complex and architecturally elaborate sleep (mammals, birds) are also those with the most complex brains. Does this mean sleep acquired new functions as brains became more complex, or does increasing brain complexity simply require *more* of sleep's original function(s)? How would you design an experiment to distinguish between these two hypotheses?

2. The adolescent circadian phase delay is a well-documented biological phenomenon, yet most high schools have historically started between 7 and 8 a.m. — a schedule that forces millions of adolescents into chronic sleep insufficiency during a period of ongoing brain development. The American Academy of Pediatrics, the CDC, and the American Academy of Sleep Medicine all recommend start times of 8:30 a.m. or later for middle and high schools. Given the strength of the evidence, why do you think the adoption of later start times has been slow, and what does this tell us more broadly about how biological evidence translates (or fails to translate) into educational policy?

3. The slow decline of slow-wave sleep with aging appears to be both a consequence of prefrontal cortical atrophy and, through the vicious cycle of reduced amyloid clearance, a potential *driver* of further neurodegeneration. Early intervention — enhancing slow-wave sleep before dementia symptoms appear — is therefore theoretically attractive. If a safe method of increasing slow-wave activity in older adults were developed, at what age would you begin it, in whom, and how would you weigh the benefits against the uncertainties? What ethical frameworks apply to preventive neurological interventions in cognitively healthy older adults?
