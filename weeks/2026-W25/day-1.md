# The science of sleep — Day 1/7: Panorama — architecture, regulation, and the mystery of why we sleep

## Context

Sleep is one of biology's great universals: every animal species carefully studied so far — from the nematode *C. elegans* to elephants — shows some form of it. That universality is the first hint that sleep is not optional. In humans, going without sleep for roughly 11 days is believed to be lethal, and even modest deprivation of 17–19 hours produces cognitive impairment equivalent in measurable tests to a blood alcohol concentration at the legal driving limit in most countries. Yet despite this apparent necessity, scientists cannot agree on a single answer to the deceptively simple question: *why do we sleep?*

This week we tackle the science of sleep from foundations to frontiers. Today's panorama covers the architecture of a night of sleep, the two biological systems that regulate it, and the main scientific theories proposed to explain what sleep is actually *for* — including a landmark 2024 controversy that upended one of the most popular hypotheses of the past decade.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### The architecture of sleep

A night of human sleep is not a uniform state. Polysomnography — the gold-standard recording that simultaneously measures brain electrical activity (EEG), eye movements, and muscle tone — reveals a predictable cycling structure. Sleep divides into two alternating phases: **non-rapid-eye-movement (NREM)** sleep and **rapid-eye-movement (REM)** sleep.

NREM sleep itself has three stages. **N1** is the hypnagogic transition — light, easily disrupted, lasting only minutes. **N2** is established sleep, marked by two signature EEG features: *sleep spindles* (brief bursts of 12–16 Hz oscillations generated by thalamo-cortical loops) and *K-complexes* (large slow deflections thought to suppress external arousal). **N3**, or slow-wave sleep (SWS), is the deepest stage: large-amplitude, synchronous oscillations below 1 Hz sweep across the cortex, and the brain is maximally disconnected from external input. SWS dominates the first half of the night.

**REM sleep** is paradoxical: the EEG looks much like waking — fast, desynchronised, low-amplitude — yet the body is essentially paralysed (a state called REM atonia, achieved via active inhibition of motor neurons). Rapid conjugate eye movements occur, and dreaming is most vivid and narrative during this stage. REM periods lengthen across the night, dominating the second half.

A full NREM–REM cycle lasts roughly 90 minutes, and a typical adult completes 4–6 cycles per night. The proportion of time in each stage changes across the night: SWS is concentrated early, REM late — a fact with important implications for memory consolidation, as we will explore on Day 3.

### The two-process model: what makes you sleepy

The timing and depth of sleep are controlled by two interacting biological systems, formalized as the **two-process model** by Alexander Borbély in 1982.

**Process S** — the *homeostatic* drive — is a measure of accumulated sleep pressure. Throughout every waking hour, the neuromodulator **adenosine** accumulates in the basal forebrain and striatum. Adenosine is a byproduct of neuronal energy metabolism; as neurons consume ATP, adenosine builds up as a signal of metabolic debt. When adenosine concentration in key brain circuits reaches a threshold, it inhibits wake-promoting neurons and activates sleep-promoting ones, generating irresistible sleepiness. (Caffeine works by blocking adenosine receptors, temporarily masking this signal — but the adenosine accumulation continues regardless, which is why the "crash" follows.) Sleep clears adenosine, resetting Process S to a low baseline.

**Process C** — the *circadian* drive — is an approximately 24-hour rhythm generated by the **suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)** of the hypothalamus, a bilateral cluster of roughly 20,000 neurons whose intrinsic period is set by interlocked transcription-translation feedback loops involving the clock genes *CLOCK*, *BMAL1*, *PER1/2/3*, and *CRY1/2*. The SCN synchronises to ambient light via the retinohypothalamic tract and broadcasts timing signals throughout the body via neural projections and the pineal gland's secretion of **melatonin** (which rises after dark and suppresses after light exposure). Process C regulates not only *when* we fall asleep and wake, but how deeply we sleep at a given time: even after a night of total sleep deprivation, if you try to sleep at the peak of your circadian wake-promotion signal (mid-afternoon or early evening), you will be surprisingly alert.

Sleep is the outcome of Process S overcoming Process C. The system is elegantly tuned so that by the time the homeostatic pressure has built up across a waking day, the circadian clock has shifted into a phase that allows sleep — and together they enforce a robust, predictable rest–wake cycle.

### Why do we sleep? Competing theories

Here is where the science gets contentious. No single theory has achieved consensus. Three major frameworks, and one recently contested hypothesis, define the landscape:

**1. The restoration theory** is the oldest and perhaps most intuitive: sleep allows the body and brain to repair the damage accumulated during wakefulness — clearing reactive oxygen species, repairing DNA strand breaks, replenishing energy stores, and restoring hormone levels. The clearest support comes from studies showing that sleep deprivation rapidly elevates markers of oxidative stress and cellular damage in the brain, and that many restoration processes (growth hormone secretion, immune cytokine release, cellular protein synthesis) are gated to occur primarily during sleep or specific sleep stages.

**2. The synaptic homeostasis hypothesis (SHY)**, proposed by Giulio Tononi and Chiara Cirelli and refined over two decades, offers a more specific neural mechanism. During wakefulness, learning and experience continuously *potentiate* synapses throughout the cortex — strengthening connections that encode new information. This potentiation is metabolically expensive, saturates the available dynamic range of neurons, and reduces the signal-to-noise ratio for new encoding. Sleep, according to SHY, is the period during which the brain *downscales* synaptic strength back toward a sustainable baseline, renewing the capacity for learning the next day. Slow-wave oscillations during NREM sleep are the proposed mechanism of downscaling. Direct molecular evidence in *Drosophila* and in rodents — showing that synaptic proteins involved in potentiation are reduced after sleep and increased after sleep deprivation — has given SHY substantial traction, though the precise mechanisms in humans remain under active investigation.

**3. Memory consolidation** overlaps with SHY but is broader: sleep does not merely reset synapses, it *transforms* recently acquired memories into stable long-term representations through active replay. During NREM sleep, sequential "memory reactivation" events — sharp-wave ripples in the hippocampus, coupled with sleep spindles in the thalamo-cortical circuit and slow oscillations in the cortex — appear to drive the transfer of labile hippocampal memories to more stable neocortical storage. REM sleep plays a complementary role in emotional processing and the integration of new with old memories. We will explore this in depth on Day 3.

**4. The glymphatic hypothesis** — proposed by Maiken Nedergaard's group in 2013 — held that sleep is the brain's "cleaning cycle": cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) floods the interstitial space during sleep, driven by expansion of that space by up to 60%, and flushes out neurotoxic waste products including **amyloid-beta** and **tau** — proteins implicated in Alzheimer's disease. The hypothesis gained enormous traction as an explanation of why poor sleep predicts dementia risk. However, a 2024 study in *Nature Neuroscience* by Miao and colleagues at Imperial College London measured the actual movement of tracer molecules in living mouse brains and found the opposite: clearance was *reduced*, not enhanced, during sleep and anesthesia compared with wakefulness. This result triggered a high-profile controversy — the original proponents challenged the methodology, and multiple groups are now working to reconcile the discrepant findings. The mechanism by which sleep may protect against neurodegeneration remains real and important; what role glymphatic flow specifically plays is now genuinely uncertain.

### Sleep is polyfunctional — and that is the point

A recurring finding in the essay by Sharon and Walker (2024) is that sleep's functions are not mutually exclusive. Sleep almost certainly evolved to serve many purposes simultaneously: restoration, synaptic calibration, memory consolidation, immune function, hormonal regulation, and potentially brain clearance. The fact that total sleep deprivation is so rapidly lethal in rodents suggests the aggregated necessity of all these functions — there is no single "essence" of sleep, but rather a bundle of processes that can only occur efficiently when the organism is in a state of reduced interaction with the external world.

This also explains why, despite extensive evolutionary pressure to reduce sleep (it is biologically costly to be unconscious and vulnerable), all animals still sleep. The costs of going without outweigh the considerable costs of the sleep state itself — something must be urgently necessary about this daily shutdown.

## Questions to think about

1. The two-process model elegantly explains the timing of sleep — but it says nothing about *what* sleep actually accomplishes once initiated. If both Process S and Process C are fundamentally about *when* to sleep, not *why*, what additional level of explanation do the functional theories provide?

2. The glymphatic hypothesis and the Miao et al. challenge are both based on mouse experiments with technically demanding methods. What would a definitive test of whether sleep clears neurotoxic waste in humans look like, and what ethical constraints would such a test face?

3. Given that sleep is evolutionarily ancient and polyfunctional, is there any principled reason to expect that a single intervention (better sleep hygiene, sleep-extension drugs, targeted enhancement of slow-wave sleep) would be broadly health-protective? What would evidence of that claim actually look like?
