# Misinformation Dynamics — Day 4/7: Real-World Harms — From Health to Democracy

## Context

The first three days of this week built the analytical framework. Day 1 established that false information travels faster and farther than true information, and introduced the key cognitive concepts — the continued influence effect, the illusory truth effect, the inattention hypothesis. Day 2 showed the mechanisms in the individual brain: fluency, repetition-based familiarity, and the decoupling of sharing from accuracy judgement. Day 3 zoomed out to the network level, where the main findings were that echo chambers are less totalizing than assumed, algorithms are secondary to follow-network effects, and a small number of superspreaders drive a disproportionate share of low-credibility content.

Today we turn to the consequential question: does any of this actually matter? The word "misinformation" carries an implicit assumption of harm, but documenting harm is methodologically harder than it sounds. People who are exposed to more health misinformation might also be less educated, less healthy, or less trusting of institutions for reasons unrelated to misinformation. People who distrust elections might have pre-existing ideological motivations. Establishing that misinformation *caused* an outcome — rather than merely correlating with it — requires careful study design. Today we read the best available evidence on two of the most consequential harm domains: **public health** (specifically vaccination) and **democratic integrity** (election confidence). We then survey the broader landscape of documented harms more briefly in the guided summary.

One theme to carry forward into Day 5 is the dose-response question: the evidence on actual harm is real but often smaller and more context-dependent than the loudest public claims suggest. This calibration matters for designing proportionate responses.

## Today's readings (~20 min total)

**1. Online misinformation is linked to early COVID-19 vaccination hesitancy and refusal** — Pierri, Perry, DeVerna, Yang, Flammini, Menczer & Bryden (*Scientific Reports*, 2022)
<https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-10070-w>
*Estimated reading time: 9 min (abstract + Results + Discussion)*

This study by a team at Indiana University and Indiana University-Purdue University Indianapolis used county-level data across the United States to examine whether exposure to COVID-19 vaccine misinformation on Twitter predicted vaccination rates. The researchers matched Twitter data on misinformation exposure (derived from low-credibility domain sharing) with county-level vaccination rates from the CDC during the early rollout in 2021. The approach controls for key confounders including political leaning, socioeconomic factors, and prior vaccination history. Published in *Scientific Reports* (Nature Publishing Group, open-access, peer-reviewed), this is among the most methodologically careful early studies linking online misinformation to a real-world public-health outcome.

**2. The Effects of Unsubstantiated Claims of Voter Fraud on Confidence in Elections** — Berlinski, Doyle, Guess, Levy, Lyons, Montgomery, Nyhan & Reifler (*Journal of Experimental Political Science*, 2023)
<https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-experimental-political-science/article/effects-of-unsubstantiated-claims-of-voter-fraud-on-confidence-in-elections/9B4CE6DF2F573955071948B9F649DF7A>
*Estimated reading time: 9 min (abstract + Design + Results + Implications)*

This study, by a group of leading political scientists (Guess, Nyhan, and Reifler are among the most-cited researchers in political misinformation), uses a nationwide survey experiment conducted after the 2018 U.S. midterm elections to causally test whether exposure to unsubstantiated claims of voter fraud reduces confidence in electoral integrity. Critically, it is an experiment — participants were randomly assigned to see or not see fraudulent-voter-fraud claims — which allows causal inference that purely observational studies cannot provide. The *Journal of Experimental Political Science* is a Cambridge University Press journal dedicated to the experimental study of political questions. The paper's publication coincides with a period when false election claims had become central to U.S. political discourse.

## Guided summary (~9 min)

**The public-health domain: vaccines and the infodemic**

The World Health Organization coined the term *infodemic* in February 2020 to describe the simultaneous epidemic of misinformation that accompanied COVID-19 — an overabundance of accurate and inaccurate information that made it difficult for people to find reliable guidance. In retrospect, this framing was prescient. By mid-2021, social media platforms hosted hundreds of millions of posts about COVID-19 vaccines, a substantial proportion containing false or misleading claims (microchips, DNA alteration, death, infertility). The question Pierri and colleagues tackled was: did this exposure *translate* into reduced vaccination?

Their answer is carefully hedged but positive. Counties with higher Twitter exposure to low-credibility vaccine content showed lower vaccination rates during the early rollout (January–April 2021), after controlling for political affiliation, rurality, income, and age distribution. The relationship remained significant in regression models and robustness checks. The study is correlational at the county level (not individual-level causal), so residual confounding cannot be fully excluded. But it provides the kind of large-scale, multi-variate evidence that complements the experimental literature: across hundreds of counties, the pattern is consistent.

The public-health stakes are substantial. Epidemiological modelling by the Brown University School of Public Health estimated that roughly 319,000 COVID-19 deaths in the United States between January 2021 and April 2022 could have been prevented if individuals who remained unvaccinated had received vaccination — not all of whom were vaccine-hesitant due to misinformation, but vaccination hesitancy and refusal were the dominant factor. A separate line of evidence concerns measles. The WHO reported more than 42,000 measles cases globally in 2023, up from 941 in 2022 — a 40-fold increase — occurring in a context where the MMR vaccine is highly effective and widely available. Anti-vaccine misinformation (rooted in the now-retracted Wakefield paper falsely linking MMR to autism) has persistently suppressed vaccination rates in communities that saw outbreaks. While drawing a direct causal line from any specific tweet to a measles death is not possible, the structural relationship between persistent false beliefs about vaccine safety and reduced vaccination coverage — and between reduced coverage and outbreaks — is well documented.

**The democratic integrity domain: election misinformation**

Berlinski et al.'s experiment addresses a different harm: the erosion of confidence in democratic institutions. Participants who were shown claims that significant voter fraud had occurred in the 2018 midterms (claims that fact-checkers rated as unsubstantiated) showed measurably lower confidence in the accuracy of election outcomes than control participants who were not exposed to those claims. The effect was statistically robust and replicated across partisan subgroups — both Republicans and Democrats showed reduced confidence after exposure, though the magnitude was larger among Democrats (who were less predisposed to believe the claims and thus showed larger belief updating when exposed).

Crucially, the study found that exposure to election fraud claims reduced *confidence* but did not reduce *support for democracy* as an abstract system. This is an important distinction: people can lose faith in a specific election's integrity while still endorsing democratic principles. The policy implication is nuanced — eroded confidence in specific elections is a serious problem for governance and political participation, but the claim that misinformation is destroying commitment to democracy itself is not well-supported by experimental evidence. The mechanism appears to be more specific: false claims about particular events produce false beliefs about those events, which then compound across repeated cycles of misinformation.

**Broader harm landscape: what else is documented**

Beyond vaccines and elections, the misinformation-harm literature documents effects in several other domains:

*Climate science*: Repeated exposure to contrarian narratives about climate change reduces stated willingness to support climate policy, even among people who have accurate baseline knowledge about the scientific consensus. The mechanism here is not simply persuasion — studies show that exposure to climate misinformation increases *perceived uncertainty* about the science, which suppresses action even without shifting explicit beliefs.

*Financial harm*: Health misinformation has been linked to purchases of unproven treatments, delays in seeking evidence-based care, and in extreme cases, deaths (the FDA has documented fatalities from products promoted as COVID treatments). Financial scams amplified by misinformation caused billions of dollars in consumer losses during 2020–2023.

*Violence*: The most extreme documented harm is the "Pizzagate" case (2016), in which a man fired a gun in a Washington D.C. pizzeria after being convinced by online misinformation that it hosted a child trafficking ring. This is an outlier, but researchers have mapped dozens of cases globally where health, political, or ethnic misinformation contributed to real-world violence.

**The calibration problem**

A responsible reading of the harm evidence requires holding two things simultaneously. On one hand, the harms are real, documented, and sometimes severe — preventable deaths, eroded institutional trust, violence. On the other hand, most studies find that the *average* exposure effect on belief or behavior is modest, and that effects are concentrated among those with pre-existing vulnerabilities (high prior distrust, low media literacy, strong prior belief in the claim). The majority of people who encounter a piece of health misinformation do not change their vaccination behavior; the majority of people who see an election fraud claim do not abandon support for democracy.

This calibration matters for policy design in two ways. First, disproportionate responses — censorship, heavy-handed platform removal — risk chilling legitimate speech in pursuit of modest harm reduction. Second, the finding that effects are concentrated among vulnerable populations suggests that targeted, context-sensitive interventions (media literacy education in communities with low baseline trust, tailored health communication in low-vaccination counties) may be more efficient than blanket approaches. Day 5 will take up the intervention evidence directly; today's reading provides the harm evidence that justifies the investment in interventions at all.

## Questions to think about

1. The Pierri et al. study finds a county-level correlation between misinformation exposure on Twitter and vaccination rates, even after controlling for political affiliation. How confident are you in the causal interpretation? What additional evidence would you want to see before recommending policy based on this finding?

2. Berlinski et al. found that election fraud claims reduced confidence in elections even among Democrats — the group least predisposed to believe them. Does this suggest that corrections of false election claims should be proactively provided before elections, rather than reactively offered after the claims circulate? What are the risks of that approach?

3. The "harm calibration" section argues that most individuals exposed to misinformation do not change their behavior. Does this mean the harms are overstated, or does a small average effect on a very large population still constitute a serious public-health problem — analogous to how a small per-smoker increase in risk is still a major driver of cancer mortality at the population level?
