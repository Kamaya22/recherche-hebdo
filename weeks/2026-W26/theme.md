# Week 2026-W26 — Misinformation Dynamics

## Why it matters

False information is not a new problem, but the digital age has given it unprecedented reach, speed, and resilience. A landmark 2018 *Science* study showed that false news travels six times faster than true news on social media and is 70% more likely to be retweeted — and it is humans, not automated bots, who are primarily responsible. The consequences extend well beyond annoyance: misinformation has suppressed vaccination uptake, distorted election outcomes, and hampered public-health responses during crises such as the COVID-19 pandemic. Understanding *why* false beliefs form, how they propagate, and what actually works against them has become one of the most active intersections of psychology, network science, and communication research.

The field is richer and more surprising than popular framings suggest. Susceptibility to misinformation turns out to be driven less by partisan bias than by simple inattention and reduced analytical thinking — meaning most people can be helped to do better. At the same time, corrections are surprisingly weak: even after people learn that a claim is false, the original misinformation continues to color their reasoning, a phenomenon researchers call the **continued influence effect**. And while debunking is difficult, a newer approach — **prebunking**, or inoculating people before they encounter false content — shows real promise.

This week we examine misinformation dynamics from the ground up: its psychological roots, its social and network amplifiers, its real-world harms, the evidence on interventions, and the emerging challenge posed by AI-generated synthetic content.

## Tentative 7-day outline

- **Day 1 — Panorama**: Core definitions (misinformation / disinformation / malinformation), why the field exists, landmark findings on speed and reach, and a map of the week ahead.
- **Day 2 — Cognitive mechanisms**: Why our brains are vulnerable — the illusory truth effect, fluency, motivated reasoning vs. inattention, and the continued influence effect.
- **Day 3 — Network and social dynamics**: How false content cascades through social networks; the role of echo chambers, influencers, and platform algorithms.
- **Day 4 — Real-world harms**: Documented impacts on public health (vaccines, COVID-19), democratic integrity (elections), and science communication.
- **Day 5 — What works? Interventions**: Accuracy nudges, debunking strategies, prebunking / inoculation, platform-level tools (labels, downranking, community notes).
- **Day 6 — The AI and synthetic-media frontier**: Large language models, deepfakes, and autonomous bots as new vectors; detection arms races.
- **Day 7 — Synthesis**: What is settled, what remains contested, the biggest open questions, and pointers for going deeper.
