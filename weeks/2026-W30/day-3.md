# The neuroscience of pain — Day 3/7: The Brain in Pain — Which Regions Encode What, and What Neuroimaging Has Taught Us

## Context (2–3 paragraphs)

In Day 1, we established the foundational distinction between nociception (the neural encoding of potentially damaging stimuli) and pain (the subjective experience the brain may — or may not — generate in response). We learned that pain is not a passive read-out of tissue damage but an active construction shaped by expectation, emotion, meaning, and context, and we were introduced to the three mechanistic categories — nociceptive, neuropathic, and nociplastic — that shape modern diagnosis. If day 1 was the conceptual map, today's reading gives us the anatomy: which specific brain structures participate in constructing the pain experience, and what each contributes. The signals that arrive at the brain via the spinal cord's dorsal horn — having been processed, gated, and modulated there — project onward through the thalamus to a distributed set of cortical and subcortical regions that together generate the full, multidimensional experience we call pain.

Pain is not processed in a single "pain center." Beginning in the 1990s, neuroimaging techniques (first PET, then fMRI) made it possible to watch the living brain during painful experiences, and they revealed something both clarifying and puzzling: a consistent set of brain regions activates together, a pattern researchers came to call the "pain matrix." This network includes at minimum the primary somatosensory cortex, the secondary somatosensory cortex, the posterior and anterior insulae, the anterior cingulate cortex, the prefrontal cortex, the thalamus, and the amygdala. Each region, it turns out, encodes a different *dimension* of the pain experience — where it is and how intense, how unpleasant and threatening it feels, what it means in context, and whether it should be attended to or suppressed. Pain, in other words, is not one thing in the brain; it is the simultaneous engagement of multiple systems that normally handle sensation, emotion, memory, and action-selection.

Today's three readings span three landmark contributions. The first (Tracey & Mantyh, 2007) is the most comprehensive overview of the "cerebral signature" for pain — still the clearest map of which brain region does what. The second (Rainville et al., 1997) is a short but transformative experiment that used hypnotic suggestion to separately alter the intensity and the unpleasantness of pain, showing that the anterior cingulate cortex tracks how *bad* pain feels, not how *strong* the signal is — a crucial dissociation. The third (Wager et al., 2013) is a NEJM paper that used machine learning on fMRI data to derive an objective "neurologic pain signature," asking whether a brain scan can tell you whether someone is in pain. Together, they take us from anatomy, to function, to the aspiration of measurable pain.

## Today's readings (2–3 items, ~20 min total)

**1. "The Cerebral Signature for Pain Perception and Its Modulation"** — Tracey, I. & Mantyh, P.W., *Neuron*, vol. 55, no. 3 (2007), pp. 377–391 — <https://www.cell.com/neuron/fulltext/S0896-6273(07)00533-8> — ~14 min (read the abstract, introduction, and the sections on primary somatosensory, secondary somatosensory, insular, and anterior cingulate cortices; full text requires institutional access, but the abstract and a detailed summary are freely visible on PubMed: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17678852/>) — This is the canonical review of pain neuroimaging, co-authored by Irene Tracey (then at Oxford, now Vice-Chancellor of Oxford University and one of the world's foremost pain neuroscientists) and Patrick Mantyh (University of Arizona). Published in *Neuron* — one of the two leading journals in neuroscience — it synthesizes a decade of PET and fMRI findings into a coherent map of the "cerebral signature": which cortical and subcortical regions activate during pain, what manipulations (attention, expectation, emotion, opioids) modulate each, and what lesion studies reveal about the necessity of each region. It is long by journal-article standards (15 pages) but structured so that any section stands on its own. For today's purposes, focus on the four cortical regions that encode the sensory and affective dimensions of pain.

**2. "Pain Affect Encoded in Human Anterior Cingulate But Not Somatosensory Cortex"** — Rainville, P., Duncan, G.H., Price, D.D., Carrier, B. & Bushnell, M.C., *Science*, vol. 277, no. 5328 (1997), pp. 968–971 — <https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.277.5328.968> — ~5 min (full paper; access requires institutional subscription but is very short — three pages — and the abstract, which captures the key finding, is freely visible) — This is one of the most elegant experiments in pain neuroscience: Rainville and colleagues used hypnotic suggestion to alter specifically the *unpleasantness* of a noxious heat stimulus, while leaving its perceived *intensity* unchanged, then tracked brain activity with PET scanning. Activity in the anterior cingulate cortex changed with unpleasantness; activity in the primary somatosensory cortex did not. The study provided the first direct evidence in humans that the affective dimension of pain (how much it bothers you) can be neurally separated from the sensory dimension (how strong it is). *Science* is one of the world's most prestigious peer-reviewed journals (American Association for the Advancement of Science), and this paper has been cited over 4,000 times. PMID: 9252330.

**3. "An fMRI-Based Neurologic Signature of Physical Pain"** — Wager, T.D., Atlas, L.Y., Lindquist, M.A., Roy, M., Woo, C.-W. & Kross, E., *N Engl J Med*, vol. 368, no. 15 (2013), pp. 1388–1397 — <https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1204471> (PMC free full text: <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3691100/>) — ~5 min (read the abstract and the "Results" first paragraph; the full paper is 9 pages and rewarding if you have time) — Tor Wager's group at the University of Colorado Boulder trained a machine-learning algorithm on fMRI data from 114 participants to find the combination of brain activity patterns that best predicts whether someone is in pain — and specifically, whether pain can be distinguished from other aversive or unpleasant experiences. The resulting "neurologic pain signature" (NPS) includes positive weights in the dorsal posterior insula, secondary somatosensory cortex, anterior insula, thalamus, and dorsal anterior cingulate cortex — a pattern that the algorithm can distinguish from social rejection or warm (non-noxious) stimulation with high accuracy. Published in the *New England Journal of Medicine*, the world's most-cited medical journal, this paper asks one of pain medicine's most practically important questions: can we ever have an objective measure of pain?

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### The pain matrix: a distributed network, not a single center

The first important lesson from 35 years of pain neuroimaging is negative: there is no dedicated "pain center." No single brain region, when damaged, eliminates pain entirely. No single region, when stimulated, reliably produces pain. What exists instead is a network of regions — the so-called pain matrix — that together generate the pain experience by each encoding a different dimension of it. The regions consistently identified across hundreds of neuroimaging studies include the primary somatosensory cortex (S1), the secondary somatosensory cortex (S2), the posterior insula, the anterior insula, the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), the prefrontal cortex, the thalamus, and the amygdala. Other regions — the cerebellum, the basal ganglia, the periaqueductal grey — also contribute, particularly to motor responses and descending modulation (the latter was previewed in Day 1 and will be explored fully in Day 5). For today, we focus on the cortical core and what each component contributes.

### The sensory dimension: S1, S2, and the posterior insula

The primary somatosensory cortex (S1) is organized as a body map — the famous "homunculus" — and its activation during pain is somatotopic: stimulating the left hand activates a specific region of right S1, stimulating the foot activates a different zone. fMRI studies show that the intensity of S1 activation correlates with the *intensity* of pain: stronger stimuli, more activation. Lesion studies are consistent: damage to S1 does not typically abolish pain but blurs its sensory qualities — patients may struggle to localize or characterize their pain but still suffer. The secondary somatosensory cortex (S2), partially overlapping with the posterior operculum, plays a more integrative role — processing pain quality (burning versus stabbing), and showing stronger activation for sustained or chronic pain. Both S1 and S2 receive input primarily via the thalamus, which relays nociceptive signals from the spinal cord (specifically the spinothalamic tract).

The posterior insula deserves special attention. It lies buried in the lateral sulcus and is the primary cortical recipient of interoceptive signals — information about the body's internal state, including temperature, visceral pain, and the autonomic state of the organs. Activation in the posterior insula closely tracks nociceptive input, and lesions to the posterior insula in some patients produce *pain asymbolia* — the person can detect and localize noxious stimuli but feels no distress. Garcia-Larrea and Peyron, reviewing the neuroimaging and lesion literature in their major 2013 paper in *Pain*, describe the posterior insulo-opercular region as the "nociceptive matrix" proper — the only part of the cortical pain network whose destruction selectively eliminates pain, and the part that receives direct spinothalamic input. Everything else, they argue, is built on top of this core.

### The affective dimension: the anterior cingulate cortex

The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), located on the medial surface of the frontal lobe, is the region most robustly linked to the *unpleasantness* of pain — what it means to be in pain, as opposed to simply detecting a noxious stimulus. The evidence for this came most dramatically from the Rainville et al. 1997 experiment: using hypnotic suggestions that specifically altered participants' subjective rating of pain unpleasantness (without changing their intensity ratings), the team showed that ACC activity tracked unpleasantness while S1 activity did not change. This was a causal dissociation: the manipulation specifically changed one dimension of pain and its corresponding brain region, leaving the other intact.

Clinical lesion data provide converging evidence. Patients who undergo cingulotomy (surgical lesioning of the ACC, used historically for intractable pain), or who suffer accidental damage to this region, sometimes show a peculiar syndrome: they report that pain is still present and strong, but it no longer distresses them. They know they are in pain, but they don't seem to mind it much. This "pain without suffering" syndrome precisely maps onto the ACC's role in encoding the *motivational salience* of pain — its demand for behavioral response. The ACC is densely connected to limbic structures (amygdala, hippocampus) and to motor planning regions, which explains why it links pain to the emotional drive to escape or protect.

### The anterior insula: where sensation meets feeling

The anterior insula is the region perhaps most deeply implicated in translating nociceptive input into conscious suffering. Anatomically, the insula is a "hidden" cortical structure lying beneath the temporal and parietal lobes, and it shows a functional gradient from posterior (more sensory) to anterior (more affective and cognitive). Posterior insula activation tracks sensory aspects of pain; anterior insula activation tracks the intensity of unpleasantness and integrates it with other bodily feelings (hunger, nausea, breathlessness) to produce what some researchers call "interoceptive awareness" — the felt sense of the body's state. The anterior insula shows particularly strong activation in studies of empathy for others' pain: when you watch someone being hurt, your anterior insula activates. This suggests it is computing not a raw nociceptive signal but something closer to "this matters, to me or to someone I care about."

### The cognitive dimension: prefrontal cortex and attention

The prefrontal cortex (PFC) — particularly the dorsolateral PFC and the orbitofrontal cortex — contributes what we might call the *evaluative* dimension of pain: what does this pain mean? Is it catastrophic or manageable? Is it expected or surprising? Should I attend to it or try to ignore it? This is why attention and distraction have such powerful effects on pain experience: engaging the prefrontal cortex in a demanding cognitive task diverts resources away from pain processing. The medial PFC is particularly important in the context of chronic pain, where it appears to be responsible for maintaining a kind of background "pain narrative" — the ongoing appraisal of one's pain situation that shapes functional disability. The Wager et al. (2013) machine-learning paper found that prefrontal regions contributed relatively *negative* weights to the neurologic pain signature — that is, higher prefrontal activity was associated with *less* pain — consistent with the PFC's role in top-down regulation of pain signals.

### Three tiers of the pain matrix

Garcia-Larrea and Peyron's 2013 review in the journal *Pain* offers a useful three-tier model that organizes these findings. The **first tier** — the "nociceptive matrix" proper — is the posterior insula and S2/operculum: these receive direct spinothalamic input, show somatotopic organization, and are the only regions whose lesioning produces genuine analgesia. The **second tier** includes the anterior insula, ACC, and prefrontal cortex: these encode the affective and cognitive dimensions of pain, are not nociception-specific (they respond to other salient or aversive events), and their lesioning does not selectively abolish pain but changes its emotional character or the person's ability to regulate it. The **third tier** involves orbitofrontal and limbic networks that bring past experience, beliefs, and emotional context to bear on the current pain state — explaining why someone's history of trauma or their catastrophic beliefs about pain so powerfully amplify their suffering.

### Can we measure pain from brain activity?

Wager et al.'s 2013 NEJM paper confronted the most practically important question in this field: given that pain is defined as subjective, can an fMRI scan tell us whether someone is in pain? Using four studies and 114 participants, the team used machine learning to find the combination of brain regions whose activation pattern best predicted pain intensity at the level of individual subjects. The resulting neurologic pain signature (NPS) was impressive: it predicted pain intensity across individuals with roughly 90% classification accuracy, and it was sensitive to opioid analgesia (remifentanil reduced NPS activity). Crucially, the signature was largely *specific* to physical pain: it did not activate equally to social rejection — even though social rejection activates the ACC and anterior insula, the full signature pattern differs. This is important because it suggests that the ACC and anterior insula activations observed in "pain empathy" and "social pain" studies reflect a broader salience/distress response, while the *combination* of activations in the NPS — including strong posterior insular and S2 signals — is more specific to nociception. The NPS cannot yet be used clinically (it requires group-level calibration and doesn't account for chronic pain states), but it represents the most rigorous attempt to date to make pain objectively measurable — a goal with profound implications for medicine and law.

### The salience challenge: is the pain matrix pain-specific?

A critical challenge to the pain matrix concept must be mentioned, because it bears directly on what the matrix *is*. Legrain, Iannetti, and colleagues (2011, *Progress in Neurobiology*) observed that many of the regions in the so-called pain matrix also activate robustly to other sudden, salient stimuli — loud noises, unexpected visual flashes — even non-painful ones. They argued that the pain matrix is in large part a *salience detection system*: a network that responds to any stimulus demanding priority processing, not to nociception per se. This is not wrong — but it is also not the full picture. The Garcia-Larrea tier-one nociceptive matrix (posterior insula/S2) is more nociception-specific; the outer tiers respond to many aversive events. The Wager NPS finding — that the full pattern does distinguish pain from social rejection — suggests the network is not simply a general alarm. The most accurate picture is probably: the outer regions of the pain matrix (ACC, anterior insula) encode threat and aversive salience broadly; the inner regions (posterior insula, S2, S1) provide the nociception-specific signal; and the full-pattern combination is what makes it pain.

## Questions to think about

1. The Rainville experiment showed that hypnosis can selectively change pain unpleasantness (ACC activity) without changing intensity (S1 activity). What does this tell us about the nature of pain as an experience? If unpleasantness is neurally separable from intensity, does it make sense to reduce "pain" to a single rating scale — the 0–10 numerical scale used in most clinical settings?

2. The Wager neurologic pain signature achieved ~90% accuracy in distinguishing pain from non-pain states in controlled laboratory conditions. But chronic pain involves persistent sensitization and structural brain changes. What obstacles would stand in the way of using such a signature clinically — for example, in court (to determine whether someone is genuinely in pain) or in drug development (as an endpoint)?

3. Garcia-Larrea and Peyron propose that only the first-tier posterior insulo-opercular network is truly nociception-specific, while the ACC and anterior insula respond to all aversive salience. If that is correct, what are the implications for interventions targeting the ACC — such as cingulotomy for intractable pain, or mindfulness-based therapies that appear to reduce pain affect? Are they reducing *pain* specifically, or reducing the organism's response to threat in general, and does the distinction matter clinically?
