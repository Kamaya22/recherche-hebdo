# The neuroscience of pain — Day 5/7: How the Brain Constructs and Controls Pain — Descending Pathways, Predictive Coding, and the Neuroscience of Placebo

## Context (2–3 paragraphs)

The previous four days have built a progressively richer picture of pain. Day 1 introduced the foundational distinction between nociception and pain, and established that pain is an active construction of the brain. Day 2 traced the signal from the skin to the spinal cord — the molecular sensors, the Aδ and C fiber pathways, and the dorsal horn's gate-control role. Day 3 mapped the cortical and subcortical structures that generate the pain experience: the somatosensory cortices encoding where it is and how intense, the anterior cingulate encoding how bad it feels, the anterior insula integrating body state with emotional valence, and the prefrontal cortex appraising meaning. Day 4 showed what happens when this system becomes chronically amplified — central sensitization, glial amplification, and structural brain changes that explain why "the damage is healed but the pain persists." Today we return to a claim that appeared in Day 1 and has been invoked throughout: that the brain does not passively receive pain signals but actively constructs, regulates, and can suppress them.

Today's reading explains the *mechanism* by which top-down brain activity gates nociceptive signals. At the center of that mechanism is the periaqueductal grey (PAG) — a midbrain structure that serves as the principal hub of the brain's descending pain control system. The PAG receives information from the prefrontal cortex, the anterior cingulate, the amygdala, and the hypothalamus — all the structures involved in emotion, expectation, and context — and projects to the rostral ventromedial medulla (RVM), which in turn sends fibers down into the spinal cord dorsal horn, where they can either suppress or amplify nociceptive transmission. This circuit is what stress-induced analgesia, hypnotic analgesia, opioid drugs, and placebo analgesia all have in common: they all ultimately recruit the PAG-RVM pathway.

The predictive coding framework, explored in today's second reading, provides the theoretical architecture that ties these observations together. The brain, in this view, is not a passive recipient of sensory signals but a prediction machine that continuously generates expectations about incoming input and compares them to what actually arrives. Pain, on this account, is the brain's best guess about the state of the body — an inference shaped by prior experience, current context, and attentional priority, not a direct read-out of tissue injury. Placebo and nocebo effects are not anomalies that complicate this picture; they are among its clearest demonstrations. When a sham treatment reliably reduces pain by 30–40% — and does so through the same neural and neurochemical machinery as genuine opioid analgesia — it is providing direct evidence that expectation is itself a driver of the pain construction process.

The authors explain how "top-down" modulatory circuits — activated by emotional state, expectation, attention, past experience, and pharmacological agents — profoundly alter the sensory experience of pain, and how the same descending system that inhibits pain in some contexts can shift to *facilitate* it in others, contributing to the maintenance of chronic pain states. The *Journal of Clinical Investigation* is a peer-reviewed journal of the American Society for Clinical Investigation, published since 1924 and ranked among the most authoritative in biomedical research.

**2. "Placebo Analgesia: A Predictive Coding Perspective"** — Büchel, C., Geuter, S., Sprenger, C. & Eippert, F., *Neuron*, vol. 81, no. 6 (March 2014), pp. 1223–1239 — <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24656247/> — ~7 min (listed as a free article on PubMed; read the abstract and the sections "Predictive Coding and Pain" and "The PAG and RVM as Implementations of Expectation," approximately 4 pages) — This is the most important single paper linking predictive coding theory to pain and placebo analgesia. ## Guided summary (~8 min)

### The PAG: the brain's master switch for pain

The periaqueductal grey (PAG) is a structure about the size of a peanut, wrapped around the cerebral aqueduct in the midbrain. Despite its modest size, it is one of the most important integrative hubs in the nervous system — the structure where the brain's higher computations about threat, expectation, and context are translated into direct modulation of pain transmission. It was first identified as pain-relevant in the 1960s, when electrical stimulation of the PAG in animals produced profound analgesia; rats given PAG stimulation tolerated surgical procedures without any reaction to pain. Subsequent work showed that the PAG is densely packed with μ-opioid receptors, and that morphine's analgesic effect operates in large part through these receptors in the PAG.

Ossipov, Dussor and Porreca's review describes the PAG's anatomical position as an integrator between cortical and limbic inputs — receiving projections from the medial prefrontal cortex, the anterior cingulate cortex, the amygdala, the hypothalamus, and the insula — and subcortical output: the PAG does not itself project directly to the spinal cord, but projects to the rostral ventromedial medulla (RVM), which does. The RVM contains three functionally distinct populations of neurons: **off-cells**, which are activated by opioids and inhibit pain transmission in the spinal cord; **on-cells**, which are activated by noxious stimuli and facilitate pain transmission; and **neutral cells**, which do not respond reliably to either. In normal acute pain conditions, off-cells dominate. In animal models of chronic pain (and likely in humans), on-cells become tonically active, and the balance shifts toward descending *facilitation* — the brain is now actively *amplifying* incoming pain signals rather than suppressing them. This provides a neural mechanism for the clinical observation that chronic pain can persist and worsen independently of peripheral tissue state: the descending system itself has switched polarity.

The neurotransmitters of the descending pathway matter clinically. Serotonin and noradrenaline are the main messengers released by RVM fibers onto spinal dorsal horn neurons. This explains why drugs that enhance serotonin and noradrenaline (certain antidepressants — the SNRIs duloxetine and venlafaxine, and the older tricyclic antidepressants) have genuine analgesic effects in neuropathic and chronic pain, independent of their antidepressant action: they boost the descending inhibitory signals that the PAG-RVM system sends to the spinal cord. This is not a placebo effect of being less depressed and therefore reporting less pain — it is a direct pharmacological enhancement of the brain's own pain-suppression machinery.

### Predictive coding: the brain as a pain-prediction machine

The predictive coding framework, developed in computational neuroscience by Karl Friston and others as a general theory of perception, has been applied to pain with compelling results. The core idea is simple but radical: the brain does not passively process incoming sensory information. Instead, it continuously generates *predictions* about what sensory input will arrive, sends those predictions downward through the neural hierarchy, and then computes the *prediction error* — the difference between what was expected and what actually arrived. Only prediction errors need to be transmitted upward, because correctly predicted inputs convey no new information. The result is an efficient system that spends most of its energy on the unexpected, not on confirming what it already knows.

Büchel and colleagues' 2014 *Neuron* paper extends this framework to pain and shows how it explains both normal pain processing and placebo effects. In the predictive coding model of pain, the descending PAG-RVM pathway carries the brain's *predictions* about upcoming nociception down to the spinal cord. These predictions suppress or amplify spinal nociceptive processing before signals even reach the brain. When the brain predicts high pain (as in the nocebo effect — the patient who has been told the procedure will be agonizing), it sends a "prepare for danger" signal downward, which *disinhibits* spinal pain transmission and amplifies whatever signals arrive. When the brain predicts low pain (as in the placebo effect — the patient who trusts the treatment will work), it sends a "safe" signal downward that recruits descending inhibition, suppressing nociceptive transmission in the dorsal horn.

The crucial theoretical refinement Büchel et al. add is the concept of *precision*: it matters not only what the brain predicts, but how *confident* it is in its prediction. A high-precision prediction (the patient is very confident the painkiller will work) suppresses spinal pain transmission more strongly than a low-precision prediction (the patient is uncertain). In their neuroimaging experiments, expectation precision signals were specifically correlated with PAG and RVM activity — the exact structures that implement descending modulation. This closes the loop: the predictive coding model makes the PAG not merely a relay station but the key interface between the brain's higher-level predictions and their implementation in the spinal cord.

### Placebo analgesia: a biology lesson, not a curiosity

Placebo analgesia is often treated in medical culture as an inconvenient confound — something to be controlled away in randomized trials. The neuroscience of the past forty years recasts it as one of the most important phenomena in pain medicine: direct, reproducible evidence that expectation is a primary driver of pain construction.

The pharmacological evidence came first. In 1978, Levine, Gordon and Fields published a study in *The Lancet* showing that placebo analgesia in dental surgery patients could be partially reversed by naloxone — an opioid antagonist that has no analgesic effect of its own. If placebo worked by some non-biological "belief" mechanism, naloxone should have been irrelevant. Instead, blocking opioid receptors reduced placebo analgesia, demonstrating that placebo recruited the brain's own opioid system — the same system that morphine acts on. The 2002 imaging study by Petrovic, Kalso, Petersson and Ingvar in *Science* (doi: 10.1126/science.1067176) extended this: using PET imaging, they showed that both opioid analgesia and placebo analgesia activated the same brain regions — the rostral anterior cingulate cortex (rACC) and the brainstem (in the region of the PAG-RVM) — and that activity in the rACC and brainstem *covaried* specifically under conditions of analgesia, whether drug-induced or placebo-induced.

Eippert et al.'s 2009 *Science* study went a step further by imaging not just the brain but the spinal cord. This is technically formidable — the spinal cord is a tiny, mobile structure surrounded by noise-generating cerebrospinal fluid, and most fMRI studies of pain have been unable to image it reliably. The Hamburg group managed it, and their finding was unambiguous: pain-related BOLD activity in the cervical spinal cord dorsal horn was significantly reduced under placebo compared to control conditions, matched to participants' behavioral reports of reduced pain. The top-down placebo signal is not merely an cortical interpretation of pain signals that have already arrived — it reaches the spinal cord and attenuates nociceptive processing at its very first synapse in the central nervous system. The PAG-RVM descending inhibitory pathway is the route by which this happens.

### Nocebo: the dark mirror

If predictive coding explains how positive expectations reduce pain, the same logic predicts that negative expectations should *increase* pain — and this is exactly what the nocebo literature shows. When participants are told that a treatment will increase their pain, their pain increases, even when the "treatment" is pharmacologically inert. When patients are told "this injection will sting" in a harsh, warning tone rather than a neutral tone, they report more pain from the same injection. When doctors communicate a diagnosis in a way that emphasizes danger, permanence, and suffering, their patients' pain worsens — not because of any new tissue damage, but because the prediction now primes the PAG-RVM system for facilitation rather than inhibition.

The neurochemical substrate of nocebo is partly distinct from placebo. While placebo analgesia depends heavily on endogenous μ-opioid release (blocked by naloxone), nocebo hyperalgesia appears to involve cholecystokinin (CCK), a peptide that is often described as an "anti-opioid": it antagonizes the effects of endogenous opioids and is released by anxiety. This provides a neurochemical account of why anxiety specifically amplifies pain: anxious patients release more CCK, which blocks endogenous opioid tone, which reduces descending inhibition, which amplifies spinal nociceptive transmission. The clinical implication is uncomfortable but important: the way a clinician communicates about pain, the visual environment in which a patient receives care, and the nature of diagnostic labeling are not soft "bedside manner" concerns. They are direct inputs to a neurochemical system that determines whether descending pain modulation is switched to inhibit or facilitate.

### Top-down control is not "all in the mind": a clarification

A persistent cultural confusion surrounds the concepts introduced today. Acknowledging that expectations, beliefs, and emotions modulate pain through descending circuits is sometimes taken to mean that "the pain is not real" or that patients are generating their pain deliberately. This is precisely wrong. The descending pathways activated by expectation operate through the same PAG-RVM circuitry that endogenous and exogenous opioids act through. The spinal suppression demonstrated by Eippert et al. is a measurable change in BOLD signal in spinal cord tissue — it is as biological as anything measured with a scanner. The neurochemical changes that drive nocebo hyperalgesia (CCK release, opioid antagonism) are detectable in cerebrospinal fluid. Predictive coding does not mean that pain is "only a thought" — it means that pain is a computation in which thoughts (more precisely: probabilistic predictions encoded in neural activity) are genuine biological inputs to the computation. The mind-body dualism implied by "it's all in your head" dissolves when you recognize that the head is where all pain processing happens, and that what happens in the head is implemented in neurons, synapses, receptors, and neurotransmitters. By Day 6, we will see what this neurobiological understanding demands of treatment.

## Questions to think about

1. The PAG-RVM system can switch between descending inhibition and descending facilitation. If, in chronic pain states, the balance has shifted to facilitation — so that the brain is actively amplifying rather than suppressing pain — what would an ideal treatment look like? Would it target the switch itself (trying to restore inhibitory tone), or would it work upstream at the predictions and expectations that drive the switch?

2. Placebo analgesia works partly through endogenous opioid release, blocked by naloxone. This means that giving a patient naloxone alongside a placebo would reduce its effect. Does this raise ethical concerns about open-label placebo trials (where patients are told they are receiving a placebo but show benefit anyway)? What does the fact that open-label placebos work tell us about the predictive coding model?

3. Nocebo effects mean that negative communication from a clinician reliably increases a patient's pain. Should informed consent for painful procedures include information about the nocebo effect — in other words, should doctors warn patients that *warning* them too graphically about pain might make the pain worse? How would a healthcare system practically manage this tension between transparency and harm reduction?
