# The neuroscience of pain — Day 2/7: From Skin to Spinal Cord

## Context

Yesterday's reading established the conceptual ground: pain is not a simple alarm triggered by tissue damage, but an active construction of the brain that weights sensory signals against expectation, emotion, memory, and context. The same injury can produce wildly different pain depending on the state of the nervous system processing it. But a construction still needs raw material. Today we go to the source: the peripheral nervous system, where specialized neurons called nociceptors sit at the boundary between body and world, converting potentially dangerous stimuli into the electrical language of the nervous system.

Understanding how nociception begins at the periphery matters for two reasons. First, it reveals why some drugs work and others fail — an anti-inflammatory that quiets peripheral nociceptors is genuinely helpful for acute nociceptive pain, but largely irrelevant when the problem has shifted to a sensitized central nervous system. Second, it anchors the more surprising finding we will reach in Day 5: that the brain can override, amplify, or suppress this peripheral signal depending on context. You cannot fully appreciate top-down control until you understand what it is controlling.

Today's reading follows the nociceptive signal from its origins in the skin through the two-fiber highway to the spinal cord's dorsal horn — and then to the pivotal 1965 theory that redrew the map of pain. The gate control theory of Melzack and Wall was not anatomically precise in every detail, but it was conceptually revolutionary: it forced neuroscience to recognize the spinal cord not as a passive relay station but as an active, modulatable processing gate — and it laid the intellectual foundation for everything discussed in the rest of this week.

The mechanisms they proposed were not anatomically accurate in every detail, but the conceptual leap was enormous: the spinal cord is a processing gate, not a telephone line. Every treatment discussed in Day 6 — transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation, spinal cord stimulation, psychological interventions — traces at least part of its intellectual ancestry to this paper.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### What are nociceptors?

Nociceptors are the specialized sensory neurons whose job is to detect stimuli that are actually or potentially tissue-damaging. They are not "pain fibers" in any simple sense — they report the inputs to the spinal cord and brain, but whether the brain generates pain from those inputs depends, as Day 1 showed, on many other factors. Their cell bodies reside in the **dorsal root ganglia** (DRG) — small clusters of neurons along the spinal column, one pair per spinal segment — and in the **trigeminal ganglion** for sensory signals from the face and head. Each nociceptor is a pseudounipolar neuron: a single axon that splits, sending one branch into peripheral tissues (skin, muscle, joint, viscera) and the other into the spinal cord's dorsal horn. This architecture means a single cell spans the entire distance from its sensory territory to the central nervous system.

Not all nociceptors are identical. Some respond only to heat, others only to mechanical pressure, others only to chemical irritants — but many are **polymodal**, responding to all three categories. They also differ in the neurotransmitters they release and in the surface molecules they express. The molecular heterogeneity of nociceptors is not biological noise: it reflects evolutionary specialization, and it creates exploitable therapeutic targets.

### The molecular sensors: TRP channels

The key molecules that allow nociceptors to convert physical energy and chemical signals into electrical activity are ion channels. When a channel opens, charged ions flow across the cell membrane, depolarizing it; if enough depolarization accumulates, an action potential fires and travels to the spinal cord. Two major channel families dominate the nociceptor sensory repertoire.

The **transient receptor potential (TRP) channels** are the primary sensors of temperature and chemical irritants. In 1997, David Julius's lab used capsaicin — the compound that makes chilli peppers burn — as a molecular probe to identify the ion channel that responds to both capsaicin and heat: **TRPV1** (transient receptor potential vanilloid 1). TRPV1 opens at temperatures above approximately 43°C (the threshold at which heat becomes noxious), at low pH (the chemical signal of inflammation and tissue acidosis), and in response to endogenous lipid molecules called endocannabinoids. Its discovery earned Julius the 2021 Nobel Prize and explained, at a molecular level, why hot pepper extract on injured skin causes such exquisite pain: the channel is already sensitized by inflammation, so temperatures that would normally be merely warm now activate it. **TRPA1** (transient receptor potential ankyrin 1) is the sensor for chemical irritants like allyl isothiocyanate (the pungent molecule in wasabi and mustard), acrolein (in cigarette smoke), and some electrophilic compounds produced during tissue damage and oxidative stress. It is expressed mainly in C fibers and typically co-expressed with TRPV1. **TRPM8** responds to cool temperatures (below about 25°C) and to menthol — which is why menthol feels cold even at skin temperature.

The second Nobel laureate of 2021, Ardem Patapoutian, identified a different family of channels: the **Piezo channels**. Piezo2 is the primary mechanosensitive channel for gentle touch and proprioception; Piezo1 and Piezo2 are also involved in detecting more intense mechanical stimuli at nociceptors. Together, the TRP and Piezo families account for much of the nociceptor's ability to transduce the physical world into electrical signals.

### The molecular sensors: voltage-gated sodium channels

TRP and Piezo channels initiate the electrical signal, but it is **voltage-gated sodium (Nav) channels** that generate and propagate action potentials along the axon to the spinal cord. Three Nav subtypes are particularly important in nociceptors, and their clinical significance is hard to overstate.

**Nav1.7** (encoded by the gene *SCN9A*) sits at a pivotal threshold: it amplifies small depolarizing inputs, lowering the barrier for a full action potential to fire. Loss-of-function mutations in *SCN9A* produce **congenital insensitivity to pain** — people with this condition feel virtually no pain from birth, accumulate injuries unnoticed, and have a shortened life expectancy as a result. Gain-of-function mutations cause **primary erythromelalgia**, a devastating condition of extreme burning pain in the hands and feet triggered by mild warmth. These human experiments of nature establish Nav1.7 as a critical determinant of whether nociceptive input translates into a pain experience — and make it a major drug target.

**Nav1.8** is resistant to the puffer-fish toxin tetrodotoxin (TTX) and is expressed almost exclusively in peripheral sensory neurons rather than the central nervous system. This selectivity is therapeutically attractive: a Nav1.8 blocker could in principle dampen peripheral nociceptor firing without central side effects. Nav1.8 is particularly important in inflammatory pain: when inflammation lowers the resting membrane potential of nociceptors, Nav1.8 maintains the ability to fire action potentials under conditions that would silence the faster TTX-sensitive channels. **Nav1.9**, also TTX-resistant, has unusually slow inactivation kinetics, generating a persistent depolarizing current that lowers firing threshold. Loss-of-function variants in *SCN11A* produce yet another congenital pain insensitivity syndrome.

### The two-fiber highway: Aδ and C

Nociceptors project to the spinal cord via two fundamentally different types of axons, each optimized for a different role in the pain system.

**Aδ fibers** are lightly myelinated. The myelin sheath — a fatty insulating layer wrapped around the axon by Schwann cells — allows action potentials to jump rapidly between gaps in the insulation (nodes of Ranvier), achieving conduction velocities of **5 to 30 metres per second**. Because of this speed, Aδ-mediated pain arrives quickly: the sharp, well-localized, piercing sensation you feel the instant you stub a toe or cut a finger — what clinicians call "**first pain**" — travels on Aδ fibers. Aδ nociceptors respond predominantly to intense mechanical stimuli and to heat above about 53°C (high-threshold mechanoreceptors) or above 43°C (heat-sensitive subtypes).

**C fibers** are unmyelinated and conduct at less than **2 metres per second**. The dull, burning, aching, poorly localized sensation that follows first pain — "**second pain**" — arrives on C fibers, delayed by their slow conduction. C fibers outnumber Aδ fibers in most tissues and are subdivided into two major populations with different molecular identities and different functions:

- **Peptidergic C fibers** express neuropeptides including **substance P** and **calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP)**, and carry receptors for nerve growth factor (TrkA). When activated, they not only signal to the spinal cord but also release peptides into peripheral tissues, triggering local vasodilation and plasma extravasation — neurogenic inflammation. CGRP, notably, has become the target of several approved migraine drugs.
- **Non-peptidergic C fibers** express the receptor tyrosine kinase c-Ret and bind the lectin IB4; they carry P2X3 receptors activated by ATP released from damaged cells.

This molecular distinction is not merely taxonomic: the two C-fiber populations have different axonal targets in the spinal cord and, increasingly, appear to serve different functional roles in acute and chronic pain.

### The dorsal horn: the first processing gate

The dorsal horn of the spinal cord is where peripheral nociceptive signals first encounter the central nervous system — and where they are shaped, filtered, amplified, and inhibited before any signal reaches the brain. Its organization is not chaotic: it is arranged in precisely defined layers called **Rexed laminae**, numbered I through VI from dorsal to ventral.

**Lamina I** (the marginal zone) receives input primarily from Aδ and C fibers and contains two major neuronal types: nociceptive-specific neurons (respond only to noxious stimuli) and wide-dynamic-range neurons (respond to a broad range of stimulus intensities). Crucially, lamina I contains many of the projection neurons that send axons up the spinothalamic tract to the thalamus and then the cortex — it is the output layer for conscious pain.

**Lamina II** (the substantia gelatinosa) receives predominantly C-fiber input (with peptidergic fibers targeting outer lamina II and non-peptidergic fibers targeting inner lamina II) and consists almost entirely of interneurons — local circuit neurons, both excitatory and inhibitory, that modulate the signal before it reaches projection neurons. This dense interneuronal network is the neuroanatomical substrate of Melzack and Wall's "gate."

**Laminae III–V** receive input from larger-caliber myelinated fibers, including non-nociceptive Aβ fibers (which normally encode touch). The wide-dynamic-range neurons in **lamina V** receive convergent input from nociceptors *and* from low-threshold mechanoreceptors — a convergence that plays a key role in both the gate control mechanism and in referred pain (why a cardiac infarction produces arm pain: visceral and somatic inputs converge onto the same spinal neurons).

### The gate control theory: a conceptual revolution

Before 1965, the dominant theory of pain was the specificity theory, championed by the 19th-century anatomist Maximilian von Frey: pain has its own dedicated receptors, dedicated fibers, and a dedicated "pain center" in the brain. Pain intensity, on this account, was simply proportional to the amount of tissue damage and the quantity of signals traveling up the dedicated pain pathway. The implications were mechanistic and therapeutic: to treat pain, find the pathway and cut it or block it.

Melzack and Wall dismantled this picture. Their 1965 *Science* paper proposed that the **balance of activity in large-diameter (Aβ) fibers versus small-diameter (Aδ and C) fibers determines how much nociceptive information reaches the brain**. The mechanism was the substantia gelatinosa in lamina II:

- Large-diameter Aβ fibers (carrying touch and proprioception) activate inhibitory interneurons in the substantia gelatinosa, which suppress the output of transmission ("T") cells — they **close the gate**.
- Small-diameter Aδ and C nociceptive fibers inhibit the same inhibitory interneurons, thereby releasing the T cells from inhibition — they **open the gate**.
- Crucially, descending signals from the brain can also bias the gate in either direction.

The theory immediately explained several clinical puzzles. Rubbing a painful area provides relief: mechanical stimulation activates Aβ fibers, closing the gate. Anxiety and fear worsen pain: negative emotional states activate descending facilitatory pathways, opening the gate. And it predicted, correctly, that stimulating large-diameter fibers electrically should reduce pain — a prediction exploited in **transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS)**, which delivers mild electrical current through skin electrodes, and in **spinal cord stimulation (SCS)**, which uses implanted electrodes in the dorsal epidural space. Both technologies owe their existence, conceptually if not mechanically, to this 1965 paper.

The specific cellular mechanism Melzack and Wall proposed turned out to be incorrect in its details — the exact populations of cells they identified in the substantia gelatinosa don't quite behave as described. But the *conceptual architecture* was right: the spinal cord is not a telephone line but a processing gate, whose state is determined by the interplay of peripheral inputs, local interneurons, and descending brain signals. This framework remains the organizing principle of spinal cord pain research to this day.

## Questions to think about

1. Nav1.7 loss-of-function mutations cause congenital insensitivity to pain — people feel nothing from injury. This sounds beneficial, but the condition is dangerous: those affected accumulate unnoticed injuries, lose joints and teeth, and have shortened life expectancy. What does this suggest about the evolutionary function of pain, and about the risks of trying to abolish it pharmacologically?

2. The gate control theory's specific neuroanatomical mechanism was wrong, yet the paper was transformative. What does this tell us about the value of theoretical frameworks in science, even imperfect ones — and how does it compare to other cases where a "wrong" model still advanced a field?

3. The two-fiber system — fast Aδ carrying sharp localized pain, slow C carrying diffuse aching pain — divides nociceptive information into two temporal streams arriving with a detectable delay between them. What adaptive advantage might this two-stage alert system confer, and can you think of behavioral situations where distinguishing first pain from second pain matters?
