# CRISPR and genome editing therapies — Day 2/7: The molecular machinery of Cas9

## Context

Day 1 introduced CRISPR-Cas9 from the outside: the discovery story, the historical context, and the practical reasons why it displaced its predecessors. Today we go inside the machine. Understanding CRISPR at the molecular level matters not as an exercise in biochemical aesthetics but because the therapy-relevant questions — why does an edit sometimes miss, why does it sometimes cause unintended changes, why are some targets easier to edit than others, and why do base editors and prime editors (Day 4) represent a genuine advance — all have their answers in the mechanics of what Cas9 actually does once it has been delivered to a cell.

Two interlocking molecular stories occupy today's reading. The first is the structure and mechanism of the Cas9–sgRNA–DNA ternary complex: how a protein and a short RNA molecule together scan billions of base pairs, identify a specific 20-base-pair address, unwind the double helix, and sever both strands with surgical precision. The second story is what happens *after* the cut: the cell's own DNA repair machinery takes over, and the outcome of editing — disruption, correction, or replacement — is determined by which of the cell's two main repair pathways activates. These repair pathways are not under the experimenter's direct control, and the therapeutic challenge of directing cells toward the desired repair outcome is one of the central engineering problems of the field.

**2. Crystal Structure of Cas9 in Complex with Guide RNA and Target DNA** — Nishimasu, Ran, Hsu, Konermann, Shehata, Dohmae, Ishitani, Zhang, Nureki (*Cell*, 2014)
<https://www.cell.com/fulltext/S0092-8674(14)00156-1>
*Estimated reading time: 10 min — abstract, Figure 1 (overall structure), and the "PAM Recognition" and "Implications for Specificity and Off-Target Cleavage" sections*

If Jinek et al. 2012 showed what Cas9 can do, Nishimasu et al. 2014 showed what it looks like while doing it. Published in *Cell* in March 2014, this paper presents the first crystal structure of the SpCas9–sgRNA–DNA ternary complex — the assembly in its active, DNA-bound form — resolved to 2.5 Å. The structure revealed that Cas9 is a bilobed protein: a recognition lobe (REC) that cradles the RNA–DNA heteroduplex, and a nuclease lobe (NUC) that houses the two catalytic domains, HNH and RuvC. The structure explains, in atomic detail, why the PAM motif must be read *before* the guide RNA can interrogate the target — Cas9 contacts the PAM via the nuclease lobe, and this contact triggers a conformational change that enables R-loop formation. It also explains why mismatches near the PAM ("seed region") are far more disruptive to cleavage than mismatches at the 5′ end of the guide — the seed region is the first part of the protospacer to be read. These structural insights directly inform the design of guides that minimise off-target activity. *Cell* is among the highest-impact peer-reviewed journals in biology; this paper has been cited over 6,000 times and is considered one of the foundational structural works of the CRISPR field.

**3. CRISPR-Cas9-mediated genome editing and guide RNA design** — Ran, Hsu, Wright, Agarwala, Scott, Zhang (*Nature Protocols*, 2013)
<https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4602062/>
*Estimated reading time: 8–10 min — Introduction and the "Cellular Outcomes of Genome Editing" section*

Feng Zhang's group at the Broad Institute was among the first to demonstrate CRISPR-Cas9 editing in human cells (published in January 2013, simultaneous with George Church's lab). This *Nature Protocols* paper from the same group is one of the most-cited practical guides to using the system, but its first section contains something more durable than protocol: a clear conceptual explanation of the two DNA repair pathways (NHEJ and HDR) and what each produces in the context of genome editing. Non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) rejoins the broken ends quickly but imprecisely, producing small insertions and deletions (indels) that typically disrupt the reading frame and inactivate the gene — the outcome exploited by Casgevy to disable BCL11A. Homology-directed repair (HDR) uses a DNA template to reconstitute the sequence with precision — the pathway needed if you want to correct a point mutation or insert a new sequence. The paper explains why NHEJ is the dominant pathway in most cell types and why HDR efficiency is a persistent bottleneck for correction-type therapies. Available open-access via PubMed Central.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

**How Cas9 finds its target among three billion base pairs**

The human genome contains roughly 3.2 billion base pairs. Finding a specific 20-base-pair address among that vastness is not a trivial problem, and understanding how Cas9 does it — and occasionally gets it wrong — is central to both the power and the safety profile of the technology.

Cas9 in complex with its sgRNA is a large (~160 kDa) protein that diffuses through the nucleus in a state of constant, three-dimensional DNA interrogation. It does not scan randomly: it preferentially inspects DNA near nucleosomes (where DNA is locally accessible), and it uses a two-step recognition mechanism. In the first step, the PAM-interacting domain of Cas9's nuclease lobe makes transient contacts with DNA at candidate PAM sites (NGG sequences in SpCas9). Because NGG occurs once roughly every 8 base pairs on average, the protein encounters millions of potential PAM sites in a typical genome. At each site, it briefly "reads" the PAM sequence; if the PAM is not correct, the protein rapidly dissociates and moves on.

At a valid PAM, Cas9 undergoes a conformational change that opens the DNA at the adjacent protospacer, exposing the first few bases to interrogation by the complementary seed region of the sgRNA (~12 bases adjacent to the PAM). If those seed-region bases pair successfully with the guide, the R-loop extends upstream toward the 5′ end of the guide sequence. If the full 20-base pair match is achieved, the R-loop is complete, the HNH domain repositions over the target strand, and the conformational change required for catalysis occurs. The HNH domain cleaves the strand complementary to the sgRNA; the RuvC domain cleaves the non-complementary strand. Both cuts occur approximately 3 base pairs upstream of the PAM, producing a predominantly blunt-ended double-strand break.

**The two repair pathways and why the choice matters**

A double-strand break is one of the most dangerous lesions a cell can experience. Unrepaired, it leads to chromosomal fragmentation and cell death; misrepaired, it can cause translocations, deletions, or insertions that may be oncogenic. Cells therefore repair DSBs rapidly, through one of two principal pathways.

*Non-homologous end joining (NHEJ)* is the default pathway in most mammalian cells throughout the cell cycle. It works by directly ligating the broken ends, often after limited end processing by nucleases and polymerases. Because this processing is imprecise, NHEJ repairs frequently introduce small insertions or deletions — "indels" — at the break site. In a coding exon, an indel of 1 or 2 nucleotides will typically shift the reading frame, causing premature termination and producing a non-functional protein. This makes NHEJ ideal for gene disruption: to inactivate BCL11A's erythroid enhancer in the Casgevy therapy, NHEJ is exactly what is needed. NHEJ is also the default outcome when Cas9 cuts in post-mitotic cells (neurons, cardiomyocytes) or in quiescent stem cells — cells in which the HDR pathway is not robustly active.

*Homology-directed repair (HDR)* uses a homologous DNA template to repair the break with high fidelity. If a researcher provides an exogenous DNA template — a single-stranded oligonucleotide or a plasmid with homology arms flanking the intended edit — the cell's recombination machinery can use it to copy the desired sequence into the break site. HDR is the pathway required for precise correction: replacing a disease-causing point mutation with the normal sequence, inserting a reporter gene at a specific locus, or correcting a deletion. The limitation is that HDR is active primarily in S and G2 phases of the cell cycle, when sister chromatids are present as endogenous repair templates. In most primary human cells relevant to therapy — especially hematopoietic stem cells and non-dividing differentiated cells — HDR efficiency is low relative to NHEJ, typically in the single-digit-percent range without additional strategies to bias repair. Approaches to improving HDR efficiency include: synchronising cells in S/G2 phase, inhibiting key NHEJ factors (e.g., Ku70/Ku80, DNA-PKcs), and using asymmetric single-stranded DNA templates. In the clinic, the low efficiency of HDR in quiescent HSCs has been one of the motivations for developing base editing and prime editing — technologies that achieve precise edits without requiring HDR at all (Day 4).

**The crystal structure and what it explained**

The Nishimasu 2014 Cell structure clarified three important features of Cas9 specificity that had been known empirically but not mechanistically explained.

First, the seed region: bases 1–12 of the protospacer (counting from the PAM) are interrogated before the distal bases, and mismatches in the seed region almost completely abolish cleavage, while mismatches at positions 17–20 are often tolerated. This is because R-loop formation proceeds directionally from the PAM-proximal end; a mismatch early in the process prevents the conformational change needed for cleavage.

Second, PAM readout is performed by specific amino acid contacts in the PAM-interacting domain (primarily Arg1333 and Arg1335 of SpCas9 for the GG dinucleotide). This understanding has enabled the engineering of Cas9 variants — SpRY, SaCas9, Cas12a — with altered or relaxed PAM requirements, greatly expanding the sequences addressable by CRISPR editing.

Third, the overall bilobed structure explains the large conformational changes Cas9 undergoes upon sgRNA and DNA binding. The protein in the apo state (without RNA or DNA) is compact and catalytically inactive; binding the sgRNA induces a conformational change that opens a positively charged channel for DNA interrogation; and R-loop completion triggers a further conformational change that activates both catalytic domains simultaneously. This "two-checkpoint" activation mechanism — PAM recognition, then full R-loop completion — provides built-in specificity: cutting can only occur if both conditions are satisfied.

## Questions to think about

1. NHEJ is the default repair pathway and typically produces indels that disrupt gene function — which is exactly what Casgevy exploits. But could an off-target Cas9 cut in a tumour-suppressor gene, repaired by NHEJ to produce a frame-shift, contribute to cancer risk in a treated patient? How would you design a long-term safety study to test this hypothesis, and over what timeframe?

2. HDR efficiency in quiescent primary cells remains the primary bottleneck for correction-type editing. Cell cycle synchronisation, NHEJ inhibitors, and template design improvements have all been explored. If you could increase HDR efficiency to 50% in human HSCs, what disease applications would become tractable that are currently not, and what new safety questions would that raise?

3. The crystal structure of Cas9 bound to its target DNA was essential for understanding off-target activity and for engineering improved variants. How dependent is the next generation of precision editing tools (base editors, prime editors) on comparable structural knowledge — do you expect structural biology to continue to be rate-limiting for this field, or has it been largely superseded by high-throughput functional screening?
