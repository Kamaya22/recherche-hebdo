# CRISPR and genome editing therapies — Day 7/7: Synthesis — what is settled, what is open, and where to go next

## Context

Over the past seven days this series has moved from bacteria to bioethics — from the 2007 Barrangou experiment demonstrating that CRISPR is a microbial immune system, through the 2012 Jinek-Doudna-Charpentier paper that turned it into a programmable tool, into the clinic via Casgevy and nexiguran ziclumeran, forward to base editing and prime editing, sideways into off-target safety science, and finally to the governance landscape and the He Jiankui affair. Today we synthesise.

Synthesis days are useful precisely when a week's material contains a mix of settled findings and genuinely open questions, because they force a distinction that science communication often elides: what do we know with high confidence versus what do we merely believe, suspect, or hope? The CRISPR field is unusual in that both categories are exceptionally rich. The mechanistic understanding of Cas9 and its descendants is mature and deeply verified. The efficacy of approved somatic therapies for hemoglobin disorders is striking. The governance architecture is institutionally serious. But the long-term safety record is thin, the equity problem is severe and worsening, and the deeper philosophical questions about germline editing and enhancement are no closer to resolution than they were in 2018.

*Estimated reading time: 12 min — focus on the framing essay and the first four "open questions" sections*

Published in *PLOS Genetics* in 2023 (an open-access journal of the Public Library of Science), this multi-author essay by thirty-six scientists, ethicists, social scientists, and legal scholars maps seven major open questions in the governance and science of human genome editing. The questions range from the technical (what criteria would establish that a heritable edit is safe enough?) to the political (who gets to decide, and through what institutions?) to the philosophical (is the therapy-enhancement distinction coherent, and does it matter?). The unusual breadth of the author list — including Sheila Jasanoff (Harvard), Françoise Baylis (Dalhousie), and Eric Lander (MIT/Broad) — ensures that the essay does not retreat to a single disciplinary perspective. Reading it after the week's other materials should feel like a well-oriented review: the questions it raises will be familiar, but their framing here is more precise and more frankly uncomfortable than in any single day's reading. *PLOS Genetics* is a peer-reviewed open-access journal; the article is freely available.

## Guided summary (~10 min)

**What is settled**

Several things can now be stated with high confidence.

*CRISPR-Cas9 works in human patients and produces durable responses.* The CLIMB trials for Casgevy show that >90% of patients with severe sickle cell disease or transfusion-dependent β-thalassemia achieve durable clinical remission after a single treatment. Long-term follow-up data (up to 4+ years) show no evidence of fading responses. This is not a promise or a preliminary finding — it is a clinically verified result from large (for a rare disease) controlled studies.

*The mechanistic understanding of Cas9 is deep.* The structure of the Cas9-sgRNA-DNA ternary complex is known to high resolution (Day 2). The rules governing specificity — PAM dependence, seed-region dominance, the conformational checkpoints for catalytic activation — are understood well enough to engineer dramatically improved variants (high-fidelity Cas9s, expanded-PAM variants, base editors, prime editors). This mechanistic foundation is more mature than for most drug classes in clinical use.

*Base editing and prime editing are genuine advances, not incremental refinements.* Avoiding double-strand breaks removes a mechanistically distinct category of genotoxicity risk and improves precision. Base editors have entered human trials with clinical results at least as good as Cas9-based approaches. Prime editing, though slower to clinical translation, addresses mutation classes that no other precise editing technology can currently correct. The David Liu laboratory's decade-long engineering effort has produced a toolkit whose depth will shape the field for the next generation.

*In vivo delivery via lipid nanoparticles is clinically feasible for liver-targeted editing.* NTLA-2001/nexiguran ziclumeran and CTX310 have both demonstrated that CRISPR components can be safely delivered intravenously, that the LNP vehicle does not cause prohibitive toxicity, and that the intended genomic edit (TTR silencing, ANGPTL3 knockout) occurs at clinically meaningful efficiency after a single infusion. This removes the requirement for ex vivo cell manufacturing for applications addressable in the liver.

**What is open**

Equally important — and less often stated — is what remains genuinely unknown.

*Long-term safety of somatic editing is unknown beyond 5 years.* The most relevant adverse event for Casgevy would be secondary haematological malignancy arising from a clonal advantage conferred to an HSC carrying an off-target or on-target structural variant. Current follow-up is insufficient to detect a risk of, say, 1 in 200 over 15 years. The required post-market surveillance registries are in place, but will take a decade to provide definitive data. This is not a reason to withhold effective therapies from patients with life-threatening disease, but it is a reason to maintain honest uncertainty about the full risk profile.

*Delivery to tissues beyond liver and blood is largely unsolved.* LNPs are hepatotropic; adapting them for muscle, lung, central nervous system, or tumor microenvironment is a major unsolved engineering problem. AAV delivery of CRISPR components is feasible for eye and muscle but has immunogenicity limitations. Most of the 136 active trials target blood disorders (where ex vivo editing works) or liver (where LNPs work). The diseases where need is greatest may not map onto the tissues that current delivery can reach.

*Equity remains a structural failure, not merely a policy gap.* As of 2025, no realistic pathway exists to make Casgevy available to the 80% of the world's sickle cell patients who live in sub-Saharan Africa. This is not a near-term solvable problem even with perfect political will, because it depends on infrastructure (apheresis centers, conditioning capacity, post-infusion monitoring) that does not exist in most of the affected region — let alone the $2.2 million list price. Simpler delivery modalities (in vivo LNP-based editing without ex vivo processing, cheaper conditioning) are necessary conditions for any meaningful equity advance, and none of them are in late-stage clinical development for sickle cell disease.

*The governance of germline editing is voluntary, non-binding, and not universal.* The moratorium calls, summit statements, and WHO recommendations that have shaped international discourse since 2018 (Day 6) are persuasive to scientists and policymakers who are already persuaded. They have no enforcement mechanism for a researcher in an unregulated environment who, like He Jiankui, believes the rules do not apply. The 2025 joint call by ISCT, ARM, and ASGCT for a ten-year moratorium reflects genuine consensus among professional societies — but professional society statements are, by definition, addressed to their memberships, not to all potential actors globally.

**The five biggest open questions**

Drawing across all of this week's readings, the most consequential unresolved questions are:

1. **Safety over decades in somatic editing**: Will post-market surveillance of Casgevy-treated patients over a 10–20 year follow-up reveal a detectable increase in haematological malignancy? If it does, can the risk be attributed to the editing itself, to the conditioning regimen (busulfan), or to natural history of the disease? Answering this requires well-designed registries that are already running, but the answer is 15+ years away.

2. **The delivery problem**: Can LNP delivery be generalised beyond liver to muscle, CNS, eye, and lung at therapeutic efficiency and without prohibitive immunogenicity? This is the rate-limiting step for treating the great majority of monogenic diseases in which the affected tissue is not blood or liver.

3. **Equitable access**: Is a pathway to affordable, infrastructure-light CRISPR therapy for sickle cell disease in sub-Saharan Africa achievable within 20 years? This depends on in vivo conditioning-free editing, simplified manufacturing, and pricing reform — none of which are currently on a clinical or commercial trajectory to converge on that timeline.

4. **Germline editing governance**: If a country decides in the future that germline editing is justified for a specific serious genetic condition, what international mechanism exists to validate that decision? The current answer is: none. Building credible international governance institutions before, rather than after, this decision is made by some national authority somewhere in the world is a problem of political science and international law, not molecular biology — but molecular biologists are among the few actors positioned to demand it.

5. **The therapy-enhancement boundary**: The strongest current consensus — that germline editing for enhancement is unacceptable — rests partly on a therapy-enhancement distinction that is philosophically contested and practically porous. As somatic gene therapies become routine and safe, the pressure to extend precision editing to enhancement traits will increase. How should societies decide, through what institutions, and with what level of international coordination, where to draw lines that are defensible across cultures with different values?

## Pointers for going deeper

**For the molecular science:**
- David Liu's group maintains a comprehensive resource on base editing and prime editing at the Broad Institute: <https://www.broadinstitute.org/bios/david-liu>
- The Innovative Genomics Institute publishes annual clinical trial updates and accessible mechanistic explainers: <https://innovativegenomics.org>

**For the clinical and regulatory evidence:**
- The CLIMB trial results and Casgevy's prescribing information (including the complete off-target analysis) are available in the FDA's Biologics License Application review documents, publicly posted on the FDA website.
- PubMed searches for "exagamglogene autotemcel," "nexiguran ziclumeran," and "ristoglogene autogetemcel" will return the most current clinical publications.

**For ethics and governance:**
- The Nuffield Council on Bioethics (nuffield bioethics.org) maintains the most sustained and rigorous running commentary on genome editing ethics in the UK context.
- The Hastings Center Report, *The American Journal of Bioethics*, and *The CRISPR Journal* regularly publish peer-reviewed ethics analyses of genome editing.
- Jennifer Doudna's 2020 Nobel Lecture (<https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2020/doudna/lecture/>) is the most accessible authoritative account of the discovery, the clinical transition, and the ethical weight she attaches to both.

**A closing note on epistemic humility**

This week has covered a technology whose clinical impact in the near term is certain to be transformative and whose long-term consequences — medical, social, and ethical — are genuinely uncertain. CRISPR is not "safe" in the sense that aspirin is safe, nor "dangerous" in the sense that a poorly characterised new chemical entity is dangerous. It occupies the same category as most powerful medical technologies: conditionally beneficial, with a risk profile that depends critically on how it is used, by whom, in which patients, and for what purpose. The best guide through that uncertainty is not optimism or pessimism but careful attention to evidence as it accumulates — which is, in the end, what a week of structured reading is for.
