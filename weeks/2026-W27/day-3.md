# CRISPR and genome editing therapies — Day 3/7: Approved therapies and the clinical pipeline

## Context

This is the first reading of this week's series on CRISPR and genome editing therapies — we begin on Day 3 because the week started mid-cycle. Days 1 and 2, when they appear, will cover the discovery of CRISPR and its molecular machinery; today's reading is designed to stand on its own as an introduction to where these therapies now stand clinically. We will loop back to some of today's themes from a mechanistic angle later in the week.

For most of the decade following Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier's landmark 2012 demonstration that CRISPR-Cas9 could be programmably directed to cut DNA, the therapy lived in the future tense. Researchers would say CRISPR *could* cure sickle cell disease or *might one day* treat hereditary blindness. On December 8, 2023, that future tense collapsed. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration approved Casgevy (exagamglogene autotemcel) — the first medicine to reach regulatory approval via CRISPR-Cas9 genome editing. Three weeks earlier, the UK had been the first regulator in the world to approve the same product. By December 15, the European Medicines Agency had followed. What had taken decades of development for any previous platform drug class took roughly eleven years from first CRISPR paper to prescription.

Today we examine what the approved therapies are, what the clinical data actually show, what the broader pipeline of trials promises (and where it still struggles), and why a technology capable of functionally curing a disease may be accessible to only a small fraction of the patients it could benefit.

## Today's readings (~22 min total)

**1. CRISPR-Cas9 Gene Editing for Sickle Cell Disease and β-Thalassemia** — Frangoul, Altshuler, Cappellini et al. (*New England Journal of Medicine*, 2021)
<https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2031054>
*Estimated reading time: 10 min — abstract, Patient Outcomes section, and Table 2*

Published online in December 2020 and in print in NEJM in January 2021, this brief report describes the first two patients treated in the CLIMB trials — one with severe transfusion-dependent β-thalassemia (TDT), one with sickle cell disease (SCD) — with CTX001, the therapy now marketed as Casgevy. Both patients achieved fetal hemoglobin levels far above the therapeutic threshold within weeks of infusion; neither required further transfusions or experienced vaso-occlusive crises through the observation period. This paper is the foundational clinical reference for Casgevy: it is the document that turned CRISPR from a bench tool to a potential cure. It is written for clinician-scientists but its patient outcomes section is accessible to any scientifically literate reader. *The New England Journal of Medicine* is one of the most rigorously peer-reviewed medical journals in existence.

**2. CRISPR-Cas9 In Vivo Gene Editing for Transthyretin Amyloidosis** — Gillmore, Gane, Taubel et al. (*New England Journal of Medicine*, 2021)
<https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2107454>
*Estimated reading time: 10 min — abstract, Results, and Discussion*

While Casgevy works by editing cells *outside* the body and reinfusing them, NTLA-2001 achieves genome editing *inside* the body via a single intravenous infusion. This Phase 1 paper reports the first human data for systemic in vivo CRISPR delivery: six patients with hereditary transthyretin amyloidosis received lipid nanoparticles carrying Cas9 mRNA and a guide RNA targeting the TTR gene in liver cells. At the highest dose tested, serum transthyretin levels fell by 87% within 28 days, with no serious adverse events attributable to the editing. The significance of this paper reaches well beyond ATTR amyloidosis: it demonstrated that CRISPR components can be delivered intravenously to an internal organ in humans, opening a therapeutic paradigm that does not require cell extraction, ex vivo editing, or bone marrow conditioning. Published in *NEJM* and cited as a landmark in multiple regulatory and scientific reviews of the field.

**3. CRISPR Clinical Trials: A 2026 Update** — Innovative Genomics Institute, UC Berkeley / UCSF (2026)
<https://innovativegenomics.org/news/crispr-clinical-trials-2026/>
*Estimated reading time: 5–8 min*

The Innovative Genomics Institute (IGI), co-founded by Jennifer Doudna at UC Berkeley and UCSF, publishes annual landscape reviews of CRISPR clinical trials written for scientifically literate non-specialist audiences. This 2026 edition maps the approximately 136 currently active trials across disease areas, discusses the growing share of in vivo delivery approaches (36 trials, up from near zero in 2020), and identifies where the pipeline is concentrated versus thin. It complements the two NEJM papers above by providing the panoramic view — where the technology is heading — rather than depth on any single trial. The IGI is one of the foremost academic research institutes in genome editing, and its public communications are written and reviewed by active researchers.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

**From proof-of-concept to prescription: the logic of Casgevy**

The therapeutic rationale behind Casgevy has a specific elegance that made it tractable before CRISPR tools were fully mature. Both sickle cell disease and β-thalassemia are caused by mutations in the adult hemoglobin gene (HBB). But humans carry a second hemoglobin program — fetal hemoglobin (HbF), encoded by the HBG1/HBG2 genes — that is highly active in utero and for a few months after birth. After infancy, HbF is silenced by a transcription factor called BCL11A, which acts as a molecular switch. The observation that people with naturally high HbF levels (due to a condition called hereditary persistence of fetal hemoglobin) have dramatically milder sickle cell disease suggested a therapeutic hypothesis: if BCL11A could be disrupted specifically in red blood cell progenitors, HbF would re-emerge in adults and functionally compensate for the defective HBB product. CRISPR-Cas9 provided a precise, efficient tool to cut the BCL11A erythroid enhancer in a patient's own hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs), permanently disabling the switch.

**The ex vivo manufacturing process: months of complexity**

Casgevy is an ex vivo therapy — the editing happens outside the body. The clinical workflow has five demanding steps. First, a patient receives growth factors and a mobilization agent to drive HSCs from the bone marrow into peripheral blood, from which they are collected by apheresis over several days. Second, the cells are shipped to a manufacturing facility where CRISPR-Cas9 (delivered via electroporation) edits the BCL11A enhancer; the edited cells are then expanded, exhaustively quality-tested, and frozen — a process that typically takes several months. Third — and this is the step that gives clinicians most pause — the patient undergoes myeloablative conditioning with busulfan: high-dose chemotherapy that destroys the native bone marrow to create engraftment space for the edited cells. Busulfan is effective but toxic; it causes infertility in a significant proportion of patients, and a small number have experienced serious hepatic complications. Fourth, the edited cell product is infused. Fifth, the patient is hospitalized for weeks to months while the new bone marrow reconstitutes itself, during which they are profoundly immunocompromised. Total elapsed time from stem cell collection to stable engraftment typically ranges from six to nine months.

**The pivotal trial results**

Despite this procedural burden, the efficacy data from the CLIMB trials are striking. In CLIMB SCD-121, 29 of 31 evaluable patients with severe sickle cell disease were free of vaso-occlusive crises — the agonizing, recurrent pain episodes that define and shorten lives in SCD — for at least one year after infusion. All evaluable patients achieved HbF levels exceeding 20% of total hemoglobin (the approximate threshold associated with clinical benefit), with many reaching 40–45%. In the parallel CLIMB THAL-111 trial for β-thalassemia, 39 of 42 evaluable patients became transfusion-independent for at least one year. Long-term follow-up data have confirmed that these responses are durable — HbF remains elevated years after a single treatment. Frangoul et al.'s 2021 NEJM paper described the first two patients in these trials; both had sustained responses through extended follow-up, with one β-thalassemia patient achieving 46% HbF by twelve months after infusion. These outcomes substantially exceed what conventional therapies (hydroxyurea for SCD, regular transfusions for TDT) can deliver, and for many patients represent the closest thing to a functional cure currently available.

**In vivo CRISPR: a different paradigm entirely**

Casgevy's manufacturing complexity is not an accident or a temporary engineering limitation — it is inherent to ex vivo cell therapy. Any time you extract cells, edit them outside the body, and reinfuse them, you are committing to a lengthy, expensive, patient-specific manufacturing pipeline. NTLA-2001, now known as nexiguran ziclumeran, takes an entirely different approach: it delivers CRISPR components directly into the body via lipid nanoparticles (LNPs), the same vehicle technology used in COVID-19 mRNA vaccines. A patient receives a single intravenous infusion; LNPs carrying Cas9 mRNA and a guide RNA traffic preferentially to the liver (LNPs are naturally hepatotropic) and enter liver cells, where the Cas9 protein is transiently expressed, cuts the target TTR gene, and is then degraded. The liver cells are permanently edited; the Cas9 is not. Transthyretin (TTR) protein production drops sharply and, based on current data, appears to remain low indefinitely after a single dose.

The 2021 NEJM paper (Gillmore et al.) showed up to 87% TTR reduction at the highest Phase 1 dose. By November 2023, Intellia reported Phase 1 data from over 60 patients showing a median serum TTR reduction above 90%, sustained for up to and beyond one year, with no serious adverse events attributed to the CRISPR editing. ATTR amyloidosis, the target disease, affects the heart and peripheral nerves and is progressive and often fatal; existing drugs (siRNA-based silencers and small molecule stabilizers) reduce TTR by 80–88%, so NTLA-2001 is at least competitive with established treatments, with the added potential advantage of being a single dose rather than a lifelong prescription. A Phase 3 trial (MAGNITUDE, ~765 patients) is enrolling.

**A widening pipeline: from blood and liver to eye, heart, and brain**

The IGI's 2026 landscape overview shows 136 active CRISPR clinical trials. Several developments in recent years mark the field's widening reach:

*The eye*: EDIT-101 (Editas Medicine) became the first CRISPR therapy delivered directly inside the human body when it was injected subretinally into the eyes of patients with Leber congenital amaurosis type 10 (LCA10), caused by a specific CEP290 mutation. Results published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* in 2024 showed no serious treatment-related adverse events in 14 participants and some improvements in photoreceptor function. The trial was subsequently paused due to modest overall efficacy — a reminder that safety and functional cure are distinct endpoints that the field is still calibrating.

*Cardiovascular disease*: CTX310 (CRISPR Therapeutics) targets ANGPTL3 in the liver via LNP delivery. A 2025 Phase 1 paper in *NEJM* reported that a single infusion substantially reduced both LDL cholesterol and triglycerides in 15 patients with severe lipid disorders, with effects sustained for at least 60 days. This is the first CRISPR therapy to target a cardiovascular risk factor rather than a rare monogenic disease.

*Personalized medicine*: In 2025, the FDA approved, under an accelerated individualized pathway, a bespoke in vivo CRISPR therapy designed for a single infant with carbamoyl phosphate synthetase 1 (CPS1) deficiency — a rare metabolic disease with high infant mortality. The therapy was designed, manufactured, and delivered in approximately six months. Though it affects only one patient, this case suggests that in vivo CRISPR platforms may eventually support individualized therapies for ultra-rare diseases on a practical timescale.

*Cancer and immune diseases*: Oncology now constitutes the largest single category in the CRISPR pipeline, including both engineered T-cell therapies (where CRISPR is used to delete inhibitory checkpoints from CAR-T cells) and direct targeting of tumor cells or immune dysregulation.

**The access problem: a $2.2 million therapy for a disease of the poor**

Casgevy's list price in the United States is $2.2 million per treatment; Lyfgenia (the lentiviral-vector gene therapy approved the same day) is $3.1 million. The economics behind these figures are not straightforwardly predatory — each product is patient-specific, manufacturing is complex and failure-prone, and clinical development costs were enormous. But sickle cell disease's global burden is heavily concentrated in sub-Saharan Africa, India, and the Middle East: approximately 300,000 children are born with SCD every year, the vast majority in low- and middle-income countries where a $2.2 million individual treatment is categorically inaccessible. Even within the United States, roughly 50–60% of SCD patients receive coverage through Medicaid (the means-tested public insurance program), and federal negotiators have had to secure discounts from manufacturers just to make the therapy reachable to this population. As of late 2024, only a small number of authorized treatment centers in the US were equipped to deliver Casgevy, and actual uptake remained far below the eligible population.

Researchers and bioethicists have pointed out the structural irony: CRISPR was partly developed with public funding, it targets diseases whose burden falls most heavily on historically marginalized populations (SCD disproportionately affects people of African descent), and yet its first approved incarnation is priced in a way that reinforces rather than corrects health inequity. A 2024 commentary in *The CRISPR Journal* (Rueda, De Miguel Beriain, and Montoliu) argued that affordable pricing is not merely desirable but an ethical imperative given the disease demographics and the public-good nature of the underlying science.

**CRISPR vs. earlier gene therapies**

A final framing point: what makes CRISPR different from gene therapies that have existed since the 1990s? Earlier gene therapies mostly worked by inserting a functional copy of a gene into cells using viral vectors. Lyfgenia, which was approved the same day as Casgevy but is *not* CRISPR-based, does exactly this — it uses a lentiviral vector to deliver a functional anti-sickling hemoglobin gene. CRISPR, by contrast, can make targeted edits to the existing genome: disrupting, repairing, or altering specific sequences at defined locations. This precision has two practical advantages over insertional gene therapy. First, it can correct or remodel the existing sequence rather than compensating for it with a new copy inserted at a semi-random location (random insertion carries oncogenic risk, as earlier viral gene therapies demonstrated). Second, as NTLA-2001 illustrates, CRISPR editing can be achieved with non-viral delivery vehicles (LNPs), avoiding the immune complications and manufacturing constraints of viral vectors. Days 4 and 5 of this week will examine the next generation of tools — base editors and prime editors — that take this precision even further.

## Questions to think about

1. Casgevy requires myeloablative conditioning — destruction of the patient's existing bone marrow with chemotherapy — as a prerequisite step. This conditioning carries real risks (including a high probability of infertility) that are independent of the CRISPR editing itself. How should we weigh this procedural burden against the prospect of a durable, lifelong response? Does the answer change for a patient who is 12 versus a patient who is 40?

2. NTLA-2001 and CTX310 deliver CRISPR editing via lipid nanoparticles to liver cells — cells that cannot be collected, edited in a controlled environment, and quality-checked before infusion. The editing happens inside the patient, irreversibly, based on pre-infusion manufacturing quality alone. Does the simplicity of in vivo delivery introduce a risk profile fundamentally different from ex vivo approaches, and how should regulators weigh that difference?

3. Sickle cell disease affects hundreds of thousands of children born every year in sub-Saharan Africa, nearly all of whom have no realistic path to a $2.2 million therapy within the foreseeable future. What structural changes — in intellectual property, global manufacturing agreements, R&D incentive design, or international health financing — would have to occur for CRISPR gene therapies to become genuinely global medicines rather than treatments for wealthy-country patients only?
