# Week 2026-W27 — CRISPR and genome editing therapies

## Why it matters

CRISPR-Cas9 has transformed molecular biology from a research curiosity into a clinical reality in under a decade. The 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry recognized Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier for a discovery that now underpins dozens of active clinical trials and, as of 2023-2024, the first approved gene-editing medicines for sickle-cell disease and beta-thalassemia. Understanding how CRISPR works — and where it still struggles — is essential for anyone following modern medicine, biotechnology policy, or bioethics. Beyond treating disease, the same tools raise profound questions about germline editing, equitable access, and the long-term governance of human biology.

## Tentative 7-day outline

- **Day 1 — Panorama**: What CRISPR is, how it was discovered (from a bacterial immune system to a programmable scalpel), and why it represents a step change over earlier editing tools (ZFNs, TALENs).
- **Day 2 — Molecular mechanisms**: The Cas9–guide-RNA complex in atomic detail; how DNA double-strand breaks are created; the two main repair pathways (NHEJ and HDR) and why they matter for therapeutic strategy.
- **Day 3 — Approved therapies and clinical pipeline**: The first regulatory approvals (Casgevy/exagamglogene autotemcel and Lyfgenia), how they work in practice, and the broader clinical trial landscape.
- **Day 4 — Next-generation tools**: Base editing and prime editing — what they can do that classic CRISPR cannot, and their current clinical status.
- **Day 5 — Off-target effects and safety**: How off-target cuts are detected and quantified, what the clinical safety data show so far, and remaining unknowns.
- **Day 6 — Ethics, governance, and germline editing**: The He Jiankui affair, international moratoriums, the distinction between somatic and germline editing, and equity concerns in access to gene therapies.
- **Day 7 — Synthesis**: What is settled vs. open, the three or four biggest unresolved questions, and pointers for going deeper.
