# Theme: CRISPR and Genome Editing Therapies

## Why it matters

In December 2023, the FDA approved Casgevy — the first human therapy built on CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing — marking a turning point from laboratory technique to licensed medicine. CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) borrowed from bacterial immunity offers the ability to rewrite any sequence in the human genome with a precision and simplicity that previous molecular tools could not match. The result is a new therapeutic modality that attacks disease at its genetic root rather than managing symptoms, with potential applications across hundreds of rare and common conditions. Understanding CRISPR means understanding not only a remarkable piece of molecular machinery but also a profound set of ethical questions about the limits of human intervention in the genome — including the line between somatic therapy (treating one patient) and germline editing (altering inheritable DNA for all future generations).

## Tentative 7-day outline

- **Day 1 — Panorama**: What CRISPR is; the discovery arc from bacterial immune systems to the Nobel Prize; core concepts (guide RNA, Cas9, PAM, repair pathways); a map of the week.
- **Day 2 — Mechanism in depth**: How the CRISPR-Cas9 molecular machine finds its target, binds, and cuts; NHEJ vs. HDR; why PAM matters; and the clinical proof-of-concept that connects mechanism to medicine (Casgevy).
- **Day 3 — Delivery**: Getting CRISPR cargo into the right cells in the right organ — viral vectors (AAV, lentivirus), lipid nanoparticles, and ex vivo vs. in vivo strategies; trade-offs in immunogenicity, efficiency, and tissue specificity.
- **Day 4 — Casgevy in depth**: Anatomy of the first approved CRISPR therapy — the BCL11A story, the clinical trial data for sickle cell disease and beta-thalassemia, manufacturing challenges, and the $2 million price question.
- **Day 5 — The clinical pipeline**: CRISPR in cancer (CAR-T enhancement), infectious disease (HIV reservoirs, hepatitis B), and other rare genetic conditions; what the 100+ active trials tell us about where the field is going.
- **Day 6 — Safety and next-generation tools**: Off-target editing and how it is detected; base editors and prime editors that avoid double-strand breaks; high-fidelity Cas9 variants; the regulatory science of off-target risk.
- **Day 7 — Ethics, governance, and equity**: Somatic vs. germline editing; the He Jiankui affair; ISSCR and WHO frameworks; access and global equity when a therapy costs $2 million; what the field needs to get right before heritable editing can be considered.
