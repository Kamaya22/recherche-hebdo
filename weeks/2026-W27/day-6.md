# CRISPR and genome editing therapies — Day 6/7: Ethics, governance, and the germline editing debate

## Context

Day 3 of this week introduced the first approved CRISPR therapies — Casgevy for sickle cell disease and β-thalassemia, and in vivo CRISPR approaches for conditions such as hereditary transthyretin amyloidosis — and began to surface a tension that runs through all of modern medicine but is uniquely sharp here: a technology of extraordinary therapeutic promise that is, at present, available almost exclusively to wealthy-country patients, while the heaviest disease burden falls on populations least able to pay. Days 4 and 5 (next in the series) will examine the next-generation tools — base editing and prime editing — that may eventually reduce some of the technical barriers. Today we step back from the clinical science to ask the harder questions: what are the ethical limits of genome editing, who has the authority to set them, and how should society govern a tool capable of making heritable changes to the human species?

Those questions became impossible to defer on the evening of November 25, 2018, when He Jiankui — a researcher at the Southern University of Science and Technology in Shenzhen — posted a series of YouTube videos announcing the birth of twin girls whose embryos he had edited using CRISPR-Cas9. The announcement triggered one of the most intense and globally coordinated ethical responses in the history of science. Within days, two hundred scientists signed an open letter of condemnation, multiple national academies issued statements, and the field found itself forced to confront a problem it had hoped more time and more governance machinery could prevent. He was sentenced to three years in prison by a Chinese court in December 2019 for forging ethical review documents and misleading physicians. He was released in April 2022, and his subsequent attempts to resume research have kept the question of how science governs itself from fading from view.

Today's reading traces three interlocking problems: the scientific and ethical failures of the He Jiankui experiment itself; the international governance landscape — moratoriums, reports, summits, and their limits — that has developed around germline editing since 2018; and the equity dimension, which concerns not only access to already-approved somatic therapies but also the deeper question of who participates in decisions about which human traits are worth editing. Day 7 will synthesize these threads alongside the remaining open scientific questions.

## Today's readings (~22 min total)

**1. Adopt a moratorium on heritable genome editing** — Lander, Baylis, Zhang, Charpentier, Berg et al. (*Nature*, 2019)
<https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-019-00726-5>
*Estimated reading time: 10 min*

Published in *Nature* in March 2019, three months after He Jiankui's announcement, this commentary was signed by eighteen leading scientists and bioethicists — including Emmanuelle Charpentier (co-developer of CRISPR-Cas9 and 2020 Nobel laureate), Feng Zhang (Broad Institute), Eric Lander (then-director of the Broad Institute), and Françoise Baylis (bioethicist, Dalhousie University). It does not call for a permanent ban on heritable genome editing; instead, it proposes a framework in which nations voluntarily commit not to approve clinical germline editing unless and until a specific set of conditions has been met: an international body with scientific, ethical, and societal representation to assess readiness; transparent research registries; evidence of a compelling medical need that cannot be addressed by other means; broad societal consensus in the country proposing to proceed; and sustained mechanisms for long-term follow-up. The article is essential reading because it established the terms on which the field would subsequently debate governance: not "never" versus "now," but "not until these conditions are met." *Nature* is among the highest-impact peer-reviewed journals in science; the article's signatories represent a cross-section of the people most responsible for the field's development.

**2. Heritable Human Genome Editing — Summary and Key Findings** — International Commission on the Clinical Use of Human Germline Genome Editing (U.S. National Academies / Royal Society, 2020)
<https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK561519/>
*Estimated reading time: 8 min — focus on the Summary, Chapter 1 (Introduction), and the section "Criteria for a Responsible Translational Pathway"*

This report is the product of the most authoritative governance commission yet convened on the topic, established jointly by the U.S. National Academy of Medicine, the U.S. National Academy of Sciences, and the UK Royal Society, with participation from leading scientific academies in eleven countries. Its central conclusion: no clinical use of heritable genome editing should proceed until it can be demonstrated that precise genomic changes can be made reliably and without introducing undesired changes — a standard that, the commission found, had not been met in 2020 and that remains unmet in 2026. The report also articulates a "translational pathway" defining what preclinical evidence, regulatory infrastructure, public oversight, and post-market monitoring would be required before any nation could responsibly consider approving germline editing for clinical use. It is freely available in full on the NIH Bookshelf. The National Academies of Sciences and the Royal Society are the premier scientific advisory bodies of the United States and United Kingdom respectively; their joint imprimatur represents near-consensus among the major Western scientific institutions.

**3. Statement from the Organising Committee of the Third International Summit on Human Genome Editing** — Royal Society, National Academies, Academy of Medical Sciences, The World Academy of Sciences (2023)
<https://royalsociety.org/news/2023/03/statement-third-international-summit-human-genome-editing/>
*Estimated reading time: 5–8 min*

Issued in March 2023 from the Francis Crick Institute in London, this is the most recent international consensus statement on genome editing and the third in a series that began with the first summit in Washington, D.C. in 2015 and continued in Hong Kong in 2018 — where He Jiankui made his now-infamous appearance. The 2023 statement reaffirms that "heritable human genome editing remains unacceptable at this time" because the necessary safety, efficacy, and governance preconditions have not been met. It simultaneously acknowledges the dramatic progress in somatic editing since 2018, endorses accelerating clinical access to approved therapies, and flags the unsustainability of current pricing models as a matter of urgent ethical concern. The statement is short (approximately 2,500 words) and written for a broad audience. It is issued by four of the world's most credible scientific institutions, reflecting input from scientists, clinicians, ethicists, regulators, and patient advocates from multiple countries.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

**The He Jiankui affair: what went wrong, and why it matters beyond one rogue researcher**

At first glance, the He Jiankui case might seem like an isolated breach: one researcher who lacked the ethics and scientific credibility to earn trust, who forged review documents and deceived colleagues, and who was punished accordingly. This framing, while factually accurate, misses the more instructive lesson. He did not operate in a vacuum. He had been trained at prestigious institutions (Stanford, among others) and had connections to respected figures in the field. He had attended scientific conferences where the possibility of germline editing was discussed. He believed — apparently sincerely — that he was doing something beneficial. The more unsettling question his case poses is not "how did one bad actor slip through?" but "what features of the scientific system made this possible, and do those features persist?"

He's target was CCR5, a co-receptor used by most HIV strains to enter white blood cells. A natural deletion (Δ32) in both copies of CCR5 confers near-complete resistance to HIV and appears in roughly one percent of Northern European populations. He used CRISPR-Cas9 in embryos from couples in which the father was HIV-positive to attempt to replicate this deletion. The experiment had three layers of scientific flaw. First, it introduced novel mutations never previously observed in humans, meaning neither their effectiveness against HIV nor their safety profile was known. Second, analysis of He's own data revealed mosaic editing — in at least one of the twin girls (Lulu), one copy of CCR5 remained completely unedited, meaning she likely retains full CCR5 expression and may have no HIV protection. Third, He entirely ignored the existence of HIV strains (notably X4-tropic strains) that use a different co-receptor, CXCR4, making CCR5 deletion incomplete protection even when successful. Beyond the scientific problems, the therapeutic rationale was always weak: HIV is preventable and, when contracted, treatable with antiretroviral therapy; creating germline changes in healthy embryos to prevent a manageable infection is not a compelling medical use case for a technology whose risks are unknown.

The three children — Lulu, Nana, and a third baby born the following year — are reportedly alive and under Chinese state monitoring. Almost no details of their health or development are publicly available. There is no independent long-term follow-up. This opacity is itself an ethical failure: science that affects human beings is subject to obligations of transparency and follow-up that have not been met. The scientific community has a legitimate interest, and arguably an obligation, to understand the long-term health of these children — an interest that has so far been subordinated to Chinese government control.

**The somatic / germline distinction: why the line exists and why it holds**

Understanding why the international consensus around germline editing is so much stricter than the consensus around somatic editing requires being precise about what the distinction means. Somatic editing alters cells in a living individual's body; those changes die with the individual and affect no one else. The individual can, at least in principle, consent to being a research subject or a patient. Germline editing — changes made to eggs, sperm, or early embryos — creates heritable modifications that will be present in every cell of the resulting person and in any children they have. Future generations cannot consent to these changes, because they do not yet exist.

This is not merely a rhetorical point. Consent is the moral architecture on which clinical medicine rests: we require it because bodily changes imposed without agreement are a violation. In somatic gene therapy, consent can be obtained; in germline editing of embryos that will develop into children, it cannot. This asymmetry justifies different governance standards. It is worth emphasizing that the ethical concern is not simply that we might inadvertently produce harmful effects that propagate — though that is a serious safety concern. It is that we would be making a permanent, irreversible decision about the genetic constitution of a person who has no voice in that decision, and doing so based on our current, necessarily limited, scientific knowledge and values.

A second concern is the risk of "genetic enhancement creep." Most of the governance discourse focuses on disease prevention — editing embryos to eliminate a mutation that causes Huntington's disease, for example. The ethical case for this is at least defensible, though contested. But the same technology that could prevent a fatal illness could also be used to select for traits associated with intelligence, height, or appearance. The history of eugenics is a reminder that scientific consensus on which traits are desirable has been dangerously wrong before, and that programs of genetic control have been instruments of racial and social oppression. Most governance proposals — including the 2020 National Academies/Royal Society report — restrict any hypothetical future clinical pathway to preventing serious diseases, explicitly not to enhancement. The boundary, however, is not self-enforcing.

**The international governance landscape: what exists, what works, what doesn't**

The international governance architecture around germline editing consists of several overlapping but non-binding layers. At the national level, approximately seventy countries prohibit clinical germline editing by law or binding regulation; a smaller number, including China, have updated their frameworks substantially since 2018. China's reforms — following extensive legislative and regulatory revision — now include clearer institutional oversight, mandatory ethics review, and criminal penalties for researchers who violate them. The US occupies a distinctive position: while the FDA would have jurisdiction over any clinical application, a congressional rider (in place since 2016 and renewed annually) prohibits the FDA from even reviewing applications for trials involving heritable germline editing, effectively creating a de facto moratorium without formal law. The UK's Human Fertilisation and Embryology Authority (HFEA) provides what is widely considered the most robust national framework, combining specialist regulatory authority, clear law, and delegated oversight.

At the international level, three institutions have produced the most consequential documents: the WHO Expert Advisory Committee's 2021 recommendations (nine areas covering registries, research travel, intellectual property, education, and international coordination); the 2020 National Academies/Royal Society commission report; and the triennial summits. None of these produces binding international law. The 2019 moratorium call in *Nature* by Lander, Charpentier, and sixteen co-signatories proposed a mechanism — a voluntary international framework with agreed conditions — but no such framework has been formally adopted. In May 2025, the International Society for Cell & Gene Therapy (ISCT), the Alliance for Regenerative Medicine (ARM), and the American Society of Gene & Cell Therapy (ASGCT) issued a joint statement in *Cytotherapy* calling for a formal ten-year moratorium. This is the most explicit time-bounded call yet from major scientific societies, and it signals that the field's major professional bodies have moved from "not yet" to "not for a decade, pending a fundamental change in circumstances."

**Equity: the argument that pricing is itself an ethical issue**

Day 3 established the basic equity problem in stark terms: Casgevy costs $2.2 million per patient, while approximately 80% of the world's sickle cell disease burden falls on sub-Saharan Africa. Even within the United States, where coverage through Medicaid has been negotiated, uptake in 2024–2025 remained far below the eligible population due to a shortage of authorized treatment centers, the procedural demands of myeloablative conditioning, and the difficulty of navigating insurance approval processes.

Rueda, de Miguel Beriain, and Montoliu argued in *The CRISPR Journal* in 2024 that affordable pricing is not merely desirable but an ethical imperative. Their argument rests on two pillars: first, the foundational research underlying CRISPR-based therapies was substantially funded by public money — from the NIH, the European Research Council, and other governmental sources — which creates a public-good obligation in the resulting products; second, when a disease predominantly affects a historically marginalized population, pricing a cure at $2.2 million is not neutral market behavior but an active perpetuation of structural health inequality. The argument does not require accepting that pharmaceutical companies have no legitimate return on their investment; it requires accepting that the return cannot be maximized without limit when the cost is borne as exclusion by the most burdened patients.

The structural obstacles to equitable access go beyond pricing alone. Casgevy's ex vivo manufacturing process requires apheresis centers, specialized cell therapy facilities, myeloablative conditioning capacity, and post-infusion monitoring infrastructure that most sub-Saharan African hospitals cannot provide. The Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation and Novartis have collaborated on a program to develop in vivo LNP-based delivery of similar therapeutic edits — a system that could, in principle, be administered with the same infrastructure as a vaccine infusion — but this approach remains years from clinical validation. Even in vivo approaches require cold chain logistics and a baseline of medical monitoring that is not universally available.

**What governance cannot do alone**

It is worth being precise about what the international governance apparatus can and cannot accomplish. It can establish norms that make scientific misconduct more visible and socially costly. It can slow adoption in countries with functioning regulatory institutions. It can provide a framework for shared expectations that makes coordination easier when and if clinical germline editing ever becomes genuinely safe and consensually justifiable. What it cannot do is prevent a researcher in an unmonitored context from attempting germline editing; it cannot make a $2.2 million therapy affordable by agreement; it cannot retroactively correct the genome of a child already born with an experimental edit; and it cannot substitute for the ongoing, slow, and politically contested work of building the international trust and legal infrastructure that robust governance actually requires. The He Jiankui case was not primarily a failure of rules — China had regulations that prohibited his experiment. It was a failure of the social mechanisms that make rules effective: oversight, accountability, and the professional culture that deters violations even when no one is watching.

## Questions to think about

1. The 2019 moratorium call in *Nature* proposed conditions — international framework, compelling medical need, broad societal consensus, long-term follow-up — under which clinical germline editing could eventually be acceptable. He Jiankui believed, however sincerely, that his experiment met analogous conditions. What would it actually take for society to arrive at something approaching genuine consensus on a specific germline editing application — and is such consensus achievable across cultures with fundamentally different values about children, disability, and genetic inheritance?

2. The consent-of-future-generations argument is widely cited to distinguish germline from somatic editing. But future generations cannot consent to many other heritable decisions — the financial debts, environmental conditions, or social inequalities we leave behind. Does the special status of germline editing rest on a principled distinction or on the visceral novelty of genetic modification? If the former, where exactly is the line?

3. Affordable pricing of gene therapies, equitable trial enrollment, and transfer of manufacturing capacity to low- and middle-income countries are all framed as ethical imperatives in recent literature. But each requires either voluntary action by profit-maximizing corporations or binding international agreements that no current institution has the authority to enforce. What existing mechanisms — compulsory licensing under the TRIPS Agreement, advance purchase commitments, public-option manufacturing — come closest to providing a workable lever, and what would it take to deploy them for CRISPR therapies?
