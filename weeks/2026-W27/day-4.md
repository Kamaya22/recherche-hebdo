# CRISPR and genome editing therapies — Day 4/7: Next-generation tools: base editing and prime editing

## Context

Yesterday's reading surveyed the first approved CRISPR therapies — Casgevy for sickle cell disease and β-thalassemia, and nexiguran ziclumeran for ATTR amyloidosis — and mapped the broader clinical pipeline. Both categories of approved therapy share something in common with the classic CRISPR-Cas9 system they are built on: they create or depend on a double-strand DNA break (DSB). Casgevy disrupts the BCL11A enhancer via a DSB repaired by error-prone NHEJ; in vivo therapies using Cas9 do the same at their respective genomic targets. DSBs are physiologically stressful. Cells have evolved multiple repair pathways to respond to them, and those repair pathways are not always precise — NHEJ is inherently mutagenic, repair can be incomplete, and a small fraction of DSBs can result in chromosomal rearrangements or, in extreme cases, p53 activation. For therapeutic applications in which you want to change exactly one letter in a three-billion-letter genome, creating a blunt-ended break first feels like demolishing a wall to hang a picture.

Today's reading covers the two next-generation editing platforms that take a different approach: **base editors** and **prime editors**. Both were largely developed in the laboratory of David Liu at the Broad Institute (a collaboration between Harvard and MIT), and both perform precise, targeted changes to the DNA sequence without creating a DSB. This single engineering choice removes an entire category of mutagenic risk and has enabled a new class of clinical programs that, as of 2025-2026, has produced the first human clinical data for both technologies — and one of those results, for base editing in cardiovascular disease, was published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* just weeks before this reading was written.

The field is moving fast. The 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry recognized Doudna and Charpentier for the foundational CRISPR scissors; in 2025, David Liu received the Breakthrough Prize for base editing and prime editing — the molecular equivalents of an eraser and a pencil acting on the genome's text without tearing the page.

## Today's readings (~23 min total)

**1. Base editing: precision chemistry on the genome and transcriptome of living cells** — Komor, Badran, and Liu (*Nature Reviews Genetics*, 2018)
<https://www.nature.com/articles/s41576-018-0059-1>
*Estimated reading time: 8 min — focus on the Introduction and the sections on CBEs and ABEs*

Written by Alexis Komor (who co-invented the cytosine base editor in Liu's lab), Mohammed Badran, and David Liu, this landmark review in *Nature Reviews Genetics* explains how base editors work from first principles: the architecture of the cytosine base editor (CBE) and the adenine base editor (ABE), how each is derived from a catalytically impaired Cas9 fused to a DNA-acting deaminase enzyme, what editing window limitations mean in practice, and how both differ mechanistically from classical CRISPR-Cas9. *Nature Reviews Genetics* publishes invited reviews by leading researchers in the field; this article has been cited several thousand times and remains the clearest mechanistic introduction to the technology. Reading through the Introduction and the first two major sections will give you the conceptual vocabulary needed to understand the clinical results in readings 2 and 3.

**2. In Vivo Base Editing of PCSK9 with VERVE-102 for Hypercholesterolemia** — *New England Journal of Medicine* (2026)
<https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2601283>
*Estimated reading time: 8 min — abstract, Results section, and Table 2*

Published alongside a late-breaking presentation at the European Atherosclerosis Society Congress in May 2026, this paper reports the Phase 1b Heart-2 trial of VERVE-102 — an adenine base editor delivered via lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) that permanently inactivates the PCSK9 gene in liver cells with a single intravenous infusion. In 35 participants with heterozygous familial hypercholesterolemia (HeFH) or premature coronary artery disease, a single dose produced dose-dependent reductions in serum PCSK9 (up to 88% at 1.0 mg/kg) and LDL cholesterol (up to 62%, an absolute reduction of 78 mg/dL), with effects that remained stable in the 15 participants followed for at least one year. No dose-limiting toxicities occurred. *NEJM* is the world's most-cited medical journal, and this paper represents a pivotal milestone: the first demonstration in a large human cohort that in vivo base editing can produce durable, clinically meaningful reductions in a major cardiovascular risk factor.

**3. Prime Editing for p47phox-Deficient Chronic Granulomatous Disease** — *New England Journal of Medicine* (December 2025)
<https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2509807>
*Estimated reading time: 7 min — abstract, Methods overview, and Results*

Published in *NEJM* on December 7, 2025, this paper reports the first-in-human clinical data for prime editing. PM359, developed by Prime Medicine, is an ex vivo therapy in which a patient's own CD34+ hematopoietic stem cells are prime-edited to correct the specific delGT mutation in the NCF1 gene that causes p47phox-deficient chronic granulomatous disease (CGD) — an inherited immunodeficiency in which neutrophils are unable to kill ingested bacteria and fungi. Two patients received PM359 following myeloablative conditioning; both achieved rapid neutrophil engraftment, with 69% and 83% dihydrorhodamine-positive neutrophils by Day 30 (the clinical threshold for meaningful benefit is approximately 20%; normal is ~100%), and NADPH oxidase activity was maintained through at least 4-6 months of follow-up. Both patients also experienced resolution of CGD-associated colitis with no serious adverse events attributable to the editing. This paper is the first peer-reviewed proof that prime editing can correct a disease-causing mutation in human patients with functional benefit.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

**The problem with scissors: why avoid double-strand breaks?**

Classic CRISPR-Cas9 creates a blunt-ended DSB in both strands of the DNA helix at the target site. The cell must then repair this break. The two main repair pathways — NHEJ and HDR — each have drawbacks. NHEJ is error-prone: it rejoins the broken ends but frequently introduces small insertions or deletions (indels) at the cut site. This is useful when you want to disrupt a gene (as in BCL11A enhancer editing for Casgevy), but useless if your goal is to install a precise sequence change. HDR is more precise but requires a donor template and occurs primarily in dividing cells, which excludes many of the cell types most relevant to therapy (post-mitotic neurons, cardiomyocytes). DSBs also carry a small but nonzero risk of triggering chromosomal rearrangements if two breaks occur simultaneously, and they can activate the p53 tumor suppressor pathway in ways that may select against successfully edited cells. Base editing and prime editing sidestep all of these issues by never breaking the double helix.

**Base editors: a deaminase on a Cas9 frame**

A base editor has two functional domains fused together. The first is a catalytically impaired Cas9 (usually a "nickase" that cuts only one DNA strand, or a fully "dead" dCas9 that cuts neither) directed to the target site by a guide RNA, exactly as in standard CRISPR. The second is a DNA-modifying enzyme — a deaminase — that chemically converts one nucleotide to another within a small window of the exposed, single-stranded DNA near the Cas9 binding site.

The cytosine base editor (CBE), first reported by Komor, Kim, Packer, Zuris, and Liu in *Nature* in 2016 (DOI: 10.1038/nature17946), uses a cytidine deaminase to convert cytosine (C) to uracil (U), which the cell reads and ultimately replicates as thymine (T). The net result: a C·G base pair becomes a T·A base pair. The adenine base editor (ABE), reported by Gaudelli, Komor, Rees et al. in *Nature* in 2017, achieves the reverse conversion: adenine (A) to inosine (I, read as guanine), producing an A·T → G·C change. Together, CBEs and ABEs can mediate all four possible transition mutations (C→T, G→A, A→G, T→C) — the most common class of pathogenic point mutations. A 2024 analysis in *npj Genomic Medicine* found that approximately 62% of all pathogenic single nucleotide variants in ClinVar are addressable by DNA or RNA base editors, covering roughly 30% correctable by CBE and 30% by ABE. What base editors cannot do: they cannot perform transversion mutations (C→A, C→G, A→T, etc.), and they cannot insert or delete nucleotides. For those operations, prime editing is required.

**VERVE-102: base editing the cardiovascular system**

PCSK9 is a protease that degrades LDL receptors on the surface of liver cells. When PCSK9 is active, fewer LDL receptors are available and LDL cholesterol accumulates in the blood. Loss-of-function variants in PCSK9 are already known from human genetics to dramatically lower LDL and reduce cardiovascular events with no apparent adverse effects — people with these natural variants live normal, healthy lives with persistently low LDL. This makes PCSK9 an ideal target for a permanent reduction in LDL via gene editing: you are mimicking a known human genetic variant rather than creating a novel biological state.

VERVE-102 uses an adenine base editor packaged in an LNP formulation that, like the siRNA and CRISPR tools discussed in yesterday's reading, preferentially targets liver cells after intravenous infusion. The ABE makes a precise A→G change in the PCSK9 gene, introducing a stop codon that permanently disables PCSK9 production in treated hepatocytes. Unlike daily statin pills or biweekly subcutaneous injections of PCSK9 monoclonal antibodies, the effect of a single VERVE-102 infusion appears to be permanent — at least over the one-year follow-up reported in the Heart-2 trial, with no evidence of waning.

The Phase 1b results are striking in their magnitude. At the 0.6 mg/kg dose, the mean LDL reduction was 53% with a maximum of 69%; at 1.0 mg/kg, mean LDL fell by 56% with a maximum of 69% and an absolute reduction of 78 mg/dL. Serum PCSK9 fell by up to 88%. Fifteen participants had at least one year of follow-up, and the reductions appear stable. No serious adverse events were attributed to the base editing. The trial population had HeFH or premature coronary artery disease — people at very high cardiovascular risk whose LDL control with existing therapies is often inadequate.

Two important caveats apply. First, this is a Phase 1b trial, not a Phase 3 efficacy study; the trial was powered for safety, not for cardiovascular event reduction, so we cannot yet say that VERVE-102 prevents heart attacks. Second, the therapy edits hepatocyte DNA permanently and irreversibly. If an unexpected problem emerges years later, there is no antidote — in contrast to statins or PCSK9 antibodies, which can simply be stopped.

**Prime editing: a search-and-replace function for the genome**

In October 2019, Andrew Anzalone, Peyton Randolph, and colleagues in David Liu's laboratory published "Search-and-replace genome editing without double-strand breaks or donor DNA" in *Nature* (576:149-157; DOI: 10.1038/s41586-019-1711-4). The paper described prime editing, a more versatile but more mechanistically complex tool than base editing.

A prime editor consists of three components: (1) a Cas9 nickase that cuts only one strand of DNA; (2) an engineered reverse transcriptase fused to the Cas9; and (3) a prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA), which is longer and more sophisticated than a standard guide RNA. The pegRNA has two functional parts: a spacer that guides the complex to the target site (as in standard CRISPR), and a 3′ extension that carries both a primer-binding site and the desired sequence change as a reverse-transcription template. The workflow: Cas9 binds and nicks one DNA strand; the nicked strand folds back and anneals to the primer-binding site on the pegRNA; the reverse transcriptase then uses the pegRNA's extension as a template and writes the new, corrected sequence directly onto the DNA strand; cellular mismatch repair machinery incorporates the change into both strands, completing the edit.

This mechanism supports any type of point mutation (all 12 possible substitutions, not just the 4 transitions addressable by base editors), as well as small insertions and deletions — effectively any small edit that can be encoded in a pegRNA template. The 2019 paper demonstrated more than 175 distinct edits in human cells at efficiencies competitive with HDR-based approaches and without DSBs. Prime editing does not require a donor DNA template, and its off-target rate in reported studies has been very low, partly because the reverse transcriptase step is templated by the pegRNA rather than by endogenous DNA repair machinery.

The limitation of early prime editing systems was efficiency: the absolute fraction of edited alleles per target in cells was often lower than what base editors or HDR approaches could achieve. The field has made rapid progress on this — improved pegRNA architectures (epegRNAs), engineered reverse transcriptases, and optimized prime editor protein variants have substantially closed this efficiency gap over 2021-2025.

**PM359: the first prime editing clinical result**

Chronic granulomatous disease is a rare inherited immunodeficiency (prevalence approximately 1 in 200,000) in which neutrophils — the white blood cells that engulf and kill bacteria and fungi — cannot produce the reactive oxygen species (ROS) needed to destroy ingested pathogens. The most common autosomal recessive form is caused by mutations in NCF1, the gene encoding the p47phox subunit of the NADPH oxidase enzyme complex. Without functional p47phox, the NADPH oxidase cannot assemble properly and fails to generate the respiratory burst. Patients suffer from recurrent, life-threatening infections with catalase-positive bacteria (Staphylococcus, Burkholderia) and fungi (Aspergillus), as well as inflammatory complications including colitis.

PM359, the therapy reported in the December 2025 NEJM paper, targets the most common NCF1 mutation: a GT dinucleotide deletion (delGT) that disrupts the reading frame. Prime editing is an essentially ideal tool for this correction because the delGT mutation requires a small insertion (restoring the two deleted bases) — a task that is impossible for base editors (which cannot insert nucleotides) but tractable for prime editing. The therapy is manufactured ex vivo: a patient's CD34+ hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) are collected by apheresis, prime-edited to restore the wild-type NCF1 sequence, and reinfused after myeloablative conditioning.

The first two patients both experienced robust and durable restoration of NADPH oxidase activity. Measured by the dihydrorhodamine (DHR) assay — which tests whether neutrophils can oxidize a fluorescent dye as a proxy for ROS production — Patient 1 showed 69% DHR-positive neutrophils by Day 30, and Patient 2 showed 83%. Both well exceed the approximately 20% threshold believed to be sufficient for clinical protection against the most serious infections (even patients with X-linked CGD, a more severe form, can experience significant clinical benefit at 10-20% DHR positivity if achieved through gene therapy). NADPH oxidase function was maintained through 6 months (Patient 1) and 4 months (Patient 2) of reported follow-up. Both patients experienced resolution of pre-existing CGD-associated colitis, a difficult-to-treat inflammatory complication that is itself a major source of morbidity. No adverse events were attributed to the prime editing itself.

**Base editing vs. prime editing: a practical comparison**

These two technologies occupy different niches. Base editors are simpler architecturally, have been in clinical development longer, and currently have more clinical programs (23 in trials as of 2025 vs. just the first few for prime editing). They are the right tool when the target mutation is a point substitution within the CBE or ABE scope (a C·G→T·A or A·T→G·C change). Prime editing is the right tool when the mutation requires a transversion, a small insertion, a small deletion, or a more complex sequence change — it covers the 38% of pathogenic variants that base editors cannot address, plus any variant addressable by base editing. Both share the fundamental advantage of avoiding DSBs and both can be delivered in vivo via LNPs (to liver and potentially other organs as delivery technology improves) or ex vivo by electroporation into hematopoietic stem cells.

A dimension neither tool has fully solved is delivery beyond the liver. VERVE-102 works because LNPs naturally accumulate in hepatocytes. Reaching the brain, heart, muscle, or lung with equivalent efficiency remains an active engineering challenge shared with all in vivo gene therapy platforms. For diseases that can be treated via bone marrow editing — blood disorders, certain immunodeficiencies — the ex vivo approach sidesteps this delivery problem but reintroduces the manufacturing complexity and myeloablative conditioning burden noted in yesterday's reading on Casgevy.

**Where the clinical pipeline stands**

As of mid-2026, base editing has the larger clinical footprint. Beyond VERVE-102, Beam Therapeutics' BEAM-101 — an ex vivo adenine base editor targeting the BCL11A enhancer in hematopoietic stem cells for sickle cell disease — has been administered to 26 patients in its Phase 1/2 BEACON trial. Updated data presented at the European Hematology Association Congress in June 2025 showed all treated patients achieving HbF levels exceeding 60% and HbS levels below 40% — outcomes comparable to Casgevy, but achieved through a more precise A→G edit rather than through the blunt disruption of the enhancer with NHEJ. The FDA has granted BEAM-101 a Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy (RMAT) designation, an accelerated regulatory pathway reserved for therapies addressing serious conditions with unmet medical need.

Prime editing's clinical portfolio is earlier-stage but expanding. Prime Medicine is advancing in vivo prime editing programs for Wilson's disease and alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency (AATD), with IND filings anticipated in 2026 and initial human data projected for 2027. ## Questions to think about

1. Base editing avoids double-strand breaks but still permanently alters genomic DNA in ways that cannot be reversed. When a mutation is well understood (like the PCSK9 variants that confer low LDL and long life), this irreversibility feels acceptable — you are mimicking a validated human phenotype. But for less-characterized targets, or targets where long-term consequences are unknown, how should the medical community set the bar for irreversible in vivo gene modification, compared to the reversible pharmacological alternatives (statins, PCSK9 antibodies) that already exist?

2. PM359 for CGD corrects exactly one specific mutation (delGT in NCF1). This precision is what prime editing makes possible — but CGD is caused by mutations in at least five different genes (CYBB, NCF1, NCF2, NCF4, CYBA) and NCF1 alone has multiple pathogenic variants. Does the specificity of prime editing make personalized therapies for ultra-rare or genetically heterogeneous diseases more or less tractable to develop and approve? How does this compare to gene-addition approaches (lentiviral vectors delivering a functional gene copy) that work regardless of the specific causative mutation?

3. Both VERVE-102 and BEAM-101 use the same conceptual strategy as Casgevy (targeting BCL11A enhancer via a different molecular mechanism, or targeting PCSK9 via liver-directed LNPs). If you were designing a base editing or prime editing program from scratch for a new disease, which disease features would make the disease a strong candidate — and which would make it a poor one? Consider: disease severity, genetic architecture, target cell accessibility, the existence of validated human loss-of-function variants, and unmet need relative to existing treatments.
