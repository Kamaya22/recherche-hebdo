# CRISPR and genome editing therapies — Day 5/7: Off-target effects and safety

## Context

Day 3 of this week introduced the first approved CRISPR medicines — Casgevy for sickle cell disease and β-thalassemia, and NTLA-2001 for hereditary transthyretin amyloidosis — and examined the remarkable efficacy data behind them. Today we turn to the question that has shadowed every discussion of CRISPR since 2012: what happens when the molecular scissors cut in the wrong place?

Off-target editing — the deposition of insertions, deletions, or other mutations at genomic sites other than the intended target — is the central safety concern for CRISPR therapeutics. The worry is not hypothetical. The same mechanism that makes CRISPR powerful, the ability of a guide RNA to direct Cas9 to a precise DNA sequence, also means that sufficiently similar sequences elsewhere in the genome can attract unintended cuts. In the worst case, an off-target cut near a tumour suppressor gene or a proto-oncogene could initiate cancer; a cluster of off-target mutations could compromise cellular function. In a therapeutic context — where the edited cells are returned to a patient who may live for decades — even a small probability of harm at scale matters.

The reassuring news, as we will see, is that the clinical data accumulated through 2025 have been more favorable than the most pessimistic early predictions. The more nuanced news is that our ability to detect and interpret off-target events is still maturing. A 2025 framework paper in *Nature Genetics* argues for moving from a binary "safe vs. unsafe" question to a quantitative benefit-risk analysis — a shift that reflects how much the field has learned, and how much remains genuinely uncertain.

## Today's readings (~23 min total)

**1. Specificity of CRISPR-Cas9 Editing in Exagamglogene Autotemcel** — Yen A, Zappala Z, Fine RS, Majarian TD, Sripakdeevong P, and Altshuler D (*New England Journal of Medicine*, 2024)
<https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMc2313119>
*Estimated reading time: 8 min — full text is a focused correspondence piece*

Published in *NEJM* in 2024, this correspondence by Vertex Pharmaceuticals' research team describes the complete off-target evaluation performed for exa-cel (Casgevy). It is the primary published account of how the first approved CRISPR therapy was tested for off-target editing: starting from in silico predictions and GUIDE-seq experiments in healthy donor hematopoietic stem cells, the team nominated 5,002 candidate off-target sites, then narrowed the focus to the 223 sites judged most likely to carry detectable editing, and applied high-coverage hybrid-capture sequencing to the actual drug product from clinical trial patients. The piece is short and data-dense, making it an unusually direct window into how off-target safety is operationalized under regulatory scrutiny. *NEJM* is the world's most-cited clinical journal; this is peer-reviewed correspondence from the drug's manufacturer — read it alongside the editorial context where possible.

**2. Chromothripsis as an on-target consequence of CRISPR–Cas9 genome editing** — Leibowitz ML, Zhang C-Z, Papathanasiou S et al. (*Nature Genetics*, 2021)
<https://www.nature.com/articles/s41588-021-00838-7>
*Estimated reading time: 10 min — abstract, figures 1-3, and discussion*

This 2021 landmark paper from the Pellman laboratory at Harvard / Dana-Farber Cancer Institute identified a category of genomic damage that standard CRISPR safety assays were missing: chromothripsis, a catastrophic form of chromosomal rearrangement in which a chromosome is shattered and the fragments are rejoined haphazardly. Leibowitz et al. showed that when Cas9 cuts its target, the severed chromosome can be sequestered in a micronucleus — a membrane-enclosed fragment outside the main nucleus — and undergo extensive shattering there. They reported rates of chromothripsis ranging from 0% to 45% depending on the cell type and experimental condition. Critically, these events were frequently invisible to the short PCR amplicon genotyping approaches that had been standard in the field. Published in *Nature Genetics*, the top-tier journal for human genetics, this paper prompted a reexamination of safety monitoring protocols across academic and clinical CRISPR programs.

**3. Measurement and clinical interpretation of CRISPR off-targets** — Stewart AA, Ahrens-Nicklas R, Tsai SQ, Musunuru K et al. (*Nature Genetics*, 2025)
<https://www.nature.com/articles/s41588-025-02428-3>
*Estimated reading time: 7 min — full Perspective piece*

Published online in *Nature Genetics* on 24 November 2025 (volume 58, pages 20–27; DOI: 10.1038/s41588-025-02428-3), this Perspective from leading genome-editing and clinical researchers — including Shengdar Tsai (one of the principal architects of GUIDE-seq) and Kiran Musunuru (a pioneer of base editing in cardiovascular disease) — provides the most current authoritative framework for thinking about off-target risk in clinical context. It argues that the right question is not "are there off-target edits?" (there almost always are some detectable signal) but "what is the probability that a detected or undetected off-target event causes clinically meaningful harm, and how does that risk compare to the disease being treated?" The piece reviews detection methods, their sensitivity limits, what the existing clinical data do and do not tell us, and what regulatory and scientific standards are needed going forward. Its publication in a peer-reviewed journal of this caliber, authored by scientists directly responsible for both the methods and the therapies, makes it essential reading for anyone trying to calibrate the real state of CRISPR safety science.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

**The taxonomy of off-target events**

To appreciate the challenge of off-target safety, it helps to categorize the kinds of genomic changes that can occur. The simplest are **small indels** (insertions or deletions of 1–50 base pairs) at sites other than the intended target, arising when NHEJ repair seals an off-target cut imprecisely. These are the easiest to detect and the ones that most current safety assays are calibrated to find. More serious are **large deletions** at the on-target or off-target site — kilobase-to-megabase scale losses of genomic sequence that delete entire genes. Then there are **chromosomal translocations**, in which the repair machinery mistakenly joins two separate chromosomal breaks to each other, creating an abnormal chromosome that may fuse parts of two different genes. And finally, at the most extreme end, there is **chromothripsis** — the catastrophic shattering and disordered reassembly of a chromosome, typically after it has been sequestered in a micronucleus following a Cas9-induced break.

The clinical significance of these events differs. A small indel in a non-coding, non-regulatory stretch of DNA is almost certainly harmless. A translocation that places a proto-oncogene under the control of a strong promoter could initiate a malignancy. The research field has made substantial progress in detecting small indels and translocations; it is only beginning to develop routine assays for the full spectrum of structural rearrangements, including chromothripsis.

**The detection toolkit: from in silico to single-cell whole genome sequencing**

The first step in evaluating off-target risk is always computational. In silico tools (Cas-OFFinder, CRISPRscan, and more recently machine-learning approaches) predict candidate off-target sites by identifying genomic sequences with homology to the guide RNA, allowing up to a specified number of mismatches. These tools are fast and inexpensive — any therapeutic program now routinely screens billions of genomic positions before a guide RNA is selected for clinical use. Their limitation is a high false-positive rate: many predicted sites are never actually cut in living cells, and some sites that are cut bear no obvious sequence similarity to the guide (so-called "atypical" off-target sites that emerged as a concern in 2023 research).

Empirical cell-based methods are the next tier. **GUIDE-seq** (Genome-wide Unbiased Identification of DSBs Evaluated by Sequencing) inserts a short double-stranded oligonucleotide tag at every double-strand break and then reads out the genomic locations of those insertions by next-generation sequencing. GUIDE-seq became the dominant empirical tool for ex vivo editing programs precisely because it measures cutting in relevant human cells — including the hematopoietic stem cells used in Casgevy manufacturing — at a sensitivity of roughly 0.1%. It was used to identify the 223 most-likely off-target sites for exa-cel. A related method, **DISCOVER-seq**, works by chromatin immunoprecipitation of DNA repair factors (specifically MRE11, which binds to DNA break ends) followed by sequencing. Its key advantage is that it can be applied in living tissues in animal models, opening a path toward eventual in vivo monitoring in patients.

Biochemical in vitro methods — **CIRCLE-seq**, **SITE-seq**, and **Digenome-seq** — cut purified genomic DNA with Cas9-guide RNA complexes in a test tube and sequence the products. Their sensitivity is extremely high, but because they test naked genomic DNA rather than intact cells, they identify many sites that are accessible to Cas9 biochemically but are actually protected in living cells by chromatin structure. They are most useful for comprehensive nomination of potential sites; they are less predictive of which sites will actually be cut at meaningful rates in the specific cell type of interest.

At the highest resolution, **whole-genome sequencing (WGS) of single-cell-derived clones** involves expanding individual edited cells into clonal populations and then sequencing the full genome (~3 billion base pairs) of each clone. This is the only method that can detect all categories of off-target change — small indels, large deletions, structural variants, and chromothripsis — without prior knowledge of where to look. A 2020 study performed WGS on 24 Cas9-edited HSPC clones targeting two genomic loci and found no significant increase in the total mutational burden compared with unedited controls; no indels above background were detected at 623 predicted off-target sites. This was a landmark reassurance — but the study examined two guide RNAs in one cell type, and clonal WGS at the depth needed to detect rare large structural variants is expensive and not yet scalable to clinical manufacturing.

**What the Casgevy data actually show**

The off-target evaluation summarized by Yen et al. in *NEJM* 2024 is the most detailed publicly available safety dataset for a licensed CRISPR medicine. The guide RNA targeting the BCL11A erythroid enhancer was deliberately selected, in part, because the targeted sequence has unusually low homology to the rest of the human genome — reducing the field of plausible off-target sites from the outset. Combined in silico and GUIDE-seq screening across HSPCs from multiple healthy donors produced an initial list of 5,002 candidate sites; of these, 223 were judged to have the highest likelihood of actual editing and were tested by high-coverage hybrid-capture next-generation sequencing in the manufactured drug product from clinical trial patients. None were detected above the assay's limit of detection.

These results are genuinely reassuring but require careful interpretation. The assay was highly sensitive for small indels at nominated sites — the sites predicted by sequence homology and GUIDE-seq. It was not designed to detect structural variants, chromothripsis, or off-target effects at sites not nominated by the standard pipeline. The FDA, recognizing this limitation, required Vertex to conduct post-marketing studies including long-term malignancy surveillance and, importantly, updated the Casgevy prescribing information in August 2025 with new language about off-target genome editing risk. This is not an indication that harm has been detected; it is the regulatory apparatus correctly acknowledging that the long-term cancer latency period — typically 10–20 years for radiation-induced or chemotherapy-induced malignancies — far exceeds the current follow-up available in any CRISPR clinical trial.

**Chromothripsis: the event the field was not looking for**

The Leibowitz et al. 2021 *Nature Genetics* paper deserves particular attention because it identified a mechanism that was systematically missed by the then-standard safety toolkit. When Cas9 creates a double-strand break, the severed chromosome end must be captured by repair machinery. Occasionally — especially if the break occurs late in the cell cycle or near the chromosome end — the fragment instead becomes encased in a micronucleus, a small membrane sac separate from the main nucleus. Micronuclei are inherently unstable: they have defective nuclear envelope assembly, they mis-replicate, and they can pulverize the DNA they contain. The resulting fragments are then irregularly reintegrated into the genome, producing a chromothripsis signature — tens to hundreds of structural rearrangements concentrated on a single chromosome.

Leibowitz et al. observed chromothripsis at rates ranging from 0% to 45% across different human and mouse cell lines and embryos, with the highest rates in cells that are hypersensitive to DNA damage. Importantly, these events were not detected by the short PCR amplicons that were standard in the field; they required chromosome-scale copy number analysis (SNP arrays or WGS) to see. In the 2021 *CRISPR Journal* commentary responding to this paper, Mack and Russell urged the field to adopt "proceed with caution" — not because chromothripsis was proven to occur at therapeutic rates in HSPCs specifically, but because the detection gap was real and needed to close.

Subsequent work has been partially, but not fully, reassuring. WGS of CRISPR-edited HSPC clones has not found elevated rates of chromothripsis in those specific cells. But HSPCs are a relatively radiation-sensitive population, and the manufacturing process for Casgevy involves multiple perturbations — mobilization, apheresis, electroporation, culture — that could themselves generate DNA damage and affect the rate of post-cut chromosomal instability. The honest position is that we do not yet have WGS-based clonal analysis at the scale and depth needed to rule out chromothripsis at rates that would be clinically meaningful across thousands of treated patients.

**A benefit-risk framework for clinical interpretation**

The 2025 *Nature Genetics* Perspective by Stewart, Tsai, Musunuru and colleagues argues that the field must move beyond asking "are there off-target edits?" to asking "what is the probability that a given off-target edit causes harm, and how does that probability compare to the harm from the untreated disease?"

This reframing matters practically. Sickle cell disease untreated causes a median reduction in life expectancy of roughly two decades, with a childhood characterized by frequent painful crises, organ damage, and stroke risk. Against this baseline, even a residual uncertainty about rare off-target events tilts the risk-benefit ratio clearly toward treatment. The calculus looks very different for a less severe condition, or for prophylactic use in healthy individuals. The framework the authors propose involves: (1) disease severity assessment, (2) the expected number of edits and probability each off-target edit hits a cancer-relevant locus, (3) the background cancer rate in the patient population (which may already be elevated by their disease, prior treatments, or age), and (4) the sensitivity of monitoring tools.

This benefit-risk framing is now embedded in regulatory thinking at both the FDA and the EMA. Post-marketing commitments for Casgevy include 15 years of long-term follow-up on treated patients, with specific monitoring for haematological malignancies. No therapy in the current CRISPR pipeline has been near markets long enough for that data to mature.

**Mitigation: engineering precision into the tool**

The field has not waited passively for better assays. Since 2016, protein engineers have developed **high-fidelity Cas9 variants** — SpCas9-HF1, eSpCas9, HypaCas9, evoCas9 — that retain near-wild-type on-target activity while reducing off-target cutting by one to three orders of magnitude. These variants work by weakening the protein's interaction with non-cognate DNA: when there is a mismatch between guide RNA and DNA, the energy cost of the mismatch more reliably aborts binding before cutting. They do not eliminate off-target activity, but they push the profile substantially toward specificity.

Delivery strategy also matters. Because Cas9 protein persists longer when delivered as a DNA plasmid or viral vector (which drives continued protein production), off-target probability accumulates over time. RNP (ribonucleoprotein) electroporation — delivering pre-assembled Cas9 protein and guide RNA directly — gives a transient peak of activity followed by rapid protein turnover. This is the delivery method used for Casgevy and most current ex vivo therapeutic programs, and it is believed to account for some of the favorable off-target profiles observed in HSPCs relative to earlier plasmid-based editing experiments.

Truncated guide RNAs (17–18 nucleotides instead of the usual 20) increase specificity at some loci by reducing the tolerance for mismatches. Chemically modified guide RNAs can also affect specificity. And anti-CRISPR proteins — viral proteins that naturally inhibit CRISPR systems in bacteria — are being explored as off-switches that could terminate Cas9 activity at a defined moment after a therapeutic edit, limiting the window during which off-target cuts can accumulate.

**The immunogenicity dimension**

Off-target genomic events are not the only safety question. CRISPR components are foreign proteins derived from bacteria, and the immune system of most humans has encountered the source organisms. Studies have found pre-existing antibodies against SpCas9 (the most widely used Cas9, derived from *Staphylococcus pyogenes*) in 58–79% of healthy adult donors, and T cells recognizing Cas9 peptides have also been detected. For ex vivo therapies like Casgevy, where the Cas9 protein is used only in the manufacturing process and is not present in the product infused into the patient, pre-existing immunity is less of a clinical concern (though Cas9 sequences could theoretically be presented to T cells by edited cells in some scenarios). For in vivo delivery approaches — particularly LNP-delivered Cas9 mRNA for NTLA-2001 or AAV-delivered Cas9 for ocular therapies — the immune response to the Cas9 protein itself, and to the delivery vehicle, adds a layer of safety complexity that does not apply to ex vivo programs.

**Where the field stands**

To summarize the current state of CRISPR off-target safety evidence fairly: for the specific molecule (exa-cel), the specific target cell (HSPCs), the specific on-target locus (BCL11A enhancer), and the specific detection methods applied (GUIDE-seq + hybrid-capture), no off-target editing above detection thresholds has been observed in patients. This is a meaningful result. The detection methods used were not designed to find all categories of off-target events — large structural variants and chromothripsis remain inadequately quantified in clinically relevant conditions. No malignancy attributable to CRISPR editing has been reported. Long-term surveillance is ongoing and required.

For in vivo delivery programs (NTLA-2001, EDIT-101, CTX310), the off-target safety dataset is smaller and the detection challenge harder — cells cannot be sampled clonally before reinfusion. The FDA's position, and the field's emerging consensus, is that benefit-risk analysis must be disease-specific, detection must go beyond small indels at predicted sites, and 15+ year follow-up is necessary before the safety profile of any CRISPR therapy can be considered definitively established.

## Questions to think about

1. The detection assays applied to Casgevy were optimized for finding small insertions and deletions at predicted sites. The Leibowitz 2021 paper showed that chromothripsis — a far more catastrophic event — is often invisible to such assays. At what point should a therapy be considered adequately characterized for safety, if the most serious potential harms are also the hardest to detect, and if cancer latency may be longer than any clinical trial timeline?

2. Pre-existing immunity to SpCas9 protein exists in the majority of adults. For in vivo CRISPR delivery (where Cas9 is injected into the body), this pre-existing immunity could reduce efficacy, cause inflammation, or prevent re-administration. Does the widespread use of Cas9-based therapies risk creating a population that cannot be re-treated with CRISPR tools if a first attempt partially fails? How does this affect the design of in vivo programs?

3. High-fidelity Cas9 variants dramatically reduce off-target editing but may also reduce on-target editing efficiency at some target sites, which would reduce therapeutic benefit. Should regulatory agencies require the use of high-fidelity variants for all therapeutic applications regardless of this trade-off, or should guide-RNA-specific data on on-target efficiency be part of the safety discussion?
