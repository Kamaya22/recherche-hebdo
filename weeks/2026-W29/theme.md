# The behavioral economics of poverty — how scarcity shapes decision-making

## Why it matters

Classical economics assumed that rational agents make optimal choices regardless of their circumstances — poverty was a resource problem, not a decision-making problem. Over the past two decades, behavioral economists and cognitive psychologists have overturned that picture: the lived experience of scarcity actively reshapes how people think, what they notice, and what they discount. Research published in *Science* and other leading journals shows that financial worry consumes cognitive bandwidth, narrows attention to immediate pressures, and triggers stress responses that systematically raise risk aversion and time discounting. This creates a feedback loop — poverty impairs the very mental faculties needed to plan, save, and invest one's way out of poverty. Understanding these mechanisms does not excuse structural inequality; it makes visible how the psychological burden of scarcity compounds material disadvantage, and it has direct implications for policy design, from welfare administration to cash-transfer programs.

## Tentative 7-day outline

- **Day 1 — Panorama**: What behavioral economics is, why classical models fail the poor, and the scarcity framework as an integrated account.
- **Day 2 — Tunneling and attention**: How scarcity narrows focus — the benefits (efficiency under pressure) and the costs (neglect of everything else).
- **Day 3 — Mental bandwidth and cognitive performance**: The experimental and field evidence that financial worry drains working memory, executive control, and fluid intelligence.
- **Day 4 — Stress, cortisol, and risk preferences**: The physiological pathway — how poverty-induced stress changes risk aversion, time discounting, and habitual vs. goal-directed behavior.
- **Day 5 — Present bias, debt traps, and savings**: Why poor individuals overborrow and undersave — and whether the fault lies in preferences, constraints, or the cognitive tax of scarcity.
- **Day 6 — Interventions and their limits**: Cash transfers, nudges, aspirations programs, and slack-creating designs — what works, what doesn't, and the replication controversies.
- **Day 7 — Synthesis**: What is settled vs. open, where the structural and psychological explanations are complementary, and the big open questions for research and policy.
