# The behavioral economics of poverty — Day 2/7: Tunneling and the Geometry of Attention

## Context (2–3 paragraphs)

Yesterday's reading established the core claim of scarcity theory: poverty is not merely a problem of insufficient resources but a cognitive condition. Financial worry captures mental bandwidth, elevates stress hormones, and reshapes decision-making in ways that would impair anyone placed in that situation. The foundational 2012 *Science* paper by Shah, Mullainathan, and Shafir introduced the concept of "tunneling" — the mechanism by which scarcity produces this cognitive state — and showed it could be induced in the laboratory by simply giving participants small resource budgets. Today we go inside the tunnel.

Tunneling is a specific claim about attention. When resources are tight, the mind prioritizes the scarce resource above all else, focusing processing capacity on managing it. This is not simply being preoccupied with a problem — it is an automatic, pre-reflective shift in what the mind treats as foreground and background. The shift has two sides, and understanding both is essential to evaluating scarcity theory. On one side is what Mullainathan and Shafir call the "focus dividend": attention to the immediate resource problem sharpens, engagement deepens, and trade-off thinking kicks in automatically. On the other side is attentional neglect: everything outside the tunnel fades. The urgent crowds out the non-urgent; the salient crowds out the abstract. A person tunneled on covering this month's rent may genuinely fail to notice — not merely deprioritize — a benefits enrollment window, a medication refill, or a discount that would save them money.

Today's readings examine this two-sided structure from three complementary angles: a laboratory investigation of the focus dividend and trade-off thinking; a large-panel study testing scarcity's effects on vigilance; and a field study measuring real-world attentional failures among low-income parents under financial stress. Together they move the concept of tunneling from analogy to measurable, falsifiable construct.

## Today's readings (2–3 items, ~20 min total)

**1. "Scarcity Frames Value"** — Shah, A.K., Shafir, E., & Mullainathan, S., *Psychological Science*, vol. 26, no. 4 (2015), pp. 402–412 — <https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0956797614563958> — ~8 min — This peer-reviewed laboratory study examines the positive face of tunneling: how scarcity changes the *frame* through which people evaluate options. When resources are scarce, people automatically think in terms of trade-offs — "if I buy this, what do I forgo?" — producing more consistent preferences that are less susceptible to normatively irrelevant contextual cues (such as whether a price is listed against a low or high anchor). The paper documents this "trade-off thinking" across multiple experiments involving time and money, and argues it represents a genuine cognitive benefit of scarcity. Anuj K. Shah is an economist at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business; Eldar Shafir is a psychologist at Princeton; Sendhil Mullainathan is an economist at Harvard. Published in *Psychological Science*, the flagship journal of the Association for Psychological Science, this paper is the most direct experimental treatment of the focus dividend.

**2. "Poverty and Limited Attention"** — Schmitt, S., & Schlatterer, M., *Economics and Human Biology*, vol. 41 (2021), 100987 — <https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1570677X21000113> — ~8 min (focus on abstract, introduction, and results sections) — This paper uses data from the German Socio-Economic Panel Study (SOEP-IS) to test scarcity theory's predictions about attention in a real-world setting outside the laboratory. The authors examine two dimensions of attention: vigilance to unexpected environmental events (inattentional blindness paradigm) and performance on a primary task requiring concentration. Their striking main finding is that individuals under financial strain are *more* likely to detect unexpected events than their wealthier counterparts — and that this heightened vigilance reverses after payday, when the same individuals are less financially pressured. *Economics and Human Biology* is a peer-reviewed journal of the Elsevier group specializing in the intersection of economics, health, and human development; the SOEP-IS is a major nationally representative longitudinal study run by the German Institute for Economic Research (DIW Berlin).

**3. "Scarcity and Inattention"** — Kalil, A., Mayer, S., & Shah, R., *Journal of Behavioral Economics for Policy*, vol. 7, no. 1 (2023), pp. 35–42 — <https://bfi.uchicago.edu/working-paper/scarcity-and-inattention/> — ~5 min — A field study that takes tunneling into an everyday setting: parental engagement with preschool information. The authors surveyed 345 low-income parents and the directors of their children's preschools shortly after the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, comparing what schools said they sent with what parents recalled receiving. Financial scarcity (self-reported inability to make ends meet) was significantly and independently associated with missing school communications. The effect held alongside a parallel finding for social scarcity (loneliness). Ariel Kalil and Susan Mayer are researchers at the University of Chicago's Harris School of Public Policy; the working paper is published by the Becker Friedman Institute for Economics at the University of Chicago. This paper provides one of the most concrete and policy-relevant field demonstrations of attentional neglect under scarcity.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### What tunneling actually is

The word "tunneling" captures a specific visual metaphor: a person looking through a narrow tube sees what is directly in front of them with great clarity, while everything in the peripheral field disappears. Applied to cognition, it describes a state in which the mind allocates virtually all processing resources to managing the scarcity problem — whatever the resource at stake — while information and concerns that lie outside that focal frame receive little or no encoding. This is not the same as being distracted. A distracted person's attention is split and degraded across many things. A tunneled person's attention is focused, sharply and usefully, on one thing — and it is precisely this focus that generates the neglect.

Mullainathan and Shafir were careful to frame tunneling as an *automatic* response to perceived scarcity, not a deliberate choice. The person experiencing financial pressure does not decide to ignore their medical appointment; the appointment simply fails to register as urgent against the background noise of the immediate crisis. Laboratory experiments verified this by creating scarcity in controlled settings — game budgets, time limits, shot allocations in Angry Birds-style tasks — and measuring both performance on the resource-related task and behavior on orthogonal tasks. Participants under scarcity consistently showed sharper, more engaged performance on the primary resource task and more mistakes on secondary obligations.

### The focus dividend: trade-off thinking and consistent valuation

Shah, Shafir, and Mullainathan's 2015 *Psychological Science* paper provides the clearest experimental treatment of the benefit side of tunneling. When resources are constrained, people spontaneously engage in opportunity-cost reasoning: spending on X means giving up Y, and this trade-off is held in mind as a frame through which all choices are evaluated. The practical consequence is that people under scarcity are *less* susceptible to classic contextual biases. Their valuation of the same dollar or the same unit of time remains stable across different framing conditions that reliably manipulate the choices of people who are not under resource pressure.

This is, in one sense, genuinely rational behavior. The canonical result in behavioral economics is that people are inconsistent in their valuations, easily swayed by anchors, framing effects, and arbitrary reference points. Scarcity appears to suppress some of these irrationalities: when every decision feels like a real trade-off with real consequences, people become sharper judges of value. A low-income shopper comparing unit prices at the grocery store, or a cash-strapped student weighing the immediate cost of a textbook against the semester's budget, is exercising a form of economic reasoning that people with more slack resources often forgo. This is the focus dividend — a genuine cognitive benefit that comes for free with the territory of scarcity.

### The cost: neglect and the payday reversal

The same attentional geometry that sharpens the tunnel also creates the dark rim around it. Schmitt and Schlatterer's (2021) analysis of the German panel data finds a striking "payday effect": before payday, when households are under financial pressure, people in the study show heightened detection of unexpected events in an inattentional blindness task — they are, in a sense, more on alert, more vigilant to changes in their environment. This is consistent with the scarcity-induced vigilance predicted by tunneling: when resources are scarce, the mind scans the environment more actively for information that might be resource-relevant. But after payday — when the same individuals are, at least briefly, financially comfortable — this vigilance diminishes. They become, by standard measures, more inattentionally blind.

This finding is initially counterintuitive: shouldn't financial stress *reduce* cognitive capacity? The result reconciles with scarcity theory when we distinguish between low-level environmental vigilance (detecting something unexpected appears) and higher-order cognitive operations (planning ahead, executing intentions set in the past, integrating multiple goals). Scarcity may heighten the former while depleting the latter. The person under financial stress notices more but plans less — they are highly responsive to what is happening right now while losing track of what they committed to do next week. The inattentional blindness result thus captures the vigilance side of tunneling; the neglect of deadlines, appointments, and long-term intentions captures its costs.

### Real-world neglect: missing information that matters

Kalil, Mayer, and Shah's (2023) field study closes the gap between laboratory paradigms and the lived experience of scarcity. By comparing what preschool directors said they communicated to parents with what parents actually recalled, the authors obtained an objective measure of information gaps that is independent of either party's self-presentation. Parents under financial stress — those who reported not having enough money to make ends meet — missed more school communications than parents in more comfortable financial situations, even after accounting for other relevant differences.

This is tunneling in action at the community level. A parent overwhelmed by the immediate demands of financial survival — negotiating bills, managing irregular income, responding to housing or food insecurity — has, in effect, a narrowed tunnel. The preschool newsletter, the deadline for a field trip permission slip, the reminder about a vaccination — none of these have the urgency of the financial crisis in the foreground, so they fail to be encoded and acted upon. Importantly, this is not indifference; there is no reason to think low-income parents care less about their children's preschool education. It is the geometry of attention under pressure.

The study also found an independent effect of *social* scarcity (loneliness): parents who reported high levels of loneliness also missed more school communications. This suggests the attention-narrowing mechanism may generalize to other forms of perceived inadequacy, not just financial shortage — a parallel that scarcity theory has long predicted but which rarely receives direct empirical test.

### Overborrowing: where the tunnel meets finance

The behavioral consequence of tunneling that has attracted the most attention from economists is overborrowing. In the canonical laboratory experiments by Shah, Mullainathan, and Shafir (2012), participants under resource scarcity in game formats consistently borrowed from future rounds at high interest rates — decisions that observers can clearly see are against the participants' own long-term interests, but which seem locally rational when you are tunneled on the current round. The cost of the loan is in the future; the benefit (surviving the current round) is immediate; and the tunnel makes the future cost feel abstract and distant even when the entire game spans only minutes.

The real-world analog is stark. Payday loans, high-interest credit cards, rent-to-own furniture, and buy-now-pay-later schemes all share a common structure: they shift the cost of consumption to a future moment and charge handsomely for the privilege. From outside the tunnel, these are obviously expensive ways to borrow. From inside the tunnel — when the immediate need (food, utilities, covering a car repair that keeps you employed) is dominating cognitive resources — the future cost of the loan receives far less attention than it deserves. Scarcity theory thus provides a mechanism for what was previously described either as irrationality or as evidence of unusual time preferences among the poor. Neither characterization is needed: the same person with the same underlying preferences may make systematically different borrowing decisions depending on whether their current financial situation is creating a tunnel.

### What tunneling is not

It is worth being precise about what the tunneling account does *not* claim, because conflating it with related ideas creates confusion. Tunneling is not the same as reduced general intelligence. The Mani et al. (2013) findings from Monday, which showed that financial worry reduces performance on cognitive tests by amounts comparable to a night of lost sleep, are about bandwidth depletion — a different mechanism, explored in Thursday's reading. Tunneling describes how attention is *allocated*; bandwidth describes how much attention is *available*. A person under scarcity may be tunneling (allocating intensely to the immediate problem) and also experiencing bandwidth depletion (having less total cognitive capacity available because of persistent worry). Both effects can be present simultaneously and reinforce each other.

Tunneling is also not fatalism. The research program that documents it has produced, alongside the diagnosis, a set of design insights: administrative processes that demand attention at moments of peak financial stress predictably fail; deadlines that fall on rent-payment days will not be met by many low-income households; programs that penalize a single missed enrollment step will systematically exclude the people they are designed to serve. Redesigning these systems to account for the geometry of attention under scarcity is, in principle, possible — and Day 6 of this week will examine what the intervention evidence shows.

## Questions to think about

1. The focus dividend suggests that scarcity makes people *better* at certain economic judgments — more consistent, more trade-off aware. If this is true, why don't the poor systematically outperform the wealthy in financial decision tasks? What other factors might offset or overwhelm the focus dividend in real-world settings?

2. Schmitt and Schlatterer found that financial stress increases vigilance to unexpected events, but the same mechanism presumably underlies attentional neglect of non-urgent planned actions. What is the difference, at a cognitive level, between detecting an unexpected event and executing a planned future intention? Why might scarcity improve the former while harming the latter?

3. Kalil, Mayer, and Shah found that both financial scarcity and social scarcity (loneliness) are independently associated with missing school information. If the attentional mechanism generalizes beyond financial stress, what are the implications for how social programs and institutions communicate with people who are isolated or lonely, not just financially constrained?
