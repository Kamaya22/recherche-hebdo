# The behavioral economics of poverty — Day 6/7: Interventions and Their Limits

## Context (2–3 paragraphs)

The first five days of this week have assembled a detailed account of how poverty reshapes cognition and behavior. Day 1 introduced the scarcity framework and the Haushofer–Fehr feedback loop in which poverty generates chronic stress that in turn worsens economic decisions. Day 2 traced tunneling — the attentional geometry that sharpens focus on the immediate crisis while producing systematic neglect of everything outside it. Day 4 went inside the biology: chronic cortisol elevation impairs prefrontal cortex function, shifts decision-making from flexible goal-directed action toward rigid habitual responses, and alters risk preferences. Day 5 examined overborrowing and undersaving through the lens of present bias, showing that the problem is best understood as a combination of cognitive preference factors, material constraints, and the cognitive tax imposed by scarcity itself. Throughout, a single question has been building: if we understand all this, what can we actually do?

Today's reading turns to interventions. The behavioral economics of poverty has generated not just a diagnostic account but a policy agenda — one that has produced genuine successes, genuine failures, and ongoing controversy about which findings are robust enough to build policy on. Three classes of intervention are most important in this literature. The first is direct cash transfers: give poor households money, and see whether the material and psychological relief translates into better outcomes. The second is administrative design and nudges: if cognitive overload and attentional neglect cause people to miss benefits they are entitled to, simplifying the process should help. The third is more ambitious — interventions targeting aspirations, expectations, and mental health directly. Today's readings assess the evidence for each of these in turn, with particular attention to effect sizes and to where the evidence is weaker or contested.

One honest theme of today's reading is that the interventions literature has not been immune to the replication crisis that has swept social science since roughly 2010. Some of the specific cognitive-bandwidth findings that motivated the most ambitious interventions have replicated inconsistently. The lesson is not that the behavioral economics of poverty was wrong but that the distinction between robust findings (present bias is real; tunneling effects on attentional neglect are consistently demonstrated; cash transfers improve mental health) and fragile ones (the specific IQ-point equivalents from the Mani et al. bandwidth studies) matters enormously for which policies we invest in. The reading closes with a synthesis that tries to hold both the genuine insights and the honest uncertainties together.

## Today's readings (2–3 items, ~20 min total)

**1. "The Short-Term Impact of Unconditional Cash Transfers to the Poor: Experimental Evidence from Kenya"** — Haushofer, J. & Shapiro, J., *The Quarterly Journal of Economics*, vol. 131, no. 4 (2016), pp. 1973–2042 — <https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/131/4/1973/2468874> — ~9 min — This large randomized controlled trial assessed the effects of unconditional cash transfers from the NGO GiveDirectly on roughly 1,500 poor households in rural Kenya. Transfers were deliberately large relative to household income, explicitly unconditional, and varied in timing (lump-sum vs. monthly installments) and in recipient gender (wife vs. husband). The study measured economic outcomes (consumption, assets, food security, livestock) and, critically for this week's themes, a battery of psychological wellbeing measures including a standard depression screen, a stress index, and salivary cortisol. The *Quarterly Journal of Economics* is consistently ranked among the top two or three journals in economics; this paper has become the canonical reference for the psychological as well as economic effects of cash transfers, and its finding that cortisol patterns differed across transfer formats is directly relevant to the stress-biology account from Day 4.

**2. "Psychological Frictions and the Incomplete Take-Up of Social Benefits: Evidence from an IRS Field Experiment"** — Bhargava, S. & Manoli, D., *American Economic Review*, vol. 105, no. 11 (2015), pp. 3489–3529 — <https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20121493> — ~7 min (focus on the introduction and the results comparing control vs. simplified mailing conditions) — This is perhaps the cleanest demonstration in the literature that administrative complexity — not recipient preferences or character — is a major barrier to benefits take-up. Working with the IRS, Bhargava and Manoli sent experimental mailings to 35,050 low-income tax filers who had already received a notice informing them they were eligible for the Earned Income Tax Credit but had not claimed it, leaving $26 million in unclaimed benefits. Different mailing conditions varied the visual clarity, complexity, and information content of the notice. The *American Economic Review* is the most-cited economics journal and the flagship of the American Economic Association; this experiment is one of the strongest causal demonstrations of how cognitive friction in bureaucratic design suppresses access to benefits that people are entitled to and would willingly claim if the process were simpler.

**3. "Poverty, Depression, and Anxiety: Causal Evidence and Mechanisms"** — Ridley, M., Rao, G., Schilbach, F. & Patel, V.H., *Science*, vol. 370, no. 6522 (2020), eaay0214 — <https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aay0214> — ~8 min — A landmark invited review in *Science* synthesizing the causal evidence for the bidirectional relationship between poverty and common mental illness (depression and anxiety). The authors assess whether poverty causes mental illness, whether mental illness causes poverty, and what the evidence shows about interventions running in both directions: do anti-poverty programs (including cash transfers) improve mental health, and do psychological or mental health interventions generate measurable economic gains? The review draws on randomized experiments, natural experiments, and structural estimation approaches from multiple low-, middle-, and high-income country settings. Gautam Rao (Harvard) and Frank Schilbach (MIT) are leading behavioral development economists; Vikram Patel (Harvard Medical School) is one of the world's leading global mental health researchers. The publication in *Science* signals the review's significance for a broad scientific audience beyond economics.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### Cash transfers: material relief and psychological repair

The Haushofer and Shapiro (2016) study is not merely an economic experiment about consumption; it is a test of the Haushofer–Fehr feedback loop from Day 1. If poverty causes chronic stress which causes worse decisions, then eliminating the poverty — or at least sharply reducing financial pressure through a large unconditional transfer — should relax the stress component of the loop and, through it, improve both psychological and economic outcomes. That is exactly what the study finds.

Household monthly consumption rose from approximately $158 PPP to $193 PPP nine months after transfers began. Asset holdings (durable goods, livestock) increased substantially. These economic effects are consistent with a straightforward income effect. What is more striking is the psychological profile: recipients scored 0.26 standard deviations higher on a composite index of psychological wellbeing, driven by increases in happiness and life satisfaction and decreases in measured stress, worry, and depression symptoms. This is a meaningful effect size for a psychological outcome in a field experiment. Importantly, the psychological gains were not simply a side effect of knowing one had received money; the income improvements and the wellbeing improvements partially tracked each other across treatment arms in ways consistent with the stress-reduction pathway.

The cortisol data introduced an important nuance. There was no overall effect of cash transfers on salivary cortisol — the stress hormone that Day 4 showed is elevated under chronic poverty. But a breakdown by transfer format revealed that recipients of monthly installment transfers had significantly higher cortisol than recipients of lump-sum transfers of the same total amount. The authors interpret this finding cautiously: monthly recipients may have faced more difficulty managing the transfers across periods, or may have experienced more uncertainty about the continuation of income, generating its own stress. The finding is a reminder that implementation details — the design of how money arrives — matter for the full psychological chain, not merely the economic one.

### Administrative simplicity as an intervention

Bhargava and Manoli's IRS experiment reveals something important about where many behavioral interventions have the most leverage: not in changing poor people's preferences or behavior, but in removing friction that prevents them from accessing what they are already entitled to. The population studied — EITC-eligible filers who had failed to claim their credit despite an initial IRS notice — were not disengaged. They had filed taxes. They had received notification. The barrier was cognitive: the worksheet required to claim the credit was complex, demanded information that was confusing, and arrived in a format that was visually overwhelming.

The results were stark. The control mailing (a standard restatement of the original notice) raised take-up from baseline to 14%. Simplified mailings — either a visually redesigned notice or a shorter worksheet from which eligibility screens that every recipient already satisfied were removed — raised take-up to 23%. The headline number is a 64% relative increase in claiming unclaimed entitlements, achieved purely through document design, with no change in eligibility rules, no financial incentive, and no motivational content. Versions of the mailing that included the dollar amount of the credit the recipient stood to receive were also effective, consistent with the salience principle: people respond to concrete, immediate information more strongly than to abstract eligibility language. Notably, attempts to reduce perceived stigma associated with claiming were ineffective — recipients were not held back by embarrassment about claiming; they were held back by the complexity of doing so.

This result connects directly to Day 2's account of tunneling and attentional neglect. A household under financial stress, tunneled on immediate crises, will not allocate bandwidth to navigating a confusing multi-step form for a benefit whose value is stated in eligibility language rather than dollars. Making the form shorter, clearer, and concrete makes it accessible to a tunneled mind in a way that the original process was not. The intervention is not a nudge in the patronizing sense of manipulating choices; it is the removal of an unnecessary cognitive barrier to a choice the recipient has already, in principle, made.

### The bidirectional poverty–mental-health loop and the evidence for treatment

Ridley, Rao, Schilbach, and Patel's 2020 *Science* review consolidates the causal evidence on the relationship between poverty and mental illness in a way that this week's scarcity-framework readings have only gestured toward. Within a given setting, people with the lowest incomes are consistently 1.5 to 3 times more likely to experience depression or anxiety than those with higher incomes. This gradient is cross-nationally robust, but the question of causation — does poverty cause depression, or do depressed people become poor, or both? — requires careful causal identification that correlational data cannot provide.

The review synthesizes randomized experiments and natural experiments running in both directions. On one side: anti-poverty programs — including unconditional cash transfers, conditional cash transfer programs, housing subsidies, and microfinance interventions — consistently improve psychological wellbeing outcomes in low- and middle-income country settings. The effect sizes vary but are reliably positive. This is consistent with the stress-biology account from Day 4: removing material insecurity reduces the chronic HPA activation that damages prefrontal function and elevates time discounting. On the other side: psychological and mental health interventions — psychotherapy (particularly cognitive behavioral therapy), mental health treatment, and "psychosocial" programs combining therapy with livelihood support — generate measurable economic gains in multiple settings. Treated individuals work more, earn more, and show improved economic decision-making.

The bidirectionality has a strong policy implication: the poverty trap may have a psychological door that opens from both sides. You can attack the material poverty and get psychological improvement; you can attack the mental illness directly and get economic improvement. A purely material anti-poverty program and a purely psychological intervention are partial solutions; the frontier of the literature is integrated programs that address both simultaneously, with growing evidence that integration yields gains beyond what either component produces alone.

### The replication controversies: where the evidence is more fragile

The week's readings have cited the Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, and Zhao (2013) *Science* paper — which reported that low-income mall shoppers thinking about a large car repair bill performed worse on cognitive tests by an amount comparable to 13–14 IQ points — as a foundational result. This finding attracted enormous attention and was influential in motivating ambitious cognitive-bandwidth interventions. In 2016, Carvalho, Meier, and Wang published a direct replication attempt in the *American Economic Review* that complicates the picture. Their study randomly assigned low-income US households to complete an online survey before or after their monthly payday — the before-payday group being under more financial pressure — and administered the same types of cognitive function and intertemporal choice tasks used in the original work. The result: no effect on cognitive function performance, no effect on risk-taking, and no effect on the quality of decision-making. They did find that pre-payday participants showed marginally more present-biased behavior on *monetary* intertemporal choice tasks (but not on non-monetary tasks), suggesting a narrow, context-specific effect on how people think about money under pressure rather than a general cognitive bandwidth effect.

This does not mean the original finding was fabricated or that scarcity has no cognitive effects. Laboratory contexts, the specific stressors used, the populations studied, and the timing of measurement all plausibly moderate the strength and direction of effects. What the Carvalho et al. replication does suggest is that the strong, IQ-point-equivalent effect that made the 2013 *Science* paper so compelling should be treated with more caution than its prominence in popular accounts implies. The tunneling effects on attentional neglect — which are multiply replicated across diverse paradigms, as seen in Day 2's readings — appear more robust than the cognitive bandwidth effects on general fluid intelligence. Present bias and its connection to borrowing behavior, documented in Day 5, is replicated across many datasets and methods. But the specific claim that financial worry reduces cognitive performance on standardized tests by quantifiable fixed amounts should be held at arm's length by policy designers, who should not need that claim to justify simplification, slack creation, or income support.

### What the interventions literature implies for design

Holding the week's readings together, a policy framework begins to emerge that is both more ambitious and more precise than either the purely material or the purely behavioral approaches alone. First, direct income transfers — particularly well-designed cash transfers — have multi-channel effects: they relax material constraints, reduce stress, and (through stress reduction) may partially restore the prefrontal capacity and goal-directed decision-making documented in Day 4 as impaired under chronic poverty. The design details matter: lump-sum transfers appear to produce less ongoing stress than monthly installments for households who have trouble managing income across time.

Second, administrative processes that mediate access to benefits should be designed as if the people who need those benefits are under significant cognitive load — because they are. The default assumption should be that complexity is an exclusion mechanism, not a filter. Forms should be short. Salience of concrete benefits should be high. Friction should be removed rather than added. This follows directly from tunneling theory but is validated by the Bhargava–Manoli evidence.

Third, the bidirectional poverty–mental-health evidence suggests that mental health should be treated as an economic variable, not merely a humanitarian one. Programs that improve psychological wellbeing generate economic returns; programs that provide economic support generate psychological returns. Integrated programs that address both dimensions simultaneously are the emerging frontier.

## Questions to think about

1. The Haushofer–Shapiro study finds that lump-sum cash transfers produce lower cortisol than monthly installment transfers of the same total amount. This suggests that stress biology is sensitive not just to how much income a household has but to how *uncertain* or *irregular* that income is. What does this imply for the design of cash-transfer programs — and for other income-support systems like wages, benefits, and pensions — in terms of both amount and timing?

2. Bhargava and Manoli find that simplification raises EITC take-up from 14% to 23%, but even after simplification, 77% of eligible filers who did not claim still do not claim after receiving the mailing. What might explain the remaining non-take-up? Consider both behavioral explanations (some form of discounting or inattention) and structural ones (distrust of government, immigration status concerns, lack of information channels).

3. The replication of the Mani et al. cognitive bandwidth finding is mixed: strong effects in the original Indian farmer study and the mall shoppers experiment, null effects in the Carvalho et al. payday study. What features of the original studies and the replication might account for the discrepancy — and how should policy-makers respond when the scientific evidence base for an intervention they have already committed to becomes contested?
