# The behavioral economics of poverty — Day 4/7: Stress, Cortisol, and the Rewired Brain

## Context (2–3 paragraphs)

The first two days of this week built up two complementary cognitive accounts of how poverty shapes decision-making. Day 1 introduced the scarcity framework and the Haushofer–Fehr model of a self-reinforcing feedback loop: poverty causes chronic stress and negative affect, which in turn raise risk aversion and time discounting, which in turn make it harder to escape poverty. Day 2 zoomed in on tunneling — the attentional mechanism by which the scarcity mindset simultaneously sharpens focus on the immediate resource problem and creates neglect of everything that falls outside that narrow tunnel. Both explanations are largely psychological: they describe how attention and cognition are affected by the subjective experience of scarce resources.

Today we go one level deeper, into biology. What is happening in the body and brain when someone is living under chronic financial stress? The answer runs through the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis — the system that governs the body's response to threat — and through the neurochemical cascade that system triggers. When stress becomes chronic, elevated cortisol and noradrenaline do not merely make people feel anxious; they restructure how the brain processes risk, reward, and future consequences. The poverty-stress link is not metaphorical. Measurable physiological changes — elevated cortisol levels, altered prefrontal cortex function, a shift from flexible goal-directed behavior to rigid habitual behavior — provide the mechanistic underpinning for the cognitive effects documented earlier this week.

Understanding this pathway matters for two reasons. First, it closes the explanatory loop: we now have a complete chain from material circumstances to behavior that runs from resource constraint → subjective stress → HPA activation → cortisol/noradrenaline → altered risk preferences + habitual mode → suboptimal decisions → sustained poverty. Second, it has implications for when and how interventions can be effective. If chronic stress has literally altered how the brain processes decisions, simply providing information, incentives, or choice architecture changes may be insufficient if the biological substrate of decision-making is itself compromised.

## Today's readings (~20 min total)

**1. "Effects of stress throughout the lifespan on the brain, behaviour and cognition"** — Lupien, S.J., McEwen, B.S., Gunnar, M.R. & Heim, C., *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, vol. 10 (2009), pp. 434–445 — <https://www.nature.com/articles/nrn2639> — ~10 min (focus on the abstract, the introduction, and the sections on the hippocampus, prefrontal cortex, and economic/behavioral implications) — This landmark review, authored by four of the world's leading stress neuroscientists and published in *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, provides the mechanistic foundation for today's reading. It traces how stress hormones — particularly glucocorticoids (cortisol in humans) — act on brain structures across the lifespan, with lasting effects on memory, cognition, and behavioral flexibility. The authors show that chronic stress exposure, which disproportionately accompanies poverty, disrupts prefrontal cortex function and hippocampal architecture in ways that are measurable, partially reversible, and with documented consequences for adaptive decision-making. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* is among the highest-impact journals in the neurosciences.

**2. "Risk and Reward Are Processed Differently in Decisions Made Under Stress"** — Mather, M. & Lighthall, N.R., *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, vol. 21, no. 1 (2012), pp. 36–41 — <https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0963721411429452> — ~5 min — A concise invited review that synthesizes experimental findings on how acute stress changes the weighting of potential gains and losses in risky decisions. Critically, the authors show that stress effects on risk-taking are not uniform: males tend to become more risk-seeking under stress while females tend to become more risk-averse, a divergence mediated by dopaminergic activity in the striatum and insula. This nuanced picture complicates the simple claim that "stress makes people risk-averse," and introduces the important caveat that the direction of stress effects depends on who is stressed and in what domain. *Current Directions in Psychological Science* is the flagship review journal of the Association for Psychological Science, publishing invited syntheses by leading researchers.

**3. "Concurrent Glucocorticoid and Noradrenergic Activity Shifts Instrumental Behavior from Goal-Directed to Habitual Control"** — Schwabe, L., Tegenthoff, M., Höffken, O. & Wolf, O.T., *Journal of Neuroscience*, vol. 30, no. 24 (2010), pp. 8190–8196 — <https://www.jneurosci.org/content/30/24/8190> — ~8 min — This controlled pharmacological experiment is the most direct demonstration of the biological mechanism by which stress tilts the brain away from flexible, goal-sensitive decision-making and toward rigid, stimulus-driven habits. Participants who received both hydrocortisone (elevating cortisol) and yohimbine (elevating noradrenaline) — mimicking the hormonal profile of an acute stress response — could no longer update their behavior when the value of an outcome changed, whereas participants given either drug alone remained goal-directed. The *Journal of Neuroscience* is the flagship journal of the Society for Neuroscience, the world's largest neuroscience organization.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### The HPA axis: the body's stress broadcast system

When the brain detects a threat — a predator, a social humiliation, or an unpaid bill arriving in the mail — it activates a hormonal cascade beginning in the hypothalamus. The hypothalamus releases corticotropin-releasing hormone, which prompts the pituitary to secrete adrenocorticotropic hormone, which in turn signals the adrenal glands to release cortisol. This hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis is a system designed for rapid response: cortisol floods the bloodstream within minutes of a stressor, mobilizing energy, heightening vigilance, and suppressing non-urgent bodily functions.

The problem, as Lupien, McEwen, Gunnar, and Heim's (2009) review makes clear, is what happens when this system is chronically activated. The cortisol receptors that regulate the HPA axis are concentrated precisely in the brain regions most critical for learning, memory, and flexible decision-making: the hippocampus and the prefrontal cortex. Under prolonged cortisol exposure, dendritic branches in the hippocampus retract, synaptic connections are pruned, and the prefrontal cortex — the seat of planning, impulse control, and goal-directed thought — becomes functionally impaired. These are not metaphorical effects. They are visible in brain imaging and measurable through standard cognitive tasks. Importantly, Lupien and colleagues show that these effects are at least partially reversible when stress is removed — a finding with direct implications for the question of whether cash transfers can "repair" decision-making capacity, not just relieve material need.

### Cortisol, risk, and the gain/loss asymmetry

The intuitive prediction — that stress makes people more cautious and risk-averse — turns out to be only half the story. Mather and Lighthall's (2012) synthesis of laboratory experiments reveals a more complex picture: stress alters *how* the brain weighs potential gains against potential losses, and the direction of this shift varies predictably with the decision-maker's gender.

Under acute stress, males in these experiments tended to become more sensitive to potential rewards and less sensitive to potential losses — a profile that manifests as increased risk-seeking. Females under the same stressors showed the opposite pattern: heightened loss-aversion and reduced willingness to take risks. The underlying mechanism involves dopamine. Stress triggers dopaminergic activity in the reward-processing circuitry of the striatum, but this activation is modulated differently by testosterone and estrogen, producing divergent behavioral outcomes. Neither response is simply "better" or "worse" — each reflects a coherent if context-dependent shift in how the brain is weighting uncertain futures under physiological pressure.

The relevance to poverty is double-edged. For men experiencing poverty-related stress, the profile of increased risk-seeking could help explain a pattern that puzzles social observers: why do some men living in financial hardship take risks — in informal markets, in gambling, in high-variance but high-upside activities — that look imprudent from the outside? For women, the heightened risk aversion documented under stress may interact with the tunneling and bandwidth depletion effects from earlier in the week to produce a profile of extreme conservatism that, while rational in some respects, may also block potentially beneficial investments or decisions. Both patterns are artifacts of stress biology, not character.

### The goal-directed/habitual switch: stress's deepest effect on decision-making

Perhaps the most consequential and least-known finding in stress neuroscience is the one demonstrated by Schwabe and colleagues in 2010: stress does not merely alter the preferences that go into decisions — it changes the *system* that makes decisions.

Behavioral neuroscientists distinguish between two neural systems that govern action. The goal-directed system, anchored in the prefrontal cortex and dorsomedial striatum, encodes explicit models of the world: "If I do X, I will get outcome Y, and I currently value Y, so I should do X." It is sensitive to changes in the value of outcomes and can update rapidly when circumstances change. The habitual system, anchored in the dorsolateral striatum, operates by learned stimulus-response associations: "In situation X, I have historically done Y, so I do Y" — regardless of whether Y is still valuable. Habits are fast and efficient, but they are blind to change.

What Schwabe and colleagues showed is that the hormonal signature of acute stress — the concurrent elevation of cortisol (glucocorticoids) and noradrenaline — is sufficient to shift the balance between these two systems, from goal-directed to habitual. Participants who received both substances could no longer update their behavior when an outcome they had been trained to pursue was subsequently devalued; they continued responding out of habit even when the "prize" was no longer worth having. Neither cortisol nor noradrenaline alone produced this shift — it required their interaction, which is precisely what the acute stress response delivers.

In everyday terms: when a person is acutely stressed, they fall back on automatic routines rather than reasoning toward the best available outcome. For someone living in chronic poverty, where financial emergencies are frequent and stress is persistently elevated, this mechanism means that the cognitive tools most needed to navigate complex tradeoffs — updating beliefs, comparing options, overriding impulse — are precisely the tools that stress is continuously suppressing.

### Connecting the biology to the behavioral economics

The three readings today provide the neurobiological grounding for what the Haushofer–Fehr framework (introduced on Day 1) described at a higher level of abstraction. The feedback loop — poverty → stress → worse decisions → sustained poverty — now has a credible biological mechanism. Chronic HPA axis activation under poverty conditions elevates cortisol in measurable ways, damages prefrontal cortex function, shifts decision-making from goal-directed to habitual, and alters the weighting of risks and rewards in ways that vary by gender and domain. None of these effects require unusual personalities or weak willpower. They are the predictable neurophysiological response to a chronically threatening environment.

One important nuance that emerges from holding these findings together: the direction of stress effects on economic decisions is not uniform. Haushofer and Fehr (2014, covered on Day 1) noted that stress tends to increase risk aversion and time discounting in their synthesis of field evidence — but Mather and Lighthall (2012) show that in the laboratory, males under acute stress become *more* risk-seeking. These findings are not necessarily contradictory: the context matters enormously (chronic vs. acute stress, gains vs. losses, familiar vs. novel decisions, male vs. female respondents). The takeaway is not a single prediction but a disposition: stress destabilizes the neural systems that underpin consistent, goal-sensitive decision-making, and the resulting behavior can look either more cautious or less cautious depending on who is stressed, how, and about what. This complexity is a feature, not a bug — it reflects the genuine heterogeneity of poverty's effects on behavior, which any serious policy must eventually grapple with.

## Questions to think about

1. The Schwabe et al. experiment shows that concurrent cortisol and noradrenaline — but not either alone — shifts behavior from goal-directed to habitual. This is a very specific pharmacological interaction. How strong is the evidence that this laboratory mechanism generalizes to the chronic, diffuse stress of living in poverty, where cortisol is elevated but not necessarily in the acute spike pattern of a stress response test?

2. Mather and Lighthall find that stress has opposite effects on risk-taking in men and women. If this pattern holds in real-world financial decisions, what would it imply for how gender shapes the specific behavioral consequences of poverty-related stress? Are the policy prescriptions that follow from scarcity theory the same for men and women in poverty?

3. Lupien and colleagues show that stress-induced brain changes are at least partially reversible when the stressor is removed. This suggests that reducing financial stress — for example through unconditional cash transfers — might improve decision-making quality not only by relaxing constraints but by allowing the brain to recover. How would you design a study to test whether this "biological repair" effect is occurring in a real-world cash-transfer program?
