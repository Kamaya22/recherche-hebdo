# The behavioral economics of poverty — Day 1/7: Why Scarcity Changes How We Think

## Context (2–3 paragraphs)

Why do people in poverty sometimes make choices that seem self-defeating to outside observers — taking out high-interest loans, neglecting long-term health, missing deadlines for benefits they are entitled to? The classical economics answer was uncomfortable and largely unspoken: poor people must have different preferences, weaker self-control, or shorter time horizons than others. Over the past two decades, a convergence of behavioral economics, cognitive psychology, and field experiments has offered a radically different account. The experience of financial scarcity itself — not any fixed trait of personality — creates a cognitive state that systematically distorts attention, depletes working memory, and amplifies stress responses in ways that would impair anyone's decision-making. Poverty does not reveal character; it imposes a tax on the mind.

The intellectual roots of this week's theme run through two distinct but complementary streams. One comes from behavioral economics — the discipline that documents how real human decision-making deviates from the rational-agent ideal through cognitive biases, heuristics, and limited self-control. The other comes from cognitive psychology and neuroscience, which trace the mechanisms: how working memory is consumed by worry, how cortisol from chronic stress shifts risk preferences, and how focusing intently on one urgent problem ("tunneling") causes everything outside that tunnel to go unattended. The landmark papers from Mullainathan, Shafir, Mani, Haushofer, and colleagues brought these streams together and turned them on the specific experience of being poor.

This week, we work through the evidence and its implications day by day. Today's reading provides the panorama: the conceptual framework, the canonical studies, and the central puzzle that animates the whole field. Future days will zoom in — on tunneling (Day 2), on cognitive bandwidth and IQ-equivalent effects (Day 3), on the stress-cortisol-risk-aversion pathway (Day 4), on debt traps and savings behavior (Day 5), on interventions and their limits (Day 6), and finally a synthesis tying the week together (Day 7). By the end, you should be able to evaluate both the genuine insights and the live controversies of this research program.

## Today's readings (2–3 items, ~20 min total)

**1. "Some Consequences of Having Too Little"** — Shah, Mullainathan & Shafir, *Science*, vol. 338, no. 6107 (2012), pp. 682–685 — <https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1222426> — ~6 min — This compact but landmark paper introduces the core concept of the week: scarcity — whether of money, time, or any resource — changes how people allocate attention, leading them to engage more deeply with the scarcity problem (a phenomenon the authors call "tunneling") while neglecting other concerns. Six experiments demonstrate that resource-constrained individuals outperform wealthier peers on tasks relevant to the scarce resource, yet systematically overborrow and neglect other obligations. The paper is published in *Science*, one of the world's highest-impact multidisciplinary journals, and has accumulated thousands of citations; it is the conceptual foundation for the entire scarcity framework.

**2. "On the Psychology of Poverty"** — Haushofer & Fehr, *Science*, vol. 344, no. 6186 (2014), pp. 862–867 — <https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1232491> — ~10 min — A invited review in *Science* synthesizing evidence for a complementary pathway: poverty causes chronic stress and negative affect, which in turn reshape economic preferences — raising risk aversion, increasing time discounting, and favoring habitual over goal-directed behavior. The authors propose these relationships form a self-reinforcing feedback loop: poverty → stress → suboptimal economic choices → sustained poverty. Ernst Fehr is one of the founders of modern behavioral economics (University of Zurich); Johannes Haushofer (then MIT, later Princeton) has conducted field experiments on cash transfers and stress in Kenya. This review is the go-to reference for the psychological and physiological mechanisms underlying poverty's cognitive effects.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### The failure of classical rational-agent models

Standard microeconomics describes agents who maximize utility given their budget constraints. Under this model, a poor person's choices are simply a rational response to having less: they prefer immediate consumption over saving because they need food today; they decline insurance because premiums are a luxury. Poverty is thus a resource problem with a resource solution — give people more money and they will make better choices.

This picture is contradicted by several observations that classical theory struggles to explain: poor households frequently pay very high interest rates for short-term credit even when cheaper options exist; they under-enroll in programs they are eligible for due to complex paperwork; they make financial errors that compound their situation. These behaviors prompted two kinds of responses. One was moralistic: poor people are impulsive or irresponsible. The other, from behavioral economists, was mechanistic: something about the experience of poverty changes how people's minds work.

### What scarcity theory proposes

Mullainathan and Shafir's central insight, formalized in a 2012 *Science* paper and expanded in their 2013 book *Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much*, is that scarcity — whether of money, time, or social belonging — captures attention. It is not merely that the poor are distracted by financial worries; the mechanism is more specific and in some ways more interesting. When resources are tight, the mind focuses powerfully on the scarcity problem itself, allocating cognitive resources to managing it. This "tunneling" effect has a dual character: it can enhance performance on tasks directly related to the scarce resource (the poor are expert at stretching a dollar, the time-pressed person can be impressively efficient) while simultaneously causing neglect of everything outside the tunnel.

The practical consequences of tunneling compound: someone tunneled on a financial crisis might fail to collect a tax refund they are owed, forget a medical appointment, or miss an enrollment deadline for a program that would help them. These failures are then attributed to the person — irresponsible, disorganized, short-sighted — rather than to the attentional geometry of their situation.

Scarcity theory also introduces the concept of "bandwidth." Drawing on the analogy of computational resources, Mullainathan and Shafir argue that humans have a finite pool of cognitive capacity (working memory, executive control, fluid intelligence) and that worrying about scarcity occupies some of this bandwidth — permanently, not just during bouts of active planning. This borrowed bandwidth comes at the cost of every other cognitive task.

### The experimental and field evidence

The empirical pillar of this framework is a 2013 *Science* paper by Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, and Zhao ("Poverty Impedes Cognitive Function," DOI: 10.1126/science.1238041). In a striking two-part study, the researchers first recruited shoppers in a New Jersey mall and presented them with a financial scenario — a car repair bill. Low-income participants who were asked to consider an expensive repair (around $1,500) subsequently performed significantly worse on standard tests of fluid intelligence and cognitive control than low-income participants faced with a cheap repair or than high-income participants in either condition. The financial worry temporarily consumed bandwidth, reducing performance by an amount the authors compared to the effect of losing a full night of sleep, or roughly 13–14 IQ points.

The second part of the study took this finding out of the laboratory. Indian sugarcane farmers are paid once a year at harvest; before harvest they are cash-strapped; after harvest they are comparatively flush. The researchers tested cognitive function of the same farmers before and after the harvest. The identical person performed significantly better after harvest — when less worried about money — than before. Because it tracks within individuals across the seasonal cycle, this design rules out the obvious confound (that cognitively sharper people are also wealthier): it is the same person, better or worse, depending on their current financial situation.

### The psychological and physiological pathway

Haushofer and Fehr's 2014 *Science* review traces a complementary mechanism through stress biology. Living in poverty is objectively more stressful than not — chronic financial insecurity, housing instability, neighborhood violence, and the cumulative burden of administrative demands all activate the body's stress response. Stress elevates cortisol and other hormones that, beyond their health consequences, directly affect economic decision-making. Studies reviewed by Haushofer and Fehr show that experimentally elevated stress increases risk aversion and raises time discounting (the tendency to prefer immediate over future rewards). In a striking field study, Kenyan farmers who experienced adverse weather shocks (a negative income shock) showed measurably higher cortisol levels and greater economic risk aversion in the aftermath.

The feedback loop this creates is perhaps the most striking part of the theory. Poverty → stress → risk aversion and present bias → decisions that make it harder to save, invest, and exit poverty → sustained poverty → more stress. This is not a moral failing; it is a dynamical system with a self-reinforcing equilibrium. An important implication is that interventions targeting only the resource constraint (more money) may, if they reduce stress, simultaneously improve decision-making quality — two effects for the price of one. This is part of what makes direct cash transfers so interesting to researchers: they may work not only by relaxing the budget but also by relaxing the mind.

### Taking stock: where the evidence stands as of 2022

The de Bruijn and Antonides (2022) review in *Theory and Decision* provides the most rigorous scholarly audit of the field. The authors organize scarcity theory into three propositions: (1) scarcity causes attentional focus on the scarce resource and neglect of other concerns, leading to overborrowing; (2) scarcity induces trade-off thinking, producing more consistent consumption decisions; (3) scarcity reduces mental bandwidth, thereby increasing time discounting and risk aversion.

The review's verdict is nuanced. The first proposition — tunneling and overborrowing — has the most consistent empirical support. The second — that scarcity sharpens certain economic reasoning — also receives support from multiple experiments. The third proposition, linking bandwidth depletion directly to time discounting and risk preferences, is the most contested: the evidence is mixed, methodological issues are widespread (small samples, demand effects, measurement variability), and some replications have failed. The field is not in crisis, but it is in the process of determining which claims are robust across diverse populations and contexts and which were artifacts of specific laboratory conditions.

This nuanced view — some core insights well-supported, others still under scrutiny — is exactly where a growing science should be. The week ahead will look at each mechanism in turn, including the controversy in detail on Day 6.

## Questions to think about

1. Scarcity theory shows that poverty reduces cognitive performance — not that poor people are cognitively deficient. Yet the practical effect on choices and outcomes can look similar from the outside. What obligations does this distinction create for how policy-makers, employers, and social workers interpret the behavior of people experiencing poverty?

2. The "tunneling" metaphor suggests that attention to an urgent problem comes at the cost of non-urgent but important concerns (long-term health, savings, benefits enrollment). Can you think of domains other than poverty where the same attentional mechanism might operate — and where a policy or institutional design choice could reduce the cognitive tax?

3. Haushofer and Fehr describe a feedback loop: poverty → stress → worse decisions → deeper poverty. If this loop is real, what does it imply about the timing and size of interventions needed to break it? Is it enough to lift income slightly, or does the feedback suggest something different about the structure of effective support?

## This week's theme

**The behavioral economics of poverty — how scarcity shapes decision-making** (2026-W29)

For most of the twentieth century, the dominant view in economics held that rational individuals make the best choices their resources allow. Poverty was therefore a problem of resources, not cognition. Over the past two decades, a convergence of behavioral economics, cognitive psychology, and field experiments has challenged this view at its root. When people experience financial scarcity, something changes in how they think — not because of fixed traits, but because scarcity itself captures attention, drains cognitive bandwidth, elevates stress hormones, and reshapes risk and time preferences in measurable ways. A landmark series of papers in *Science* established that the same Indian farmer scores lower on IQ-equivalent tests before harvest (when cash-poor) than after; that thinking about a large car repair bill impairs cognitive performance in low-income mall shoppers; and that poverty-related stress drives cortisol levels high enough to alter economic risk preferences. These findings invert the old story: people do not remain poor because they make bad decisions; they sometimes make worse decisions because they are poor. This week traces the mechanisms, examines the evidence, confronts the controversies, and asks what these findings demand of policy design.
