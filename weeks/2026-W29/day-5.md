# The behavioral economics of poverty — Day 5/7: Present Bias, Debt Traps, and the Puzzle of Undersaving

## Context (2–3 paragraphs)

The first four days of this week have built a layered account of how poverty reshapes cognition. Day 1 introduced the scarcity framework and the Haushofer–Fehr feedback loop: poverty generates chronic stress and negative affect, which in turn elevate risk aversion and time discounting, which in turn make escape harder. Day 2 went inside the tunnel — the attentional geometry by which scarcity sharpens focus on the immediate resource problem while systematically generating neglect of anything that falls outside that narrow frame. Day 4 traced the physiological pathway through the HPA axis: chronic cortisol elevation impairs prefrontal cortex function, tilts decision-making from flexible goal-directed action toward rigid habitual responses, and reshapes how gains and losses are weighted. Across these three readings, a coherent portrait has emerged of a cognitive system under siege.

Today's reading takes that portrait and asks: what does it predict about the specific financial behaviors that characterize poverty — overborrowing at high interest rates, failing to build savings buffers, cycling repeatedly through expensive short-term credit? The dominant behavioral economics account centres on *present bias*, a well-documented feature of human time preferences in which people weight immediate costs and benefits far more heavily than their own future selves would endorse. Present bias is not unique to poor households — it is a near-universal property of human cognition. But its consequences are far more damaging when there is no slack: a wealthy person with present-biased preferences might fail to save for retirement at the optimal rate; a poor person with the same bias might take a 400% APR payday loan to cover rent, triggering a debt spiral that takes years to exit.

There is, however, a genuine debate about what drives the undersaving and overborrowing of poor households. Three explanations compete: (1) the *preferences* account — poor people are more present-biased, whether by disposition or because the stress and bandwidth depletion documented in earlier readings have increased effective time discounting; (2) the *constraints* account — poor people undersave not because they prefer present consumption but because they face objective barriers to saving (lack of access to safe accounts, social pressure to share resources, high transaction costs); and (3) the *cognitive tax* account — that scarcity-induced tunneling causes overborrowing not through unusual preferences but through the attentional neglect of future costs. Today's readings pit these explanations against each other, using field experiments that are able to distinguish them.

## Today's readings (2–3 items, ~20 min total)

**1. "Present-Biased Preferences and Credit Card Borrowing"** — Meier, S. & Sprenger, C., *American Economic Journal: Applied Economics*, vol. 2, no. 1 (2010), pp. 193–210 — <https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/app.2.1.193> — ~7 min — This paper provides the most direct field evidence linking individual-level present-biased time preferences to actual credit card debt. The authors elicited time preferences from over 400 participants in a field study, using incentivised intertemporal choice tasks (real payments at different dates), and then matched the resulting preference measures to participants' actual credit reports and tax returns. Present-biased individuals — those who display a sharper preference for immediacy that goes beyond normal long-run patience — had significantly higher credit card balances even after controlling for income, demographics, and credit constraints. The study is published in the *American Economic Journal: Applied Economics*, a flagship journal of the American Economic Association, and was one of the first to establish an empirical link between measured time preferences and observed debt behaviour in real data rather than in a controlled laboratory.

**2. "Tying Odysseus to the Mast: Evidence from a Commitment Savings Product in the Philippines"** — Ashraf, N., Karlan, D. & Yin, W., *The Quarterly Journal of Economics*, vol. 121, no. 2 (2006), pp. 635–672 — <https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/121/2/635/1884028> — ~8 min (focus on the introduction, the description of the SEED product, and the results sections) — This landmark randomised controlled trial offered a commitment savings account — called SEED ("Save, Earn, Enjoy Deposits") — to existing clients of a rural Philippine bank. The account allowed depositors to lock away funds until they reached a pre-chosen target amount or date, explicitly preventing early withdrawal. Of the 710 clients randomly offered the product, 28.4% accepted and opened an account; six months later, their deposit balances had grown substantially relative to the control group. The critical finding is diagnostic: people who scored as more present-biased on a baseline time-preference elicitation were *more* likely to take up the commitment account — they wanted the lock-in precisely because they knew their future selves would be tempted to spend. This result distinguishes sophisticated present bias (people who know they are biased and seek external commitment) from naïve bias (people who fail to anticipate their own self-control problem). The *Quarterly Journal of Economics* is consistently ranked among the top two or three journals in economics; this paper won the TIAA-CREF Paul A. Samuelson Award and has become the canonical reference for commitment devices in development finance.

**3. "Why Don't the Poor Save More? Evidence from Health Savings Experiments"** — Dupas, P. & Robinson, J., *American Economic Review*, vol. 103, no. 4 (2013), pp. 1138–1171 — <https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.103.4.1138> — ~8 min (focus on the introduction, the experimental design section, and the discussion) — This field experiment in rural Kenya randomises access to different savings technologies among poor households who, without intervention, maintain almost no liquid savings buffer. The central finding is striking: simply providing a safe, dedicated place to store money — a basic lockable box earmarked for health expenditures — increased health savings by 66 percent and significantly reduced vulnerability to health shocks, without requiring any change in underlying preferences or any financial incentive. This matters enormously for the preferences-vs.-constraints debate: if undersaving were driven purely by present-biased preferences, providing a container would not be sufficient — people could always choose not to deposit, or to break the lock. That it does work points toward constraints — including the absence of safe storage, the ever-present social pressure to lend to relatives, and the sheer transaction costs of accessing formal banks — as at least co-equal drivers of undersaving alongside preference factors. Dupas and Robinson also find that adding an earmarking frame helps, but mainly for households who face high demands from their social network, suggesting that what looks like "present bias" in some settings may be better described as a strategic response to social pressure. The *American Economic Review* is the world's most-cited economics journal; both Pascaline Dupas (Stanford) and Jonathan Robinson (UC Santa Cruz) are leading development economists.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### The anatomy of present bias

In standard economic theory, individuals discount the future at a constant rate: one year from now is worth some fraction β of today, and two years from now is worth β² of today, and so on exponentially. This gives time preferences a clean mathematical structure that is convenient for modelling but empirically violated. The robust finding from decades of laboratory and field studies is that people discount the near future far more steeply than the far future: when choosing between $100 today and $110 next week, most people take the $100; but when choosing between $100 in a year and $110 in a year and a week, most people wait and take the $110. The actual time interval is identical — one week — but preferences flip depending on whether "now" is in the frame.

This pattern, known as *hyperbolic discounting* or *present bias*, was formalised in the quasi-hyperbolic ("β-δ") model by Laibson (1997) and Phelps & Pollak (1968). In this framework, the discount factor between now and any future period is δ (the long-run patience parameter) multiplied by an additional β < 1 applied only to any delay from the present. The β parameter captures the extra penalty the mind applies to moving consumption out of the immediate moment. When β is substantially below 1, even a rational, foresighted agent will systematically choose immediate gratification in ways that their future selves — who will then sit at the new "present" — will regret. This is not irrationality; it is a consistent preference structure with a striking property: the person's own future behaviour is partially predictable but also partially threatening to their welfare.

### Present bias and credit card debt: the Meier–Sprenger finding

What distinguishes Meier and Sprenger's (2010) paper from the earlier laboratory literature on present bias is that it escapes the laboratory entirely. The authors recruited participants at a low-income tax-assistance centre and elicited time preferences using standard multiple price-list tasks, where participants chose between smaller sooner and larger later payment options and received actual payments according to their choices. They then matched these participants to their credit records. The result is sharp: individuals identified as present-biased by the incentivized task were more likely to carry credit card balances and carried higher balances, even after controlling for current income, age, education, financial literacy, and liquidity constraints. The magnitude is economically significant — not a statistical artefact that disappears when controls are added.

The interpretation is straightforward: the same cognitive tendency that leads someone to choose $100 now over $110 next week in the laboratory leads them, in the real world, to borrow on a credit card at 20% annual interest rather than wait and pay cash. The two decisions have identical mathematical structure. The Meier–Sprenger finding thus provides a direct empirical bridge between the cognitive science of time preferences and the financial outcomes of low-income households. It does not prove that present bias is the *primary* driver of debt — constraints, emergencies, and limited credit access all contribute — but it demonstrates that present bias is a measurable, real predictor of observed borrowing behaviour, not merely a theoretical construct.

### The debt trap as present bias in action

The payday lending industry is, in one sense, a market institution built around present bias. A payday loan typically provides $300–$500 immediately in exchange for a post-dated cheque or automatic debit for a larger amount in two weeks, at effective annual interest rates that routinely exceed 300–400%. The immediate benefit (cash today) is large and salient; the future cost (the principal plus fees due at the next paycheque) is diffuse and discounted by anyone with even modest present bias. When the paycheque arrives and the loan is due, the same financial pressures that created the initial need often remain — so the loan is rolled over for another fee, beginning a cycle that can persist for months or years.

Evidence reviewed by Bertrand, Mullainathan and Shafir in their 2004 synthesis suggests that this cycle is best understood not as rational credit use by sophisticated borrowers but as quasi-hyperbolic discounting in action. The borrower who rolls over a payday loan eight times did not, at any point, plan to borrow for eight months at 400% APR; they planned to borrow once, for two weeks. It is the repeated arrival of a new "present" moment, each time triggering a fresh underweighting of future costs, that generates the cycle from a sequence of individually locally-rational decisions. The cognitive-tax account from Day 2 (tunneling) and Day 4 (habitual mode under stress) adds a complementary mechanism: when the immediate financial crisis is consuming attentional resources, the future cost of the loan receives even less cognitive encoding than it would in a calm moment, amplifying the effective present bias.

### Why the poor undersave: three explanations tested against evidence

The Ashraf–Karlan–Yin (2006) experiment is designed to distinguish between sophistication about present bias and naïvety about it. The SEED commitment account is useful only to a *sophisticated* present-biased person — someone who knows they will be tempted to spend future savings and therefore wants a lock that prevents them from doing so. A person with no present bias has no use for a commitment account. A naïve present-biased person does not expect to face the temptation and therefore also has no use for it. The finding that take-up of the SEED account was higher among individuals who scored as more present-biased on the baseline elicitation therefore carries a strong implication: a significant fraction of poor households in the Philippines were (a) present-biased and (b) aware of it — sophisticated enough to seek external commitment to manage their own future selves.

This result has a subtler implication: if people know they are present-biased, and if commitment products are available, they may solve their own savings problem without intervention, by voluntarily restricting their future options. The demand for such products — ROSCAs (rotating savings and credit associations), informal savings clubs, locked boxes, even illiquid assets like livestock — is documented across dozens of low-income contexts. Their prevalence is itself evidence for the sophistication account: people are not blindly acting on present-biased preferences; they are actively engineering their environment to constrain their future selves.

### The constraints account: safe storage matters independently

Dupas and Robinson's (2013) finding that a simple dedicated lockbox — with no financial incentive, no commitment device with teeth, no change to the poverty environment — increased health savings by 66 percent complicates the pure present-bias story. If undersaving were primarily driven by time preferences (people simply want to consume now rather than later), then providing a storage container would not help: people would still choose not to deposit, or would simply not deposit enough. That access to a basic physical savings technology produces large savings gains points toward a constraints interpretation: many poor households *want* to save but cannot do so effectively because they lack a safe, accessible place to keep money that is also shielded from the constant social pressure to lend to relatives and community members.

This "social tax" on savings is a critical piece of the puzzle that purely preference-based accounts miss. In many low-income, high-social-capital communities, informal norms of mutual aid mean that any visible savings balance is quasi-public property — anyone in the network who needs money can legitimately ask for a loan or a gift, and refusing imposes social costs. The result is that savings under the mattress are not safe savings: they are communal resources held temporarily by one household. Dupas and Robinson find that earmarked savings accounts — formally designated for health spending — help precisely this subgroup: households with high demand from their social network benefit more from the earmarking frame, because it gives them a socially legitimate excuse to refuse redistribution requests. The earmark functions not as a cognitive device but as a social one.

### Preferences, constraints, or cognitive tax? The answer is all three

The week's readings — and today's three papers in particular — converge on an answer to the framing question that is perhaps unsatisfying but is almost certainly correct: undersaving and overborrowing among poor households are driven by a combination of preferences, constraints, and cognitive taxation, with the weight of each varying across individuals, contexts, and interventions.

Present bias is real, measurable, and empirically associated with debt. The Meier–Sprenger evidence for this is direct. But it is not uniformly distributed, it is not fixed, and it does not operate in isolation. The same neural circuits that generate present bias under calm conditions are magnified by the chronic stress documented on Day 4: elevated cortisol raises effective time discounting and tilts the brain toward habitual responses, which means the poverty environment may *create* present bias as a proximate effect of the stress it generates, not only select for individuals who are already more present-biased. The distinction matters for policy: if present bias is partly an artifact of chronic stress rather than a fixed trait, then interventions that reduce stress (cash transfers, housing stability) may reduce effective time discounting and improve savings behaviour as a by-product.

Constraints — the absence of safe storage, the social tax on visible wealth, the high fixed costs of accessing formal banking — are also independently important. The Dupas–Robinson result shows that removing even one such constraint (safe, private storage) produces large effects without any change in preferences. This suggests that some of the undersaving attributed to "poor people don't want to save" in classical development economics was actually "poor people cannot safely store savings even when they want to." And the tunneling effect from Day 2 adds a third mechanism: when the present financial crisis is consuming attention, future consequences of borrowing receive less cognitive processing than they deserve — not because the person places low utility on the future but because the tunnel narrows what enters conscious deliberation.

The practical import is that effective savings promotion and debt-cycle reduction probably require simultaneous action on all three fronts: reducing barriers (easier access to safe, formal accounts), managing present bias (commitment devices, automated saving, choice architecture), and reducing the cognitive tax (administrative simplicity, reducing the frequency of financial emergencies through income support and consumption smoothing). These are not alternatives; they are complements.

## Questions to think about

1. The Ashraf–Karlan–Yin experiment finds that the people most likely to take up a commitment savings account are those who score as most present-biased. This implies a degree of self-awareness about one's own time preferences. But for the product to work, the person must also correctly anticipate that their future self will be tempted to raid savings — which requires accurate forecasting of a future temptation. What factors might make this self-knowledge more or less reliable? Are there circumstances where a person might be present-biased but not aware of it, and what would the consequences be for their take-up of commitment devices?

2. Dupas and Robinson show that a simple dedicated savings container increases health savings by 66 percent, largely by providing a socially legitimate way to resist redistribution demands from one's social network. This suggests that some of what looks like "present bias" in communal settings is actually strategic behaviour under social pressure: saving in a way that is socially invisible. If the savings problem in these contexts is partly a coordination failure (everyone would be better off if everyone were allowed to save without being asked to share), what would an institutional solution look like? Is individual commitment technology enough, or does this require collective action?

3. The cognitive-tax hypothesis — that overborrowing and undersaving partly reflect the attentional narrowing and habitual-mode decision-making that chronic stress imposes, rather than stable time preferences — implies that the same person should display lower effective present bias when they are not under financial stress. Is there field evidence that measures time preferences of the same individuals across stress states (e.g., before and after a cash transfer, before and after harvest), and what do such studies find? What would it imply for the meaning of "present bias" as a trait if it fluctuates substantially with current material circumstances?
