# The behavioral economics of poverty — Day 3/7: Mental Bandwidth and Cognitive Performance

## Context (2–3 paragraphs)

Day 1 introduced the scarcity framework and its central claim: that the experience of financial pressure depletes the cognitive resources available for everything else in life. Day 2 examined one specific manifestation of this — tunneling, the automatic narrowing of attention toward the scarce resource and away from non-urgent concerns. Today we go deeper into the underlying mechanism: the direct empirical evidence that financial worry reduces measurable cognitive performance. The distinction matters. Tunneling is about how attention is *allocated* — the mind focuses intensely on one thing at the cost of others. Bandwidth depletion is about how much cognitive capacity is *available* in the first place. Both effects can be present simultaneously, and today's readings show that financial worry does not merely redirect the mind; it shrinks it.

The core cognitive capacities in question are working memory (the ability to hold and manipulate information in mind), executive control (the ability to inhibit competing impulses, switch between tasks, and pursue goals over time), and fluid intelligence (the capacity to reason in novel situations, independent of prior knowledge). These are not peripheral skills; they are the core machinery of planning, self-regulation, and decision-making. If scarcity depletes them, the downstream effects on behavior, health, and economic outcomes would be substantial and predictable. Today's readings examine this claim at three levels: the canonical two-study paper that established it empirically; the most recent large-scale meta-analysis that synthesizes a decade of subsequent research; and a 2021 PNAS experiment that revealed an unexpected further consequence of bandwidth depletion — that it does not merely impair what people can *do* but also reduces how much they can *enjoy* what little they have.

Building on what we covered this week, today's angle matters because it explains the mechanism underneath both tunneling and the stress-based pathway described in Day 1's Haushofer & Fehr review. Knowing that the pathway runs through working memory and executive control — not just stress hormones — opens specific questions about when the effect is largest, who is most affected, and whether it can be reversed. Day 4 will add the cortisol and stress physiology; for now, we stay at the cognitive level.

## Today's readings (2–3 items, ~20 min total)

**1. "Poverty Impedes Cognitive Function"** — Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir & Zhao, *Science*, vol. 341, no. 6149 (2013), pp. 976–980 — <https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1238041> — ~10 min — The canonical paper that put cognitive bandwidth on the poverty-research map. Two studies — one experimental (New Jersey mall shoppers faced with a car-repair financial scenario) and one field-based (464 Indian sugarcane farmers tested before and after the annual harvest) — together show that financial pressure measurably reduces performance on standard tests of fluid intelligence (Raven's Progressive Matrices) and executive control. The within-person design of the farmer study is particularly powerful: it tracks the same individual across a season, ruling out the obvious confound that wealthy people are also cognitively sharper. The authors are Anandi Mani (Warwick), Sendhil Mullainathan (Harvard), Eldar Shafir (Princeton), and Jiaying Zhao (UBC); *Science* is among the world's most selective multidisciplinary journals. This is the required foundation for everything that follows in today's reading.

**2. "Financial scarcity and cognitive performance: A meta-analysis"** — de Almeida, Scott, Soro, Fernandes, Amaral, Catarino, Arêde & Ferreira, *Journal of Economic Psychology*, vol. 101 (2024), article 102702 — <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.joep.2024.102702> — ~6 min (read abstract, "Results" overview, and "Discussion" conclusion sections) — The most comprehensive quantitative synthesis of the evidence to date. Drawing on 256 effect sizes from 29 datasets involving 111,852 respondents, the authors find an overall detrimental effect of financial scarcity on cognitive performance of Hedges' g = −0.43 — a medium-sized effect that survives across diverse study designs and cognitive task types. Crucially, when the analysis controls for education (a strong correlate of both income and baseline cognitive performance), the partial effect shrinks to g = −0.15 — still reliably negative but small. The moderation analyses reveal that the timing and severity of scarcity matter: lifetime and adulthood exposure drive larger effects than childhood exposure, and more extreme scarcity produces steeper cognitive costs. *Journal of Economic Psychology* is a peer-reviewed journal published by Elsevier covering the behavioral foundations of economic life; this paper provides the best current empirical map of when and how strongly the bandwidth effect appears.

**3. "Poverty-related bandwidth constraints reduce the value of consumption"** — Schofield & Venkataramani, *PNAS*, vol. 118, no. 35 (2021), article e2102794118 — <https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.2102794118> — ~7 min — A randomized experiment that reveals a less-discussed consequence of bandwidth depletion. Heather Schofield (Penn) and Atheendar Venkataramani (Penn) recruited participants at a behavioral economics lab in North Chennai, India, and randomly assigned them to bandwidth-altering treatments — including thinking about a stressful financial scenario, sleep deprivation, and induced thirst. After each treatment, participants rated how much they enjoyed a series of ordinary consumption activities. The striking result: reduced bandwidth significantly lowered enjoyment ratings, with the largest effects on food consumption. In other words, poverty does not merely tax what people can earn or afford; it taxes the pleasure they can extract from what they have. *PNAS* (Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences) is one of the world's most cited multidisciplinary scientific journals; this paper's randomized design allows causal inference that purely correlational field studies cannot provide.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### What bandwidth depletion means, and how it differs from tunneling

The bandwidth metaphor in scarcity theory borrows deliberately from computing: just as a network with limited bandwidth cannot transmit data as quickly or reliably when it is congested, a mind burdened by financial worry cannot execute cognitive tasks as efficiently. But it is important to be precise. "Bandwidth" in this literature refers specifically to two cognitive systems: working memory — the mental workspace where information is held active while it is being processed — and executive control — the supervisory system that manages attention, inhibits impulses, and keeps goals in mind over time. These systems are not unlimited; decades of cognitive psychology research show they have capacity ceilings and that demanding tasks deplete them.

Tunneling (Day 2) describes how the mind *uses* its bandwidth under scarcity — it directs available resources intensely toward the scarcity problem and away from peripheral concerns. Bandwidth depletion describes the additional effect: that worrying about scarcity consumes some of that capacity as background overhead, continuously, even when a person is not actively thinking about finances. The practical implication is that someone experiencing financial strain is, in effect, always partially occupied: a portion of their cognitive RAM is running a persistent, involuntary background process — financial worry — that is unavailable for other tasks. The result is not dramatic cognitive impairment in all domains; it is a systematic, measurable reduction in the performance headroom available for demanding tasks.

### The sugarcane farmers and the mall shoppers

The Mani et al. (2013) paper deserves careful attention because its two-study design is ingeniously constructed to address the strongest objection to any poverty-cognition link: the confound between wealth and baseline intelligence. If wealthier people consistently perform better on cognitive tests, this could simply mean that more cognitively capable people earn more — a selection effect, not a causal one. The paper attacks this confound from two directions.

The mall study used an experimental induction: participants of varied income levels were shown a car-repair scenario and asked to think about how they would cope financially. Critically, the cost of the repair was manipulated. For a cheap repair ($150), no income-related differences in subsequent cognitive performance appeared. For an expensive repair ($1,500), the story changed dramatically: low-income participants showed a sharp drop in scores on Raven's Progressive Matrices (a widely used test of fluid intelligence) and on a cognitive control task — a drop the authors estimated at approximately 13–14 IQ points. High-income participants showed no such drop. The manipulation induced the financial worry experimentally and measured its cognitive cost directly.

The farmer study addressed the confound through within-person longitudinal tracking. Indian sugarcane farmers in Andhra Pradesh are paid once a year at harvest. Before harvest, the same farmer who will be cash-flush in a few months is genuinely cash-poor. The researchers tested 464 farmers on the identical cognitive battery before and after the harvest and found that each farmer performed significantly better post-harvest. Because the comparison is within the same person, fixed characteristics — genetics, education, baseline IQ — are held constant. What varies is the current financial situation, and performance varies accordingly.

Together, the two studies make a compelling case: the effect is real, it is caused by the psychological burden of financial worry (not merely by poverty as a fixed characteristic), and it operates at a scale comparable to losing a full night of sleep. That scale matters. Losing 13 IQ points does not make someone incapable; it makes them measurably less able to plan ahead, resist impulsive choices, manage administrative complexity, and execute on intentions formed earlier in the day.

### What a decade of subsequent research shows

The de Almeida et al. (2024) meta-analysis covers the decade of work that followed Mani et al., incorporating 111,852 respondents across 29 datasets. The overall picture is that the bandwidth effect is real but its magnitude depends substantially on context and methodology.

The headline finding — Hedges' g = −0.43 — represents a medium-sized effect. In behavioral research, effect sizes of this magnitude are substantial: they mean the average person under financial scarcity is cognitively performing worse than roughly two-thirds of their financially comfortable peers, all else equal. But "all else equal" is doing heavy lifting here. When the analysis controls for education, the effect shrinks to g = −0.15 — small but still reliably negative. This reduction tells us that much of the raw association between poverty and cognitive performance runs through the education pathway: poverty is correlated with lower educational attainment, which in turn predicts lower performance on standardized cognitive tasks. The bandwidth mechanism — the direct effect of current financial worry on current cognitive performance — accounts for the smaller residual effect.

The moderation findings are instructive. Scarcity experienced in adulthood and across the entire lifetime produces larger cognitive costs than scarcity experienced only in childhood; this is consistent with the idea that persistent, ongoing financial pressure is more cognitively depleting than early-life hardship alone. The severity of scarcity also matters: extreme poverty produces steeper cognitive costs than moderate financial stress, suggesting a dose-response relationship. One particularly important finding is that the type of cognitive task did not moderate the effect — scarcity penalized working memory tasks, processing-speed tasks, and executive-function tasks roughly equally. This broad cross-task impact is consistent with the theory that working memory and executive control are jointly impaired, since these systems underlie performance across many cognitive domains.

### An unexpected cost: bandwidth depletion and the diminished value of consumption

Schofield and Venkataramani's (2021) PNAS paper adds a dimension to the bandwidth story that the existing literature had largely overlooked. If financial worry depletes cognitive resources, and if experiential enjoyment of ordinary activities requires some of those cognitive resources — the presence of mind to savor food, to attend to leisure, to engage socially — then poverty may also reduce the subjective value of consumption itself. This is a significant additional welfare cost, entirely separate from the material deprivation that makes poor people's consumption bundles smaller.

The experiment tested this directly. Participants randomized to receive a bandwidth-depleting treatment — either a financial stress vignette, sleep deprivation, or induced thirst — subsequently rated the activities they engaged in as less enjoyable. The strongest effects were on food: participants who were hungry (induced thirst) or financially worried reported lower hedonic ratings for the food they ate. The result can be read as a causal estimate: the same meal, eaten by the same person, generates less pleasure when bandwidth is constrained than when it is not.

The authors frame this as "taxing the poor twice." The first tax is material: poverty constrains what you can afford to buy or consume. The second tax is cognitive: poverty constrains the mental bandwidth that allows you to enjoy what you do have. Standard economic welfare measures — which focus on consumption quantities — do not capture this second tax at all. A policy that raises a poor household's income enough to afford slightly more food improves their material welfare; but if it also reduces financial worry, it may improve the hedonic quality of the consumption they already have. This second effect is invisible to conventional measurement but potentially significant for wellbeing.

### Replication, controversy, and what is settled

This week's readings have noted that the scarcity framework is a living science with contested elements, and today's material is no exception. A 2024 meta-analysis in *Collabra: Psychology* by Szécsi and Szaszi, specifically reviewing laboratory studies that used scarcity-related word cues to test for attentional effects, found an effect size close to zero (g = 0.09) and concluded that the evidence for scarcity-cue effects on sustained attention is weak. The contrast with the broader de Almeida et al. finding (g = −0.43) is real but not necessarily contradictory: the two meta-analyses differ dramatically in scope. Szécsi and Szaszi focused narrowly on a specific laboratory paradigm (cue-induced effects on sustained attention); de Almeida et al. included observational studies linking income to cognitive performance across the lifespan. The narrower test is more skeptical; the broader one is more susceptible to confounds. Where the causal bandwidth mechanism is best established — Mani et al.'s experiments and Schofield & Venkataramani's RCT — the methodology is stronger precisely because it either manipulates scarcity directly or holds the person constant. The controversies are real and will be examined more fully on Day 6; for now, it is fair to say that the core claim (financial worry has measurable cognitive costs) is supported by multiple credible study designs, while the exact magnitude and the reliability of specific laboratory paradigms remain contested.

## Questions to think about

1. The sugarcane farmer study is powerful because it holds the person constant, but critics have noted it lacks a control group — both pre- and post-harvest conditions happen to the same people in a fixed seasonal sequence. What alternative explanations (other than cognitive bandwidth) might explain better performance post-harvest? How might a researcher design a study to rule them out?

2. The de Almeida et al. meta-analysis found that controlling for education reduces the scarcity-cognition effect from g = −0.43 to g = −0.15. What does this suggest about the causal pathway? Is education a confounder (an alternative explanation for both poverty and lower cognitive performance), a mediator (a mechanism through which poverty affects cognition), or something more complex?

3. Schofield and Venkataramani argue that bandwidth depletion reduces the enjoyment of consumption — a "second tax" on the poor. If this is true, what are the implications for how economists measure poverty and welfare? What kinds of interventions might address not just material deprivation but also the cognitive conditions needed to enjoy what one has?
