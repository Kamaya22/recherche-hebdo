# Urban Heat Islands and Climate Adaptation of Cities — Day 5/7: Design Solutions — The Mitigation Portfolio

## Context

The first four days of this week established why cities overheat and who bears the greatest burden. Days 1 and 2 laid out the physics: sealed surfaces trap solar energy, the urban canyon blocks radiative cooling, and anthropogenic heat from traffic and air conditioning adds a final layer of warmth. Day 3 connected these mechanisms to public health, documenting nearly 489,000 heat deaths per year globally, with the urban heat island directly responsible for a measurable share. Day 4 showed that these deaths are not distributed at random: historically disinvested, majority-minority neighbourhoods run 2–7°C hotter than wealthy tree-lined districts nearby, a pattern that stretches back to 1930s federal redlining policy.

Today we ask: what can cities actually do about it? The mitigation portfolio spans three families of interventions. Nature-based solutions — trees, parks, green roofs, green walls, wetlands — cool through evapotranspiration and shading. High-albedo strategies — cool roofs, reflective pavements — cool through redirecting solar energy back to the sky. Blue infrastructure — ponds, fountains, bioswales — cools through evaporation of water. None of these is a silver bullet, and one of the most important findings in recent research is that the ranking of strategies depends sharply on what you are trying to achieve (outdoor comfort, building energy savings, or health), what climate you are in, and what the surrounding urban form looks like.

Today's readings survey both the global evidence base for this portfolio and a concrete real-world deployment, before the guided summary pulls the threads together. Day 6 will examine how climate change alters both the urgency and the achievable ceiling of these interventions.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### The evidence landscape: which interventions cool how much?

The 2024 meta-review by Kumar et al. provides the best current panorama of the evidence. Covering 51 types of green, blue, and grey infrastructure across the literature, it finds a consistent pattern: the more biological surface area a space contains, and the more water flows through or evaporates from it, the more it cools. The top-performing categories for *air* temperature reduction are botanical gardens (5.0 ± 3.5°C), wetlands (4.9 ± 3.2°C), green walls (4.1 ± 4.2°C), street trees (3.8 ± 3.1°C), and vegetated balconies (3.8 ± 2.7°C). These figures reflect the temperature difference *inside or directly beneath* the intervention relative to surrounding streets — they are local cooling effects, not neighbourhood-wide averages. The large standard deviations matter: a green wall in humid Singapore will cool far more than one in arid Phoenix, because evapotranspiration is the dominant mechanism and requires available moisture. The review is also a candid diagnosis of gaps: many GBGI types have been studied in only a handful of cities, nearly always in the Global North, making generalisations to tropical or arid-zone cities uncertain.

Standalone water bodies — rivers, canals, ponds — are more modest coolants than their visual prominence might suggest. Without active mist or fountain systems, the median air-temperature reduction at the water's edge is roughly 0.5°C. Fountains and mist systems do better: quantified studies find 1–4.7°C air cooling and up to 2.26°C reduction within 5 metres. The lesson is that passive still water relies entirely on surface evaporation, which is constrained in humid conditions; active water features that maximise the water-air interface dramatically amplify cooling. Wetlands, which combine open water with dense vegetation, sit at the top of the blue-infrastructure rankings precisely because they stack both mechanisms.

### Urban trees: global scale versus local scale

The McDonald et al. 2026 study pulls the camera back to the planetary view. Using remote sensing and weather-station data for close to 9,000 cities — collectively home to about 3.6 billion people — the authors estimate that current urban tree cover reduces the air-temperature urban heat island by roughly 41–49% relative to what cities would experience with no trees at all. In absolute terms, the population-weighted average cooling is 0.15°C across all cities; over 900 million city residents benefit from at least 0.25°C of cooling; and roughly 200 million enjoy reductions of 0.50°C or more. In some cities the cooling reaches 2.7°C.

The study's sobering second finding is distributional. Trees are most abundant and most effective where they are least needed: in high-income countries, in humid climates where evapotranspiration is unconstrained, and in suburban districts with space for large canopies. Cities in South and Southeast Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and the arid belt of the Middle East and North Africa — which face the fastest urban growth and the most extreme heat — benefit the least. This echoes Day 4's domestic finding (wealthy, formerly "greenlined" American neighbourhoods have far more tree canopy than historically redlined areas) but at global scale.

The study also puts a hard number on a question that matters enormously for planning: how much of projected climate-change warming can trees offset? The answer, under mid-century scenarios, is approximately 9–10%. Tree planting is essential, but it cannot substitute for emissions reductions.

### What trees actually do at the street level

For the reader on the footpath, the relevant scale is not the city average but the temperature directly beneath and around a tree. A 2024 analysis by Li et al. (*Communications Earth & Environment*, doi:10.1038/s43247-024-01908-4) synthesised 182 studies from 110 global cities and found that well-placed trees can reduce pedestrian-level air temperature by up to 12°C, primarily through two mechanisms: shading (which cuts incoming solar radiation) and transpiration (which converts stored heat into water vapour). The two mechanisms work best in combination — a tree that shades but does not transpire (stressed by drought, or an artificial structure) cools far less than a healthy, well-watered tree.

The Li et al. study also yielded actionable design guidance. In tropical, temperate, and continental climates, planting a mix of deciduous and evergreen species in relatively open urban morphology (lower building height-to-street-width ratios) provides about 0.5°C more cooling than a monoculture. In arid climates, where water is limiting, evergreen species in compact urban morphology retain the most cooling per unit of water used. This climate-specificity is important: urban forestry programmes that simply maximise the number of trees planted without matching species and configuration to local conditions may underperform substantially.

### High-albedo strategies: cool roofs and reflective pavements

Nature-based solutions dominate the popular conversation about urban cooling, but materials-science approaches — specifically increasing the solar reflectance (albedo) of urban surfaces — have a distinct and complementary role.

Cool roofs, which use white or highly reflective coatings to return solar radiation to the sky rather than absorbing it as heat, are consistently identified as the most effective single rooftop intervention for reducing *building* energy demand for cooling. A 2024 modelling study for London (Brousse et al., *Geophysical Research Letters*, doi:10.1029/2024GL109634) found that city-scale cool-roof deployment reduced 2-metre air temperatures more than equivalent-coverage green roof or rooftop solar installations. At the building scale, a cool roof with a solar reflectance of 0.7 can reduce near-surface roof temperatures by roughly 3°C. The 2007 New York City law requiring all new roofs to be white or EnergyStar-rated highly reflective is one of the most prominent mandates of this type.

Reflective pavements have been deployed at neighbourhood scale most visibly in Los Angeles. A controlled field experiment in Covina, California — reported by Taleghani et al. in *Environmental Research Letters* (17, 044027, 2022; doi:10.1088/1748-9326/ac58a8) — tested the city's CoolSeal reflective coating over 181 lane-miles of road. When pavement albedo increased by an average of 0.18, maximum surface temperatures fell by up to 5°C. Air-temperature reductions were more modest — up to 0.2°C at peak afternoon — but measurable and detectable at distances beyond the coated area. The study also identified a nuance that matters for pedestrian design: reflective pavements redirect solar radiation upward, potentially increasing thermal radiation incident on the human body if surrounding buildings are low. In dense canyons, this trade-off requires careful geometry assessment before deployment.

### Comparing strategies across multiple goals

A 2025 PNAS study by Jiang, Krayenhoff et al. (doi:10.1073/pnas.2411144122) evaluated five strategies — street trees, cool roofs, green roofs, rooftop photovoltaics, and reflective pavements — simultaneously for three outcomes (outdoor heat stress, air-conditioning energy use, and ventilation of vehicle pollution) in three climatically contrasting cities: Toronto, Phoenix, and Miami. The results illustrate why a portfolio framing is essential:

- **Street trees** performed best for *outdoor thermal comfort* across all three cities, consistently reducing heat stress at pedestrian level more than any other single strategy.
- **Cool roofs** were the top performers for *reducing indoor air-conditioning energy demand*, sometimes outperforming green roofs because their higher solar reflectance directly reduces heat entering buildings.
- **Rooftop photovoltaics** occupied a middle position: they reduce building electricity draw (and thus indirectly reduce waste heat from mechanical cooling) but contribute little direct outdoor cooling.
- **Reflective pavements** showed the most context-dependent results: effective in open-plan low-rise areas but potentially counterproductive in deep urban canyons where reflected radiation concentrates at pedestrian level.

No single strategy dominated all three metrics in all three cities. The study's conclusion — that comprehensive urban heat adaptation demands a portfolio approach tailored to local climate, morphology, and policy goals — is the most robust recommendation the literature offers.

### How much total cooling is achievable?

A 2024 review in the *Annual Review of Environment and Resources* by Zhao, Carmeliet, Hamdi and colleagues synthesised evidence from pilot projects across Asia, Africa, North America, Latin America, and Europe. Their synthesis suggests that a coordinated deployment of multiple nature-based solutions — not any single type, but a strategic combination including trees, green roofs, green walls, and water features — could achieve more than 1°C of air-temperature reduction in densely populated cities by 2035. That figure matters because a 1°C reduction in peak heat is associated, in epidemiological literature, with a measurable reduction in heat-related mortality. Day 3 showed that mortality rises steeply above 25–30°C depending on acclimatisation; a 1°C shift of the distribution can push tens of millions of city residents below their critical threshold.

Whether 1°C is achievable in the cities that need it most remains the critical open question. The McDonald et al. 2026 finding — that cooling benefits are lowest in fast-growing cities with the hottest climates and least tree cover — sets the context for Day 6's examination of how global warming alters both the urgency and the limits of these interventions.

## Questions to think about

1. The PNAS 2025 study finds that street trees best reduce outdoor heat stress while cool roofs best reduce air-conditioning energy demand. These goals can conflict: a city investing in cool roofs might reduce indoor deaths but fail to protect outdoor workers and pedestrians. How should cities prioritise between these two groups, and what institutions or governance processes would need to change to make that prioritisation explicit?

2. The LA CoolSeal experiment found surface temperatures down by up to 5°C but air temperatures down by only 0.2°C. Most heat mortality occurs when *air* temperature is extreme, not surface temperature. Does this gap between surface and air cooling undermine the case for reflective pavements, or are there other pathways (reduced radiant heat to pedestrians, lower thermal mass warming the night air) that justify the investment?

3. Li et al. 2024 found that mixing deciduous and evergreen tree species in open urban morphology provides about 0.5°C more cooling than a monoculture. Given that city tree-planting programmes are often driven by cost, logistics, and nursery availability rather than by climate optimisation, what institutional or procurement changes would be needed to actually implement species-and-configuration guidance from climate science?
