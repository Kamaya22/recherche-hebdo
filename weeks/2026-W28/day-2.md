# Urban heat islands — Day 2/7: The Energy Budget in Depth

## Context

Day 1 gave us the five physical mechanisms that make cities warm: lower albedo, reduced evapotranspiration, urban canyon trapping of longwave radiation, high thermal mass, and anthropogenic heat. Today we move from naming these mechanisms to measuring them. Urban climate scientists frame all five through a single lens: the **urban surface energy balance** — an accounting equation that records exactly where every joule of incoming energy goes once it reaches an urban surface. Understanding this equation, and how each term shifts when farmland or forest becomes a city, is the deepest answer to why urban temperatures are elevated and where the most effective levers for intervention lie.

The equation is deceptively compact: **Q\* = QH + QE + ΔQS + QF**. Net radiation (Q\*) is the total electromagnetic energy arriving at a surface — shortwave sunlight absorbed minus shortwave reflected, plus downward longwave from the atmosphere minus longwave emitted by the surface itself. That energy must be entirely accounted for by four output terms: sensible heat flux (QH), the direct convective warming of the overlying air; latent heat flux (QE), the energy consumed by evapotranspiration as water changes phase from liquid to vapour; change in heat storage (ΔQS), energy taken up by the mass of buildings, roads, and underground infrastructure; and anthropogenic heat flux (QF), the additional energy injected into the system by human activities — engines, boilers, air conditioners, metabolism. In a well-watered rural landscape in summer, most of Q\* flows through QE: the surface stays cool by sweating. Urbanisation dramatically reshapes this partition.

Two questions frame today's reading. First, of all the ways urbanisation shifts the energy balance, which one most strongly drives the spatial pattern of urban warming we observe? Second, why is the urban heat island so much more pronounced at night, long after solar energy input has stopped? Two open-access papers in *Science Advances* — one from 2019, one from 2024, from largely overlapping research teams — give precise quantitative answers that overturn some common assumptions and clarify which interventions are most likely to work.

## Today's readings (2 items, ~20 min total)

**1. "Urban heat island: Aerodynamics or imperviousness?"** — Li, D. et al., *Science Advances* (2019) — <https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.aau4299> — ~12 min — Dan Li (then at Boston University, now Columbia) and colleagues at NOAA and Princeton develop a novel attribution framework to disentangle two competing explanations for daytime urban warming. The aerodynamic hypothesis holds that cities' rough, densely packed surfaces increase turbulent exchange, altering how efficiently heat convects into the lower atmosphere. The imperviousness hypothesis holds that sealed surfaces eliminate the evapotranspiration that cools rural landscapes. Applying the framework across hundreds of US cities with eddy-covariance flux measurements, the authors show which mechanism actually explains the spatial pattern of daytime UHI intensity — and draw direct implications for what kinds of interventions are most effective. Published in *Science Advances*, the fully open-access journal of AAAS; the paper is freely downloadable without institutional access.

**2. "Persistent urban heat"** — Li, D. et al., *Science Advances* (2024) — <https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adj7398> — ~8 min — The same research group (with Gabriel Katul, Elie Bou-Zeid, and Björn Maronga added) turns to a less-discussed property of urban warmth: its *temporal persistence*. Using observations and land-surface modelling, they show that impervious materials' high thermal inertia does more than raise the nighttime temperature floor — it makes urban heat anomalies "sticky," taking days rather than hours to dissipate after a heat event. The study finds that increasing impervious cover simultaneously increases nighttime minimum temperature and lengthens the multi-day recovery time, a double penalty for heat-stressed city-dwellers. Also open access in *Science Advances* (April 2024).

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### The urban energy balance: from equation to mechanism

The four output terms in the surface energy balance (QH, QE, ΔQS, QF) compete for the incoming energy supply Q\*. In a balanced rural landscape — say, a French wheat field or a Midwestern forest — the **Bowen ratio** (β = QH/QE) is typically 0.2–0.5 in summer, meaning latent heat flux dominates. Evapotranspiration is efficient: it consumes large amounts of energy per kilogram of water evaporated (about 2.5 MJ per kilogram), cooling the surface without directly warming the overlying air the way convection does. Urbanisation then acts on multiple terms simultaneously: it raises Q\* (lower albedo absorbs more solar radiation), nearly eliminates QE (impervious cover cuts off the moisture supply for evaporation), boosts ΔQS (high-density materials store large amounts of energy), and adds QF (waste heat from engines and appliances). The Bowen ratio in a heavily built-up block climbs to 3–5 or higher — the energy that was cooling the surface through evaporation is redirected entirely into warming the air.

### Imperviousness dominates the daytime UHI: what Li et al. (2019) found

Before Li et al.'s 2019 study, aerodynamics was a plausible candidate mechanism. Rougher urban surfaces (buildings act as macro-scale roughness elements) enhance turbulent mixing, which in principle could accelerate the transfer of heat from surface to air — raising air temperature while cooling the surface. This would represent a qualitatively different pathway from evapotranspiration loss. Li and colleagues' attribution framework allowed them to isolate each mechanism's contribution.

The result was unambiguous: across US cities, spatial variation in daytime UHI intensity is explained overwhelmingly by variation in **imperviousness** (lost evapotranspiration capacity), not aerodynamics. Critically, aerodynamic roughness and imperviousness have *opposite* signatures: increased roughness cools the surface but warms near-surface air (more heat is convected upward efficiently), whereas increased imperviousness raises both surface and near-surface air temperature simultaneously. For the metric most relevant to human experience — the temperature of the air at breathing height — only imperviousness matters in the same direction as the measured UHI. The implication: any cooling strategy that addresses aerodynamics (e.g., changing building geometry) while leaving imperviousness untouched will have limited effect on felt air temperature.

This finding also illuminates why **cool pavements** (high-albedo asphalt that reflects more solar radiation) are a popular but partial solution. Cool pavements address Q\* — reducing the total energy entering the budget — but they do nothing to restore QE or shift the Bowen ratio back toward latent heat. They cool surfaces, which can be important for radiant heat exposure, but they cool the overlying air less directly than permeable surfaces that can support evapotranspiration. Both strategies matter; the energy balance tells us which mechanism each targets.

### Climate context: why the same shift matters differently in wet and dry regions

A 2019 *Nature* study by Manoli et al. (573, 55–60; DOI: 10.1038/s41586-019-1512-9) — worth reading alongside today's papers — introduced an important qualification: the energy-balance mechanism that *dominates* UHI formation varies with regional climate. In **humid climates** (high background precipitation), rural areas naturally support high evapotranspiration, so urbanisation's elimination of QE represents a large incremental loss. The UHI is primarily a story of suppressed latent heat flux. In **dry climates**, rural areas have little moisture to evaporate even without urbanisation; there, the albedo reduction and altered convection efficiency in cities become the relatively more important drivers. Manoli and colleagues used this framework to show that UHI magnitude scales nonlinearly with city population and annual precipitation — a pattern that had been observed for decades without a clean mechanistic explanation. The implication for mitigation: greening strategies (which restore QE) are most powerful in wet climates where evapotranspiration would otherwise be high, while albedo-based strategies carry proportionately more weight in droughty ones.

### Thermal mass and the nocturnal heat island: the ΔQS story

The ΔQS term — heat storage in urban fabric — is the key to understanding why UHI is consistently stronger at night. During daylight hours, urban materials (concrete, brick, dense asphalt) absorb large amounts of solar and atmospheric energy. Concrete has a volumetric heat capacity of about 2 MJ/m³K, compared to ~1.5 MJ/m³K for dry soil and significantly less for an air-leaf-soil mixture under a forest canopy. Dense urban areas can therefore absorb substantially more energy per unit surface area over the course of a day. This energy is not lost — it is stored in the thermal mass of the built fabric and released slowly as infrared radiation and convective warming through the night.

The **urban canyon** intensifies this process. A street with a height-to-width ratio of 2:1 (tall buildings, narrow street) has a sky view factor (SVF) — the fraction of the sky hemisphere visible from street level — of perhaps 0.2–0.3, versus nearly 1.0 in an open rural field. Longwave radiation emitted from hot surfaces re-intercepts nearby surfaces rather than escaping to the sky, slowing radiative cooling. After sunset, rural landscapes cool rapidly: they radiate to a nearly open sky and have limited thermal mass to sustain heat release. Cities radiate more slowly (canyon trapping), from larger reserves (high thermal mass), while simultaneously continuing to add QF (overnight traffic, heating systems). The combined result is the characteristic nocturnal temperature minimum that never quite arrives.

### Thermal persistence: the Li et al. (2024) finding

Li et al. (2024) take the thermal mass story one step further in time. Their key finding is that impervious urban surfaces do not simply shift the mean nighttime temperature upward — they lengthen the *recovery time* after heat events. A city that has absorbed three days of above-normal solar input retains that thermal debt for longer than an equivalent rural landscape: each successive cool night fails to fully drain the stored heat before the next day's solar forcing arrives. The authors quantify this as an increase in **temperature autocorrelation** (the statistical "memory" of the temperature signal) that scales with the areal fraction of impervious cover.

This finding matters acutely for human health. The physiological stress of a heatwave comes not only from peak daytime temperatures but from the absence of nocturnal recovery: core body temperature cannot return to baseline if nights are as warm as days. The cardiovascular system, kidneys, and brain all require the overnight cool to repair heat-related damage. A city with high thermal persistence effectively prevents this recovery across the duration of a heatwave — and, Li et al. show, the persistence will worsen as tropical cities continue to expand their impervious cover.

### Pulling it together: anthropogenic heat and the full budget

The QF term deserves a final note. Across temperate city cores, anthropogenic heat ranges from roughly 20–80 W/m² on an annual average, with peaks of 200–400 W/m² in dense commercial and industrial zones during business hours. In high-latitude cities during winter, space-heating waste heat can approach or exceed the solar input on a short winter day — human activity becomes the primary heat source for the urban atmosphere. The air-conditioning paradox noted on Day 1 is visible in the equation: an air conditioner with a coefficient of performance (COP) of 3 removes 3 W from an indoor space but must eject 4 W outdoors (the 3 W of indoor heat plus 1 W of electrical energy consumed). For every unit of indoor cooling achieved, roughly 1.3 W of waste heat is added to the street. This is not a flaw in any particular design; it is thermodynamic inevitability for any heat pump operating at finite efficiency. And as heat events become more frequent — driving more air-conditioning demand — the QF contribution to the outdoor urban heat budget grows in lockstep.

The complete picture of Day 2: every major term in the surface energy balance moves in a direction that raises QH when land is urbanised. Net radiation (Q\*) increases due to lower albedo. Latent heat (QE) collapses due to imperviousness — the dominant daytime mechanism. Heat storage (ΔQS) absorbs the deficit during the day and releases it overnight, setting the nocturnal temperature floor. Anthropogenic heat (QF) adds a steady external forcing. These insights will anchor every subsequent day of the week: the health burden (Day 3) is amplified by thermal persistence; the inequity story (Day 4) maps onto who lives where in the Bowen-ratio landscape; the solutions portfolio (Day 5) targets specific terms in this equation; and climate change (Day 6) primarily acts by raising Q\* through more intense and persistent solar forcing.

## Questions to think about

1. Li et al. (2019) showed that imperviousness (lost evapotranspiration) matters more than aerodynamics for daytime air-temperature UHI. Many cities have invested in "cool pavements" — high-albedo surfaces that reduce absorbed solar radiation. Using the energy balance equation, explain which term cool pavements address and why they are more effective at reducing surface temperature than air temperature. What kind of pavement strategy would more directly attack the dominant mechanism?

2. Li et al. (2024) found that higher thermal mass in cities makes heat anomalies more temporally persistent — the temperature "sticks" for several days after a heat event rather than recovering overnight. If you were a materials engineer designing next-generation urban paving, which material properties would you optimise to reduce nighttime heat release — and is there a trade-off between reducing storage capacity (which helps nights) and providing structural durability?

3. The Bowen ratio in urban areas (β ≈ 3–5) is dramatically higher than in rural areas (β ≈ 0.2–0.5). Does this mean that two cities with identical sealed-surface fractions will always have similar UHI intensities — or can you think of scenarios where cities with the same imperviousness fraction end up with very different Bowen ratios and therefore very different warming patterns?
