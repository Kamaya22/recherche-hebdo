# Urban heat islands — Day 3/7: Health Impacts — Who Gets Sick, Who Dies

## Context

Days 1 and 2 established the physics of urban overheating: how cities absorb more solar energy than the ecosystems they replace, how sealed surfaces redirect the energy budget away from evaporative cooling and into sensible heat flux, and how the thermal mass of concrete and asphalt creates a nocturnal heat reservoir that refuses to drain overnight. Today we move from the energy balance equation to the hospital ward and the morgue.

The epidemiological case against urban heat is now unambiguous. Peer-reviewed studies from London to Los Angeles to Tokyo have quantified, in deaths and hospital admissions, exactly what happens when the UHI effect meets a heatwave. The number that anchors the field comes from a landmark 2021 modelling study published in *The Lancet Planetary Health*: approximately **489,000 people die from heat exposure globally every year**, averaged over the period 2000–2019. That figure places heat among the leading causes of weather-related death worldwide — and it was compiled before the catastrophic European heat event of summer 2022. Most of those deaths happen in cities, where the UHI raises the baseline temperature against which each heat event is experienced.

Today's reading does three things. First, it provides a mechanistic account of *how* heat kills — the physiological pathways by which elevated temperature translates into organ failure and death. Second, it quantifies the specific contribution of the UHI to that mortality burden, drawing on case studies from the 2022 London heatwave and long-run US surveillance data. Third, it identifies which populations bear a disproportionate share of the burden — and begins to ask why, a question Day 4 will examine in depth through the lens of environmental justice.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### How heat kills: the physiology

The human body maintains a core temperature of approximately 37°C within a narrow tolerance. When ambient temperature rises — especially when it remains elevated overnight, preventing the normal drop of 1–2°C that allows the cardiovascular system to recover — the thermoregulatory system faces a choice between two strategies, both with costs.

The primary cooling route is **sweating**: evaporative heat loss at the skin surface. A person can lose 1–1.5 litres of sweat per hour under severe heat stress, which removes roughly 600 W of heat — but only as long as fluid and electrolytes are replenished and ambient humidity is low enough for sweat to evaporate. In very humid conditions, or when ambient temperature exceeds skin temperature (~35°C), sweating loses its thermodynamic advantage. The second strategy is **vasodilation**: the heart pumps more blood to peripheral vessels to carry core heat to the skin. Cardiac output can double or triple under heat stress. This creates a direct cardiovascular load that healthy young hearts tolerate well but that can precipitate arrhythmia, myocardial infarction, or heart failure in those with underlying cardiac disease.

When these mechanisms are overwhelmed, core temperature rises. Above 40°C, proteins begin to denature; enzyme function fails; the blood-brain barrier breaks down; skeletal and cardiac muscle cells undergo rhabdomyolysis. **Heat stroke** — defined as core temperature above 40°C with central nervous system dysfunction — carries a case fatality rate of 10–50% even with medical treatment. But heat stroke is the tip of the iceberg: the far larger death toll comes from the acceleration of pre-existing disease. Cardiovascular deaths increase sharply during heat events even among people who never lose consciousness. Kidney disease accelerates through dehydration-induced acute kidney injury. Respiratory failure worsens as air quality degrades (heat promotes ozone formation). The elderly, people with heart or kidney disease, those taking diuretics or beta-blockers, and people who lack access to air conditioning are most exposed to these pathways.

### The global mortality background: Zhao et al. (2021)

Zhao and colleagues applied a distributed lag non-linear model — the standard statistical tool for capturing the time-delayed relationship between temperature and mortality — to 750 locations across 43 countries, calibrating results to national data and then extrapolating to global estimates. Their estimate of **489,000 heat-related deaths per year** (averaged 2000–2019) is widely cited by WHO and national health agencies because it is the most methodologically rigorous multi-country estimate available.

Several features of this estimate matter for understanding urban heat islands specifically. First, the geographic distribution is skewed: 45% of heat deaths are in Asia and 36% in Europe, reflecting high population density and, in Europe's case, populations less physiologically or behaviourally acclimatised to extreme heat than, say, sub-Saharan Africans. Second, the trend is alarming: heat-related mortality for people over 65 increased by approximately **85%** between 2000–2004 and 2017–2021, driven jointly by warming temperatures, ageing populations, and expanding cities. Third, the figure is almost certainly an undercount — heat death is chronically under-attributed on death certificates, where the immediate cause (heart attack, respiratory failure) is typically recorded rather than the precipitating thermal stressor.

The UHI's contribution to these global figures cannot be directly quantified from the Zhao study, which uses background meteorological data rather than fine-grained urban temperature maps. But a conservative reading of the evidence — from studies in London, the West Midlands, Vietnam, and China — suggests that the UHI accounts for 30–50% of heat-attributable deaths in dense urban areas. Scaling that fraction against Zhao's global estimate suggests urban overheating is responsible for tens of thousands of preventable deaths every year above what a non-urbanised version of the same climate would produce.

### The UHI's specific contribution: what the 2022 London heatwave revealed

On 19 July 2022, the UK recorded air temperatures exceeding 40°C for the first time in its meteorological history. During the 10–25 July period, Greater London recorded 1,773 deaths. Simpson, Brousse, and Heaviside applied a two-step attribution chain: first estimating how many of those deaths were attributable to heat using the standard epidemiological framework (excess mortality against a counterfactual), then using a high-resolution urban climate model to ask how many of the heat-attributable deaths were specifically attributable to the **UHI increment** rather than the heatwave itself.

Their estimate: **370 deaths** were heat-attributable, and **38% of those** — approximately 141 deaths — were attributable to the UHI. This fraction is substantial, especially given that during the 2022 event, the UHI did not push daytime peak temperatures to new records (those records were reached at relatively rural stations). The UHI's key pathological contribution during that event was **nighttime elevation**: inner London temperatures that should have dropped to 16–18°C remained above 22°C in many boroughs through the night. This connects directly to the Day 2 mechanism — the thermal mass of London's buildings absorbed the extreme daytime heat and released it slowly through the night, preventing any recovery of the cardiovascular and thermoregulatory systems of the most vulnerable residents. The result: the body's nightly repair cycle was interrupted for multiple successive nights, with cumulative mortality consequences.

An earlier study of the 2003 heatwave in the West Midlands (Heaviside, Vardoulakis & Cai, 2016, *Environmental Health*) produced a similar estimate: the UHI contributed approximately 50% of total heat-related mortality in that region. The London and West Midlands figures together suggest that, in temperate city climates where the UHI's most powerful effect is on nighttime temperature minimums, roughly one in three to one in two heat deaths in the urban core is a UHI death — preventable, in principle, by the design interventions to be discussed on Day 5.

### Cardiovascular morbidity in older adults: the Cleland et al. (2023) US study

The mortality literature captures the most extreme outcomes. Cleland and colleagues' analysis of Medicare hospitalisation data captures the far larger submerged iceberg of **non-fatal illness** — emergency admissions for heart failure, arrhythmia, ischaemic heart disease, and stroke that heat events trigger.

Across 120 US metropolitan areas and 18 years of data, the study found that days with extreme heat (defined as the 99th percentile of local temperature, approximately 28.6°C on a population-weighted average) were associated with a **3.0% increase in cardiovascular hospitalisation risk** among Medicare-enrolled adults aged 65 and older. But the UHI-stratified analysis is what makes the paper particularly important for urban health policy: in ZIP codes with **high UHI intensity**, the risk increment was **2.4 percentage points** (a risk ratio roughly 2.4× baseline), compared to **1.0 percentage point** in low-UHII areas. The UHI was not merely a background condition; it was actively amplifying heat's cardiovascular impact.

The burden distribution was even more striking. The highest quartile of UHI intensity accounted for approximately **47% of all heat-attributable cardiovascular hospitalisations** over the study period — roughly 14,600 excess admissions — despite covering only a quarter of the studied geography. This spatial concentration is the epidemiological counterpart to the energy balance physics from Day 2: the places where imperviousness is highest and evapotranspiration is lowest are exactly the places where cardiovascular morbidity risk is most elevated.

The most vulnerable subgroups identified by Cleland et al. were women (who have less cardiovascular reserve and are more likely to live alone), adults aged 75 and older, and people with prior chronic conditions (heart failure, diabetes, chronic kidney disease). For these groups, heat stress during extreme events in high-UHII areas was a medical emergency, not merely discomfort.

### Who bears the burden

The epidemiological evidence across these three studies converges on a consistent profile of risk:

- **Age** is the dominant biological vulnerability factor. Adults 65 and older have reduced thermoregulatory efficiency, diminished thirst sensation, higher rates of cardiovascular and renal disease, and greater likelihood of taking medications (diuretics, beta-blockers, antihypertensives) that impair heat dissipation. They account for approximately 40% of US heat-related deaths despite representing around 17% of the population.

- **Pre-existing cardiovascular and renal disease** dramatically raises risk. The physiological burden of heat dissipation — elevated cardiac output, dehydration-driven haemoconcentration, reduced renal blood flow — directly stresses these systems.

- **Residential location within the city** determines the dose. In high-UHII ZIP codes, the UHI penalty is real, measurable, and adds to every other risk factor. A 75-year-old woman with heart failure living in a dense, low-canopy, high-imperviousness urban neighbourhood faces a qualitatively different heat risk than one living in a green, suburban neighbourhood two kilometres away — even if the ambient meteorological temperature is the same.

- **Poverty and lack of air conditioning** compound exposure. Air conditioning is the most effective single intervention for preventing heat death, but it requires upfront purchase cost, electricity access, and a dwelling type that can support window units or central systems. Low-income households in high-UHII areas are doubly penalised: higher outdoor temperatures and less ability to cool indoors. This intersection of physical exposure and socioeconomic vulnerability is the topic of Day 4.

The epidemiology of urban heat is, ultimately, an epidemiology of compounding disadvantage. The UHI is not experienced equally across the city or across the population. Its burden lands heaviest on those least able to bear it — and the mechanisms that produce that pattern are social and political as well as physical.

## Questions to think about

1. Simpson et al. found that the UHI's main lethal contribution during London's 2022 heatwave was not higher daytime peaks but higher **nighttime minimum temperatures**. Given what you know from Day 2 about thermal mass and the urban canyon effect, why is the nocturnal UHI specifically more dangerous for human health than an equivalent elevation in daytime temperature? Think about both the physiology (what the body does at night) and the behaviour (where people are and what options they have).

2. Cleland et al. found that the highest quartile of UHI intensity was responsible for approximately 47% of all heat-attributable cardiovascular hospitalisations, even though it covers only a quarter of the studied geography. If you were a city health department director given a fixed budget, how would this finding shape your decision about whether to invest in city-wide interventions (e.g., public awareness campaigns, heat action plans) versus geographically targeted ones (e.g., greening or cool pavement in the highest-UHII ZIP codes)?

3. The Zhao et al. (2021) estimate of 489,000 annual heat deaths is described as likely an **undercount** because death certificates list immediate causes (heart attack, kidney failure) rather than the precipitating heat exposure. This measurement problem is not unique to heat — many environmental stressors are similarly under-attributed on death certificates. What would better heat-attribution surveillance look like, and which institutions would need to change their practices for it to happen?
