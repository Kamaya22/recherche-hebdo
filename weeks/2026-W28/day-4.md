# Urban Heat Islands and Climate Adaptation of Cities — Day 4/7: Environmental Justice — Who Bears the Burden

## Context

The first three days of this week established the physical and biological architecture of urban overheating. Days 1 and 2 explained *why* cities are hot: sealed surfaces eliminate the evaporative cooling that keeps vegetated landscapes comfortable, concrete and asphalt store solar energy and release it through the night, and the urban canyon geometry traps outgoing radiation. Day 3 connected that physics to public health, documenting the global toll of around 489,000 heat deaths per year and showing — through the lens of London's 2022 heatwave — that the urban heat island can be directly blamed for a substantial fraction of those deaths.

But those averages obscure a pattern that is at least as important as the aggregate numbers: heat risk is not distributed evenly across cities. Walk ten minutes from a wealthy, tree-lined neighbourhood to a lower-income district nearby and the temperature difference can exceed 2°C on a summer afternoon. The residents of that hotter district are not randomly selected. Across almost every American city that has been studied, the neighbourhoods that run hottest today are the same neighbourhoods that were systematically denied investment, green space, and mortgage financing for much of the twentieth century through a federal policy known as *redlining*. Race and income both predict heat exposure — and, critically, race predicts it independently of income.

Today we dig into the mechanisms that produce this geography of heat injustice: the 1930s policy roots of present-day disparities, the tree-canopy gap as the principal physical mediator, the air-conditioning affordability trap that strips away adaptive capacity, and an emerging complication where the very greening interventions designed to cool hot neighbourhoods risk displacing the communities they were meant to protect.

## Today's readings (2 items, ~18 min total)

**1. "Disproportionate exposure to urban heat island intensity across major US cities"** — Hsu, A., Sheriff, G., Chakraborty, T. & Manya, D., *Nature Communications*, 12, 2721 (2021) — <https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-021-22799-5> — ~9 min — The largest systematic analysis of racial and income disparities in urban heat island exposure across the continental United States. The authors combine satellite-derived surface urban heat island data with census-tract demographic data for 175 of the largest urbanized areas. Their finding is striking: in 169 of those 175 cities — all but six — the average person of colour lives in a census tract with higher surface heat island intensity than the average non-Hispanic white resident of the same city. A similar income gradient appears in nearly all cities, but a key additional finding is that, in almost half the urbanized areas studied, the average person of colour faces *higher* summer daytime heat intensity than the average person living below the poverty line, despite the fact that only around 10 % of people of colour live in poverty. Racial disparities in heat exposure cannot, in other words, be reduced to income disparities. *Nature Communications* is fully open access.

**2. "The Effects of Historical Housing Policies on Resident Exposure to Intra-Urban Heat: A Study of 108 US Urban Areas"** — Hoffman, J.S., Shandas, V. & Pendleton, N., *Climate*, 8(1), 12 (2020) — <https://www.mdpi.com/2225-1154/8/1/12> — ~9 min — A landmark open-access paper that maps 1930s Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) security ratings — the original federal redlining maps — onto present-day satellite land-surface temperatures for 108 US cities. Neighbourhoods graded "D" (coloured red on the original maps, denoting areas with large Black or immigrant populations and deemed "hazardous" for real-estate investment) are on average 2.6 °C warmer today than neighbourhoods graded "A." Ninety-four percent of the 108 cities exhibit this pattern consistently; in Portland, Oregon, the gap reaches 7.1 °C. The study demonstrates that impervious land cover and low tree canopy — the legacy of decades of disinvestment — are the principal physical pathways that convert an eighty-year-old discriminatory policy into a twenty-first-century thermal penalty. Published in *Climate* (MDPI), which is fully open access.

## Guided summary (~8 min)

### How redlining became a heat map

In the 1930s, as part of the New Deal, the Home Owners' Loan Corporation graded American urban neighbourhoods from "A" (green — best investment) to "D" (red — hazardous). The ratings were explicitly racially coded: the presence of Black residents, recent immigrants, or mixed-use development was sufficient to drop a neighbourhood to "C" or "D." Neighbourhoods graded "D" were systematically denied private mortgages, municipal infrastructure investment, and commercial development for decades, a practice that continued in various forms until the Fair Housing Act of 1968. The physical consequence was cumulative: no investment meant ageing housing stock, no street trees, more paved lots and fewer parks, more heavy industry sited nearby. When researchers now overlay those 1930s HOLC maps onto modern satellite temperature images, the thermal fingerprint of redlining is unmistakable. Hoffman et al. found that 94 % of the 108 cities they studied remain measurably hotter in their "D" zones than in their "A" zones, nearly a century later. The past is not past; it radiates.

### The tree-canopy gap as the key physical mediator

The single most important physical pathway connecting historical disinvestment to present-day heat is trees. Analyses across dozens of American cities consistently find a stark gradient: formerly "greenlined" (grade A) neighbourhoods have roughly 40–43 % tree canopy coverage on average, while formerly redlined (grade D) neighbourhoods have roughly 23 %. Canopy does multiple things at once: it shades pavement and rooftops, directly lowering surface temperatures; it transpires water, consuming latent heat and cooling the overlying air; and it reduces the amount of impervious surface that stores and re-radiates heat at night. The LA Science Advances study (2023, doi: 10.1126/sciadv.ade8501) quantified how vegetation drives the income-temperature link in fine-grained detail: across Los Angeles, a $10,000 drop in median household income corresponds to roughly 0.2 °C higher land-surface temperature at moderate ambient conditions, but the gap widens to 0.7 °C during extreme heat events, because the cooling effect of vegetation becomes most valuable exactly when temperatures are highest. Heat inequality compounds itself precisely when it matters most.

### Race, income, and the independent racial penalty

A finding that consistently surprises researchers is that racial disparities in heat exposure persist even when income is held constant. Benz and Burney (Earth's Future, 2021) analysed 1,056 US counties and found that, for 76 % of counties, the poorest census tracts are significantly hotter than the wealthiest — an income effect. But they also found that, for 71 % of all counties, census tracts with higher Black, Hispanic, or Asian population shares are hotter than predominantly white tracts *within the same income band*. Hsu et al.'s 2021 Nature Communications study reinforces this: in nearly half of all cities they studied, the average person of colour faces greater heat intensity than the average person living in poverty, meaning that race is not simply a proxy for poverty in this context. The mechanisms behind this independent racial effect are not fully resolved, but several candidates have been proposed: the location of industrial facilities and highways (historically sited near communities of colour, adding anthropogenic heat and replacing vegetation), the lower quality of municipal services including park maintenance and street-tree planting in majority-Black and Latino districts, and the persistence of racial residential segregation that has kept wealthy white suburbs spatially separated from urban heat cores.

### The adaptive capacity gap: air conditioning and energy burden

Being exposed to more heat is only half of the vulnerability equation. The other half is the capacity to adapt. Air conditioning is the most effective individual-level protection against extreme heat events — Day 3 showed it substantially reduces cardiovascular hospitalisation and heat-related mortality. But air-conditioning access is itself unequally distributed. Studies of residential AC coverage across major US cities find consistent race and income gradients: Black and Hispanic households are significantly more likely to lack central air conditioning and more likely to rely on less effective window units. Even when low-income households own AC, financial constraints often prevent them from using it: energy bills during a heat wave can represent a substantial share of monthly income for minimum-wage households, leading to what researchers term "AC underuse" — the occupant runs the unit only sporadically, accepting higher indoor temperatures to avoid unaffordable utility bills. This energy-burden problem is invisible in data sets that simply track AC ownership.

Cooling centres — publicly air-conditioned spaces such as libraries, community centres, or shopping malls — partially compensate for private AC gaps, but research consistently finds that low-income residents use them far less than expected, due to transport barriers, mistrust of institutions, irregular opening hours, and — particularly for undocumented populations — fear of surveillance. The result is a double vulnerability: hotter outdoor and indoor conditions, and fewer effective refuges.

### Green gentrification: the complication in the solution

There is an uncomfortable tension at the heart of urban heat equity policy. The most effective neighbourhood-scale cooling interventions — tree planting, park creation, green roofs, bioswales — raise property values. When a formerly disinvested neighbourhood gains tree canopy and parks, rents follow. Several studies have documented this dynamic directly: in Charlotte, North Carolina, retrofitting formerly redlined areas with green infrastructure reduced land-surface temperatures — and coincided with measurable declines in the African American and low-income populations of those same neighbourhoods, as rising rents displaced existing residents to less-improved and hotter areas. This phenomenon is sometimes called "green gentrification" or "climate gentrification." It creates a painful irony: the communities that most need cooling are cooled, but the cooling makes them unaffordable for the people who needed it. Policy responses are still emerging, but researchers advocate coupling greening programmes with tenant protections, community land trusts, and participatory governance structures that give existing residents a binding say over how improvements are designed and who benefits from them.

### A global pattern with local variation

The environmental justice dimensions of urban heat are not unique to the United States, though they are particularly legible there because of the detailed HOLC records. In cities across the Global South — Delhi, Lagos, Jakarta, São Paulo — the hottest neighbourhoods are informal settlements lacking tree cover, paved drainage, or reliable electricity for cooling. In European cities, migrant and low-income communities concentrated in peripheral social-housing blocks with poor insulation and limited green space face elevated heat risk, as documented during the 2003 and 2022 European heatwaves. The mechanisms vary — redlining is an American institution, but discriminatory spatial planning, exclusionary zoning, and racialised disinvestment are not. The heat map is, almost everywhere, an inequality map.

## Questions to think about

1. The Hsu et al. study found that racial disparities in heat exposure persist independently of income disparities. What mechanisms might explain this independent racial effect, and how would you design a study to test which mechanism is most important?

2. The green gentrification literature suggests that cooling a neighbourhood can make it unaffordable for its existing residents. What policy instruments could decouple neighbourhood cooling from displacement, and what would be the political obstacles to implementing them?

3. Day 3 showed that heat mortality is concentrated among elderly people living alone, low-income households, and outdoor workers. Day 4 shows that these groups are also concentrated in the hottest neighbourhoods. Does this spatial co-location of vulnerability and heat exposure demand a different policy approach than one that treats heat exposure and vulnerability as independent variables?
